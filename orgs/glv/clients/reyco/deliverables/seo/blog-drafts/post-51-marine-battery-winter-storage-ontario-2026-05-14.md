A marine battery that is stored incorrectly through a Northern Ontario winter will either fail to start the boat in the spring or will have lost enough capacity that it performs unreliably for the season. Most marine battery failures in Ontario are storage failures, not manufacturing defects. The good news is that proper winter storage is straightforward and the battery does not need to come out of the boat to be maintained correctly.

---

## Why Northern Ontario Winter Is Hard on Marine Batteries

Lead-acid and AGM marine batteries self-discharge when not in use. In warm conditions this process is slow. In cold conditions it is slower still, which seems beneficial, but the problem is what happens when a battery is stored deeply discharged in cold temperatures.

A fully charged lead-acid or AGM battery will withstand significant cold without damage. A deeply discharged or partially discharged battery can freeze. When battery electrolyte (the sulfuric acid solution inside the cells) is diluted by discharge, its freezing point rises. A discharged battery left in an unheated space through a Northern Ontario winter can freeze and crack internally, permanently damaging the battery.

The second problem is sulfation. When a lead-acid battery remains in a low-charge state for weeks or months, the lead sulphate that forms on the plates during discharge does not dissolve back into the electrolyte. It crystallises and builds up on the plates, reducing the battery's capacity permanently. A battery that sat discharged through winter will often test at full voltage but deliver significantly less than its rated cranking amperage.

## The Right Approach: Leave It or Remove It

**Leaving the battery in the boat** works if the boat is stored in a location where a battery maintainer can be plugged in throughout the winter. A quality trickle charger or battery maintainer connected to shore power keeps the battery at full charge, prevents sulfation, and eliminates the freeze risk. If the boat is stored at a facility with electrical access, this is the simplest approach.

**Removing the battery** is the correct approach when the boat is stored without electrical access (trailer in the driveway, in a shed, or at a storage facility without power). A removed battery can be stored in a heated space and maintained on a charger indoors.

Either approach works when executed correctly. The error is leaving the battery in an unheated space without a maintainer for seven months.

---

## Battery Maintainer vs Trickle Charger

These terms are often used interchangeably but refer to different charge profiles.

**Trickle charger:** Delivers a constant low-level charge. Adequate for short-term maintenance but will overcharge a battery if left connected for months without a charge-sensing circuit.

**Battery maintainer (float charger):** Monitors battery voltage and cycles between a charge mode and a float mode automatically. When the battery reaches full charge, the maintainer reduces to float mode. It does not overcharge. This is the correct device for long-term winter storage.

For marine batteries stored for seven months, use a battery maintainer, not a basic trickle charger.

## Marine Battery Types and Winter Considerations

**Flooded lead-acid batteries:** The traditional marine battery. Requires occasional water top-up (distilled water only). Can off-gas hydrogen during charging, which requires ventilation. Most susceptible to freeze damage if discharged. The least expensive type.

**AGM (Absorbed Glass Mat) batteries:** Sealed, maintenance-free, and more resistant to vibration and discharge damage than flooded batteries. Better suited to infrequent use patterns (seasonal use + long storage). Higher initial cost. AGM batteries are more tolerant of partial discharge than flooded batteries but still benefit from winter maintenance charging.

**Lithium marine batteries:** The most expensive option, increasingly available for marine use. Lithium batteries have a very low self-discharge rate and handle partial discharge without sulfation (lithium chemistry does not sulfate). They should not, however, be stored fully discharged or left in extremely cold conditions. Lithium battery storage protocols differ from lead-acid and AGM; follow the manufacturer guidelines specifically.

## Practical Steps for Winter Storage

**1. Check the charge state before storage.** Use a multimeter to check resting voltage. A fully charged 12V lead-acid or AGM battery reads approximately 12.6 to 12.8V at rest. Below 12.4V is partially discharged; below 12.0V is significantly discharged and requires charging before storage.

**2. Charge to full before storage.** If the battery is below full charge, charge it before the temperature drops. Do not store a discharged battery, even briefly.

**3. Clean the terminals.** Corrosion at battery terminals increases resistance and can prevent the battery from accepting charge correctly. Clean with a wire brush and apply a terminal protector spray.

**4. Connect a battery maintainer.** If the battery stays in the boat with electrical access, connect a maintainer and leave it connected through winter. If the battery is removed and stored indoors, connect it to a maintainer in the storage location.

**5. Check midwinter if accessible.** If the storage location is accessible, a midwinter voltage check confirms the maintainer is working. A reading below 12.4V while connected to a maintainer indicates a failing battery or maintainer issue.

## Signs the Battery Did Not Survive Winter

A battery that was not maintained through winter often shows one of these patterns in spring:

- Charges to full voltage but cranks the engine sluggishly or not at all (capacity loss from sulfation)
- Charges to full voltage but will not hold charge for more than a few hours when disconnected (internal short or plate damage)
- Battery case is bulging or cracked (freeze damage)

A battery load tester provides a definitive assessment of whether the battery can deliver its rated cranking amperage. This is a straightforward test at a marine service shop.
