Cub Cadet and Toro are the two brands Reyco Marine carries for snow equipment, and they come up in almost every snowblower purchase conversation. Both are well-made machines from established manufacturers. The question is not which brand is objectively better but which one fits the specific Northern Ontario buyer's situation better.

---

## Who Builds These Machines

**Cub Cadet** is part of MTD Products, which is a division of Stanley Black and Decker. Cub Cadet has built snow equipment since the 1960s and has developed a product line that runs from residential two-stage machines through near-commercial units. Their engineering emphasis is on high-capacity performance and versatility, particularly their multi-stage designs.

**Toro** is an independent manufacturer based in Minnesota with roots in professional landscape and turf equipment. Their snow equipment division produces residential and professional-grade machines. Toro's emphasis in snow equipment is on ease of use and reliability, and their residential lineup reflects that orientation.

Both brands are manufactured to comparable quality standards at their respective price points. Choosing between them is primarily about which feature set and which machine design better matches your property and how you clear snow.

---

## The Auger and Impeller Design Difference

This is the most meaningful mechanical difference between comparable models from each brand at the residential level.

**Cub Cadet 2X series:** Two heavy-duty serrated augers feed into a two-stage impeller. The serrated auger design handles ice and compacted snow more aggressively than a smooth steel auger. The 2X series is well-suited to the end-of-driveway plow windrow that is a consistent feature of Northern Ontario winter, because the serrated augers can bite into packed material that would strain a lighter machine.

**Cub Cadet 3X series:** Three-stage design with two augers plus an accelerator between the augers and the impeller. The accelerator breaks up incoming snow before the impeller throws it, reducing resistance and allowing faster clearing in heavy wet snow. This is Cub Cadet's high-capacity residential design and it performs consistently in the heavy, wet snowfalls that SSM produces in November and March.

**Toro Power Max HD:** Two-stage with Toro's Power Max auger system, which uses a combination auger design intended to pull snow in efficiently without requiring the operator to push the machine forward. Toro's self-propel system on the Power Max is well-regarded for ease of use on inclines and in deep snow where operator effort would otherwise be high.

---

## Self-Propel Systems

Both brands include self-propelled drive systems on their two-stage machines, but the mechanisms differ.

Cub Cadet uses a friction-disc drive system on their residential lineup. The speed is controlled by a dial, and the machine can be set to a pace that matches the snow depth. The system is robust and straightforward to service.

Toro's Power Max uses a differential drive system on their mid-range and higher models. A differential drive turns the drive wheels at slightly different speeds when turning, which reduces the effort required to change direction. On a long driveway where you are making frequent turning passes, this makes the machine easier to manoeuvre.

For a straight driveway with simple pass patterns, either system works equally well. For irregular driveways with tight turns or obstacles, Toro's differential drive has a slight ergonomic advantage.

---

## Throw Distance and Chute Control

In Northern Ontario conditions where you need to throw snow well clear of the driveway edge, throw distance matters. Both brands' two-stage and three-stage machines have adequate throw distances for residential use (20 to 45 feet depending on snow conditions and model).

Chute control is where the machines differ in usability. Toro offers a quick-stick chute control on several models that allows one-hand adjustment of chute direction without leaving the handles. Cub Cadet uses a standard crank-style chute deflector on most residential models. The quick-stick system is genuinely more convenient when you are adjusting frequently during a clearing run.

## Which Buyers Tend to Choose Each

In practice, buyers who prioritise maximum capacity and heavy-condition performance in SSM's worst storms tend toward Cub Cadet, particularly the 3X series. The three-stage design handles the full range of Northern Ontario snow conditions with less engine strain and more consistent throw, and the serrated auger design manages the hard-packed end-of-driveway material reliably.

Buyers who prioritise ease of use, manoeuvrability on a complex or smaller driveway, and a self-propel system that minimises operator effort tend toward the Toro Power Max lineup. The differential drive and quick-stick chute control make routine clearing less physically demanding.

Both brands are serviced at Reyco Marine under warranty. For most Northern Ontario buyers, the decision comes down to driveway size and shape, snow type, and whether maximum capacity or maximum convenience is the priority.
