An outboard that ran fine at the end of last season is not guaranteed to run fine at the start of this one. Northern Ontario winters are hard on marine equipment. Six months of cold storage, condensation, and fuel sitting in the system changes what you will find when you try to start the engine in spring.

Some issues are visible before you launch. Others only show up on the water, at the worst possible moment. Here is what to look for before you back the trailer down the ramp.

---

## Starting Problems: The Most Obvious Warning

An engine that cranks but does not start, starts and immediately stalls, or requires extended cranking before it catches is telling you something specific.

Fuel system issues are the most common cause. Gasoline left in a carburettor or fuel system over winter breaks down and leaves varnish deposits in the passages and jets. This is especially common when fuel was not stabilised before storage. The engine may eventually start but will run rough or stall under load.

On fuel-injected Mercury outboards, the same degraded fuel causes injector fouling. The injector deposits restrict flow without completely blocking it, which shows up as rough idle, hesitation, and inconsistent throttle response rather than a hard no-start.

If the engine is hard to start in spring and ran fine the previous fall, the fuel system is the first place to look.

---

## Rough Idle and Smoke

An engine that idles unevenly, runs rough at low RPM, or produces unusual smoke after starting has at least one issue that should be addressed before the season begins.

White smoke from the exhaust after initial start is often condensation burning off and may be normal for the first few minutes of operation. White smoke that continues after the engine has warmed up indicates water in the combustion chamber, which points to a head gasket, a cooling system issue, or water intrusion from storage.

Blue smoke indicates oil burning. On a four-stroke outboard, blue smoke on start-up that clears after warmup can be valve seal wear. Blue smoke that persists at operating temperature is more serious.

Rough idle that does not clear after warmup, combined with a misfire or stumble at cruise RPM, often indicates spark plugs that have fouled over winter or reached the end of their service interval.

---

## Overheating Warning

Mercury outboards have a temperature warning horn and a dash indicator that triggers before the engine reaches critical temperature. If you have run the engine recently and the warning horn fired, the cooling system needs attention before the engine is operated again.

The most common cause of outboard overheating is water pump impeller failure. The impeller is a rubber component that degrades over time and does not necessarily fail completely before causing cooling issues. A partially deteriorated impeller moves less water, which can cause the engine to run at elevated temperature without triggering the full warning horn immediately.

An outboard that runs slightly warm without triggering the warning, or one that has not had an impeller replacement in two or more seasons, should have the water pump inspected before the season begins. An impeller that fails on the water causes an overheat in minutes.

---

## Gear Shift Problems

Difficulty engaging forward or reverse, grinding when shifting, or slipping between gears indicates a problem in the lower unit or the shift linkage.

Shift linkage issues are often cables that have stiffened or stretched. This is common after a winter of non-use and may be correctable with a cable adjustment or replacement.

Lower unit gear problems are more serious. If the shift lever is operating correctly but the gears are not engaging cleanly, the lower unit should be serviced before the engine takes any load. Running an outboard with a lower unit problem quickly leads to more expensive damage.

---

## Fuel Smell at Idle or After Shutdown

A fuel smell that was not present before storage warrants an inspection of the fuel system before operation.

Fuel lines develop cracks and brittleness over time, and a winter cycle of temperature variation accelerates this. A cracked fuel line that is not leaking at rest may weep at operating pressure. The primer bulb, which degrades similarly, is also a common source of fuel smell on older setups.

A fuel smell on a boat is a fire risk, not a nuisance. This is one item that should not be operated through until the source is identified and corrected.
