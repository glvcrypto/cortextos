The outboard motor is not an afterthought. For most Northern Ontario boaters, the engine choice determines how far you can comfortably travel, how long the boat lasts, and how often it gets you stranded. This is a region where the distances between ramps are long, cell service is unreliable in many areas, and on-the-water help is not a phone call away.

Getting the engine right from the start matters more here than in areas with closer access to service.

---

## Start With the Boat, Not the Engine

The boat determines the engine range. Every aluminium and fibreglass hull has a maximum horsepower rating stamped on a plate near the transom. You cannot exceed that rating safely, and going well below it means the boat performs below its designed envelope.

That plate narrows the choice considerably. If your boat is rated to 90 HP, you are selecting within that ceiling. Most buyers end up buying the highest HP the boat is rated for, because fuel efficiency with an undersized engine at full throttle is worse than fuel efficiency with a correctly matched engine at cruise.

If you are buying a boat and engine together, the dealer will match them. If you are replacing an engine on an existing hull, the rating plate is where you start.

---

## 4-Stroke vs 2-Stroke

This debate is largely settled in the current market. Four-stroke engines have been the standard for recreational outboards for over a decade.

Four-stroke outboards run quieter, use less fuel at equivalent power levels, and require less maintenance intervention than two-stroke designs. The environmental regulations that phased out carburetted two-strokes also pushed the technology further forward. A four-stroke Mercury at comparable HP will cost more upfront but lower your operating cost significantly over a typical season.

Two-stroke outboards still exist in high-performance and specific commercial contexts, but for Northern Ontario recreational use on a fishing or family boat, four-stroke is the correct baseline assumption.

---

## How Much Horsepower for Northern Ontario

The answer depends on where you are going.

For Shield lakes averaging 5 to 10 kilometres across, with moderate chop and typical summer afternoon wind, a correctly sized engine for your hull is sufficient. Most aluminium fishing boats in the 16 to 18 foot range are rated for 90 to 115 HP. That gets you to 40 to 45 mph on flat water, which is more than enough for typical use.

For Lake Superior or the larger Northern Ontario lakes where you are covering open water, you want to be at or near the top of the hull's HP rating. Superior builds fast, significant swells when the wind comes in. Being underpowered on open Superior is a safety concern, not just a comfort issue. The ability to punch through chop and run fast when conditions deteriorate changes the calculus.

For river use (St. Marys River, smaller Northern Ontario river systems), HP matters less than propeller selection and your ability to control the boat in current. A correctly propped 60 HP on a 16-foot aluminium boat handles most river situations well.

## Propeller Selection

The propeller is often more important than people expect, and it is rarely covered when someone buys a new boat and engine.

The same outboard will perform differently depending on propeller pitch and diameter. The standard prop that ships with most engines is a general-purpose choice. A 3-blade aluminium prop at a pitch matched to your typical load and speed is fine for most uses.

If you fish with heavy loads (multiple anglers, full livewell, gear), you may benefit from a propeller spec that keeps the engine in its optimal RPM range under that weight. If your primary use is running light and fast, a higher-pitch prop changes the performance profile.

Mercury dealers can help with propeller recommendations based on your engine, hull, and use case. It is a short conversation that can make a noticeable difference.
