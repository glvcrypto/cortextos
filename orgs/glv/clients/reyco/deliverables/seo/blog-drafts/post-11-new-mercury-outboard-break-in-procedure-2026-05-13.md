A new Mercury outboard is not ready for full operation the moment it leaves the dealership. Mercury specifies a break-in procedure for new four-stroke engines, and following it correctly matters for long-term engine health and, on most current models, for warranty coverage.

The procedure is not complicated, but it is different from simply running the engine at low RPM for a few hours, which is a common misconception about what break-in actually requires.

---

## Why Break-In Exists

New engine components are machined to tolerances, but they are not worn-in. During the first hours of operation, metal-to-metal contact between moving parts removes microscopic high points on surfaces, and those fine metal particles circulate in the oil. The engine management system on current Mercury four-stroke outboards operates in a slightly restricted mode during break-in to manage heat and load while this process completes.

By 20 hours, the internal surfaces have seated, the oil has accumulated the break-in debris, and the engine is ready to operate at full load. The 20-hour service changes the oil and filter at this point, clearing the system and confirming the engine is ready.

Running a new Mercury at full throttle before break-in is complete puts the engine under a load it is not yet ready to handle. This can cause accelerated wear on cylinder walls, piston rings, and valve trains that is not visible and does not produce immediate symptoms, but shortens engine life.

---

## The Mercury Break-In Procedure

Mercury's recommended break-in procedure for current four-stroke outboards follows this sequence:

**First 10 hours: vary the throttle constantly.**

This is the part most new owners get wrong. Break-in is not about running at low RPM. Mercury specifies that during the first 10 hours, the throttle should be varied continuously. Run at different speeds, accelerate and decelerate, and do not hold a constant RPM for extended periods.

Holding a constant RPM during break-in causes the piston rings to seat unevenly. Running at low steady RPM for 10 hours does not break in the engine correctly. The varied throttle requirement is what ensures the rings seat evenly across the full range of piston travel.

Avoid running at wide-open throttle during the first 10 hours. Vary the throttle from low to approximately 75 percent of full RPM. Do not hold wide-open throttle for more than a few seconds at a time during this phase.

**Hours 10 to 20: increase load gradually.**

In the second 10-hour phase, Mercury allows for increasing load but still recommends avoiding sustained wide-open throttle operation. Brief runs at full throttle become acceptable, but the majority of this phase should still be at varied, moderate loads.

**Hour 20: break-in service.**

At approximately 20 hours (Mercury specifies a range; check your specific engine's manual for the precise interval), the engine requires its first service. This is not optional or suggested. On current Mercury models, the engine management system logs hours, and the 20-hour service point is recorded.

The break-in service includes an engine oil and filter change, lower unit gear oil change, and a general inspection. This should be done at an authorised Mercury service centre.

## Fuel During Break-In

Mercury four-stroke outboards run on regular unleaded fuel (minimum 87 octane in Canada). Some owners wonder whether premium fuel is beneficial during break-in. It is not, on current Mercury four-strokes. These engines are not high-compression designs that require premium fuel, and using premium during break-in does not improve the break-in process.

Do not use fuel with more than 10 percent ethanol (E10). Mercury's warranty specifically excludes damage caused by higher ethanol blends. Most fuel at Canadian marine marinas and many fuel stations is E0 or E10, but it is worth confirming what you are pumping.

---

## Oil During Break-In

Mercury factory-fills current four-stroke outboards with Mercury-branded oil. Do not drain or change the oil before the 20-hour mark unless the oil becomes contaminated. The break-in service at 20 hours is when the factory fill comes out.

If the engine came with a break-in oil recommendation in the documentation, follow it. Mercury's current four-stroke lineup uses their full-synthetic outboard oil for the fill in most cases. Mixing oil brands during the break-in period is not recommended.

---

## What Happens If You Skip Break-In

Skipping or shortening the break-in procedure does not produce immediate symptoms. The engine will start, run, and perform normally in the short term. The consequences show up later.

Cylinder walls that were not properly broken in wear faster under load. Piston rings that did not seat evenly have higher blow-by, which affects compression over time and can cause oil consumption issues that are not present in a correctly broken-in engine. These are not warranty-claimable failures on their own, because the damage is accumulated gradually and is not directly attributable to a single event.

The 20-hour break-in service is also when the dealer creates the first service record for the engine. A gap in the service record at the 20-hour mark is notable to any future buyer and to any warranty assessor reviewing the engine history.

Running the engine correctly through break-in costs nothing extra and takes the same 20 hours either way. It is the difference between an engine that reaches 1,000 hours in original condition and one that starts degrading earlier than it should.

---

## After Break-In

Once the 20-hour service is complete and the technician confirms the engine is operating correctly, the engine can be run at full load. Wide-open throttle operation, extended high-RPM runs, and full working loads are all appropriate after break-in is confirmed complete.

The next scheduled service for most Mercury four-stroke recreational outboards is at 100 hours or one year, whichever comes first.
