Most snowblower pre-season guides cover the same universal checklist: fresh fuel, oil check, spark plug, belts, auger shear bolts. That checklist is correct but generic. Cub Cadet and Toro machines have specific things to check before the Ontario season that their generic counterparts do not cover, and a new machine has a different first-season priority set than a three-year-old unit.

This guide covers what to check by brand and situation, not the standard checklist that reads the same regardless of what you own.

---

## New Machine: First-Season Priorities

If this is the machine's first winter season, the checklist is different from an established unit.

**Complete the break-in.** Two-stage snowblowers with gasoline engines have a break-in period, typically specified in the owner's manual. If the machine was purchased in the fall and run only minimally before storage, the break-in may not be complete. An incomplete break-in means the engine has not fully seated its internal components. Completing the break-in in early-season light snow conditions before heavy use is better than skipping straight to full load.

**First service interval.** Most manufacturers specify a first-service interval at approximately 5 hours or end of first season, whichever comes first. This covers an oil change to remove break-in oil, spark plug inspection, and a check of all fasteners after initial vibration load. If the machine was purchased in the fall and stored without reaching 5 hours, this service is due before the second season starts. Check the owner's manual.

**Storage condition check.** A machine that was stored from purchase without being run significantly may have fuel that has degraded in the tank. Ethanol-blended fuel degrades in as little as 30 days without stabiliser. Drain and replace with fresh fuel if the machine sat more than 60 days with unstabilised fuel.

## Cub Cadet Two-Stage: What to Check

Cub Cadet's two-stage snowblowers are common in Northern Ontario for heavier snowfall applications. Their specific pre-season priorities:

**Drive system friction disc.** Cub Cadet's drive system uses a friction disc that engages against a drive wheel. The friction disc wears over time, and when it wears past a certain point, the drive engagement becomes soft, the machine moves slowly or loses traction in deep snow. Before the season, engage the drive with the machine stationary and check that the disc is making solid contact and is not glazed or cracked. A worn friction disc is an inexpensive part to replace in the fall rather than discovering the problem mid-storm.

**Auger gearbox grease.** Cub Cadet's auger gearboxes are lubricated with grease that should be checked annually. The fill plug location varies by model, consult the owner's manual. If the gearbox has not been greased in the last two seasons, it is overdue. A dry gearbox is audible (grinding, rougher auger noise) but the damage is already underway by the time the noise appears.

**Fuel system.** Cub Cadet's carburettors on older units are sensitive to ethanol-damaged fuel. If the machine ran at the end of last season with fuel that was not stabilised, and sat with that fuel over the summer, the carburettor jets may be partially varnished. Signs: hard starting, rough idle, poor throttle response. A carburettor cleaning is faster to address in the fall than on the morning of the first significant snowfall.

**Skid shoes.** Check the skid shoes (the steel shoes that control how close the auger housing gets to the ground) for wear. Worn skid shoes allow the auger housing to contact the ground directly, damaging the auger housing edge and creating scalping marks on asphalt. Replacement skid shoes are available and cheap.

## Toro Two-Stage: What to Check

Toro's two-stage lineup (Power Max and SnowMaster series) has some specific points to review.

**Power curve drive system.** Toro's Power Curve auger system is a one-stage design on their smaller units, but the two-stage Power Max models use a standard auger-impeller configuration. On the Power Max, the auger and impeller are driven separately. Engage both and listen for any grinding or roughness that was not present at end of last season.

**Remote chute control cables.** Toro's power chute rotation is common on mid-range and upper models. The cable controls for chute direction can develop slop or stiffness after a full season, particularly if the machine was stored without the cables being exercised. Before the season, run the chute through its full rotation range and check that both cables move freely and return to neutral cleanly.

**Toro two-stage fuel systems.** Toro uses Briggs and Stratton or Kohler engines on their two-stage units depending on year and model. Both engine families are susceptible to carburettor varnishing from stale ethanol-blended fuel, similar to Cub Cadet. If the machine sat with unstabilised fuel over summer, a fuel system inspection is worth doing before first use.

**Impeller condition.** Toro impellers in the two-stage units take the snow delivered by the auger and throw it out the chute. A cracked or chipped impeller paddle reduces throw distance significantly and creates an imbalance that is felt as vibration. Inspect the impeller paddles with the machine off and the engine cold. Replace if cracked.

## The Ethanol Problem in Ontario

Northern Ontario's winter conditions create a specific storage risk that southern Ontario homeowners do not face to the same degree: longer storage periods.

Most Ontario fuel contains up to 10 percent ethanol. Ethanol absorbs moisture from the air and, over a summer storage period, can phase-separate in a sealed tank, the ethanol and water settling to the bottom away from the gasoline. This ethanol-water layer does not combust properly and is corrosive to aluminium carburettor components.

The practical prevention: add fuel stabiliser to the tank at the end of the snowblower season (April/May) before storage. This extends the fuel's usable life through the summer. For machines stored in unheated outbuildings, common in Northern Ontario where garages are often used for boats in summer, the temperature cycling accelerates fuel degradation.

Ethanol-free fuel, where available, eliminates the phase-separation risk entirely. If your area has a fuel station that carries ethanol-free regular unleaded, using it for the last fill of the season before storage is the most reliable long-term protection.

## When to Book Service Instead of DIY

Some pre-season checks are reasonable DIY tasks. Others are better handled at a dealer.

**Do yourself:** Fuel drain and refill, spark plug replacement, oil check (or change if due), belt visual inspection, skid shoe inspection, chute operation check.

**Book a dealer:** Auger gearbox inspection and re-greasing (correct grease specification matters), carburettor cleaning on a varnished unit (requires disassembly), friction disc replacement (alignment matters), any belt replacement on units with difficult access, auger or impeller damage.
