The boat is in your driveway. You have the keys. Before you back it down a ramp for the first time, there is a list of things that need to happen, and most of them are not on the sticker in the glove box.

This is what a new boat owner in Ontario needs to address in the first few weeks of ownership, before a mechanical problem or a compliance issue becomes the story of your first season.

---

## Registration and Licensing

In Ontario, a pleasure craft powered by more than 7.5 kilowatts (approximately 10 HP) must be registered with Transport Canada or documented under the Canada Shipping Act. The registration document must be on board whenever the vessel is operated.

Additionally, anyone operating a motorised pleasure craft in Canadian waters is required to have a Pleasure Craft Operator Card (PCOC) from a Transport Canada accredited provider. This applies to all ages, with the minimum age of unsupervised operation being 12 to 16 depending on engine size.

If you purchased the boat new from a dealer, the dealer typically handles the registration paperwork. Confirm this before you take possession, and keep the registration document in the boat, not at home.

## Safety Equipment: What the Law Requires

Transport Canada sets minimum safety equipment requirements for recreational vessels. These are not suggestions, and failing an inspection can result in fines and a mandatory return to shore.

For a typical small recreational motorboat (up to 6 metres) in Ontario:

- One approved personal flotation device (PFD) or lifejacket for every person on board
- One bailer or manual pump
- One rethrow line at least 15 metres long
- A sound-signalling device (whistle or horn)
- A watertight flashlight or equivalent

For boats between 6 and 9 metres, the requirements add flares and a fire extinguisher.

Check the length category your boat falls into, because the requirements step up significantly at 6 and 9 metres. A new boat purchased at a dealership typically comes equipped to the minimum standard, but verify this before your first trip rather than assuming.

## The 20-Hour Break-In Service (New Engines)

If your new boat came with a new Mercury outboard, the engine has a required 20-hour break-in service.

During the first 20 hours, the engine runs in a slightly restricted mode and the internal components are seating against each other. Small amounts of metal circulate in the oil during this period. The 20-hour service drains and replaces the break-in oil, changes the filter, and clears the system for normal operation.

This service is a warranty requirement on most Mercury models. Take the boat to an authorised Mercury service centre at approximately 20 engine hours. Running the engine beyond 20 hours without this service is noted in the engine management system on current Mercury models.

## Get Familiar With the Boat Before You Take on Ambitious Water

Your first few trips should be short and on familiar, low-risk water. This is not caution for its own sake. It is the practical reality that every boat handles differently, and getting a feel for the throttle response, trim, and turning characteristics in a controlled environment means you are better prepared when conditions change.

Northern Ontario lakes can build significant chop quickly, especially on afternoons with southwest wind. A captain who knows their boat's behaviour in a chop handles that situation better than one who is still figuring out the basics.

For the first few hours on the water, prioritise calm conditions and modest distances. Learn where the trim needs to be at different speeds. Understand how the boat behaves at low speed in tight quarters. Know how it handles when you back off the throttle hard.

## Know Your Fuel Consumption Before a Long Trip

New boat owners consistently underestimate fuel consumption on the first few trips. The fuel gauge in most aluminium fishing boats is a float-based instrument and its accuracy at low levels is unreliable.

Before a long run, calculate your estimated fuel burn based on the engine's rated consumption at cruise RPM and carry a reserve. Running out of fuel on a Northern Ontario lake is a long paddle to shore. Most experienced Northern Ontario boaters carry a spare jerry can for runs beyond 30 kilometres from the ramp.

## Set Up Your Service Schedule

Your first annual service is due at 20 hours or at the end of the first season, whichever comes first (the 20-hour break-in service is separate). Starting a maintenance log now, while the engine is new and the hours are trackable, is easier than reconstructing the service history in year four.

Note the date you purchased the boat, the engine model and serial number, and the hours at each service. Your authorised service centre maintains their own records, but having a personal copy of the service history is useful when you eventually sell the boat.

## Your Second Season: Spring Commissioning

After your first winter, the boat will need a spring commissioning service before it launches. This is when the water pump, gear oil, fuel system, and electrical connections are checked after sitting through a Northern Ontario winter.

A commissioning at an authorised service centre at the start of year two is the single most important service event outside of the initial break-in. It catches corrosion and storage-related wear before they become in-season problems.
