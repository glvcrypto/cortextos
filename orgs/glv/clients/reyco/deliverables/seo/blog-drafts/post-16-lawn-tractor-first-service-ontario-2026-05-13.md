A new lawn tractor from an authorised dealer is ready to operate when you take delivery. What most new owners do not have is a clear picture of when the first service is due, what that service covers, and what basic maintenance they should be doing between service intervals to keep the machine running correctly.

This is the first-year maintenance picture for a new Cub Cadet or Toro lawn tractor.

---

## The First Service Interval

Cub Cadet and Toro riding tractors have an initial service interval that occurs earlier than the standard annual service. This first service is typically at 8 hours of operation.

The purpose of the early first service is the same as for any new engine: new components are seating during early operation, and the first oil change at 8 hours removes the break-in debris (fine metal particles from the initial wear-in of engine components) before it circulates further in the oil.

The 8-hour service on a new lawn tractor covers engine oil and filter change, deck blade inspection and torque check, drive belt inspection, transmission fluid check (where applicable), and an overall inspection of bolts, fittings, and linkages that may have loosened during initial use.

If you purchased your tractor from an authorised Cub Cadet or Toro dealer, confirm whether the first service is included in the purchase or quoted separately. Many dealers include the first service as part of the purchase. Having it performed at the selling dealer creates the first entry in the service record, which supports warranty coverage and resale value.

---

## Annual Maintenance: What Happens Each Season

After the first-service break-in, the ongoing maintenance schedule for a four-stroke lawn tractor engine is primarily annual, with some items checking more frequently.

**Engine oil and filter.** Change annually or every 50 hours of operation, whichever comes first. A riding tractor that mows 2 acres twice a week through a 20-week Ontario season accumulates approximately 40 to 50 hours per season. Most residential users are changing oil annually; heavier users may need a mid-season change.

**Air filter.** Paper elements should be replaced annually. In dusty conditions (gravel areas, dry grass) inspect more frequently and replace when visibly clogged. A clogged air filter causes rich running and poor throttle response.

**Spark plug.** Inspect annually, replace when fouled or when the gap has widened beyond spec. Worn plugs on a small four-stroke engine cause hard cold starting, rough idle, and reduced power under load.

**Fuel filter.** Replace annually on engines with in-line fuel filters. A restricted fuel filter shows up as hesitation at higher throttle settings and rough running under load.

**Deck blades.** Inspect at the start of each season for impact damage and sharpness. Blades should be balanced when sharpened; an unbalanced blade causes deck vibration and accelerates spindle bearing wear. Replacing both blades at the same time keeps the deck balanced.

**Drive belt.** Inspect annually for cracking, glazing, and correct tension. Drive belts are wear items; most residential tractors replace them every two to four seasons depending on use.

**Tyres.** Check tyre pressure at the start of each season. Correct pressure is on the sidewall. Uneven tyre pressure causes uneven deck height and puts uneven load on the axle.

---

## What to Watch for During the Season

Between service intervals, pay attention to:

**Unusual vibration.** Deck vibration that was not present before can indicate a blade hitting something and bending slightly, or a spindle bearing developing play. Both conditions get worse with continued operation and are less expensive to address early.

**Oil level.** Check the engine oil level at the start of each mowing session, particularly on a new engine during the break-in period. A small four-stroke that is using oil or leaking will show up in the level; running a small engine low on oil causes rapid internal wear.

**Cutting quality.** If the cut quality deteriorates without a change in settings, the blades are the most likely cause. Dull blades tear grass rather than cutting it cleanly, which produces a ragged appearance and stresses the grass.

**Starting difficulty.** A tractor that requires more cranking to start than it used to is showing a developing issue, usually spark plug or carburettor related. Addressing it early avoids a no-start situation mid-season.

---

## End of Season Storage

Northern Ontario winters are hard on small engines that are not prepared for storage.

Run the fuel tank low and add fuel stabiliser to what remains, or run the carburettor dry by closing the fuel shutoff and running until the engine stalls. This prevents carburettor varnishing over the storage period.

Change the engine oil before storage rather than after winter, so the engine is not sitting on acidic used oil for six months.

Remove the battery and store it indoors on a trickle charger if possible. A battery that discharges fully over a cold Northern Ontario winter typically does not recover completely.
