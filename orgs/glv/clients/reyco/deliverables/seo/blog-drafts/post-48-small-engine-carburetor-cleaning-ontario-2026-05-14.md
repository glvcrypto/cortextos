A dirty or varnished carburettor is the most common reason a small engine runs rough, starts hard, or refuses to start at all after storage. It is also one of the more approachable DIY repairs on small equipment, provided you understand what you are actually cleaning and why. This guide covers how carburettors fail, when cleaning is the right fix, how to do it, and when to skip the DIY attempt and call a shop.

---

## Why Carburettors Get Dirty

The carburettor mixes air and fuel in the correct ratio for combustion. It does this through a series of small passages and jets with very tight tolerances. These passages are measured in thousandths of an inch. A partial blockage that is invisible to the eye is enough to affect engine performance.

Two things cause most carburettor fouling in Ontario:

**Ethanol-blended fuel degradation.** Most Ontario fuel contains up to 10 percent ethanol. Ethanol absorbs moisture and, when fuel sits for more than 30 days without a stabiliser, the fuel breaks down. The volatile components evaporate and leave a varnish residue on every surface the fuel contacted. In carburettor passages, this varnish builds up to the point where fuel cannot flow correctly.

**Gum and sediment from old fuel.** Even without ethanol, old gasoline gels and leaves deposits. Equipment stored from one season to the next with unstabilised fuel will often have partially blocked carburettor passages when it is started the following spring.

Both problems are preventable with fuel stabiliser added before storage. Both are fixable with a proper carburettor cleaning.

## Symptoms That Point to a Dirty Carburettor

Not every starting or running problem is a carburettor problem. The symptoms that most reliably indicate carburettor fouling:

**Hard starting after storage.** The engine cranks but will not fire, or fires briefly and dies. This often means the main jet is partially blocked, restricting fuel flow below what the engine needs to run.

**Rough idle, stalling at low throttle.** The idle circuit is a separate passage from the main jet. It is often narrower and blocks earlier. An engine that runs acceptably at higher throttle but dies at idle, or surges rhythmically, often has a blocked idle circuit.

**Black smoke or rich running.** Blocked air passages (less common than fuel passage blockage, but possible) result in a rich mixture. Black smoke from the exhaust and an engine that runs with the choke partially engaged after warmup are indicators.

**Engine runs on starting fluid but not on fuel.** Spray a small amount of starting fluid into the air intake. If the engine fires and runs briefly, the engine itself is mechanically sound and the fuel system is the problem. The carburettor is the first place to look.

## What You Need for a DIY Carburettor Cleaning

**Carburettor cleaner spray.** Available at any automotive or hardware store. This is a solvent specifically formulated to dissolve fuel varnish and gum. Generic engine cleaner is not an adequate substitute.

**Compressed air.** Required to blow passages clear after dissolving the blockage. Without compressed air, carburettor cleaning is only partially effective. A small compressor or a can of compressed air works.

**Basic hand tools.** Typically a screwdriver (flathead and Phillips) and an adjustable wrench or small socket set. The specific fasteners depend on the equipment.

**A clean work surface and a parts tray.** Carburettor disassembly involves small components that are easy to lose. A magnetic parts tray is useful.

**The owner's manual or a parts diagram.** Before disassembly, locate the carburettor diagram for your specific equipment model. Knowing which passages and jets are present in your carburettor prevents reassembly errors.

---

## The Cleaning Process

**Step 1: Fuel shutoff and disconnection.** Turn off the fuel shutoff valve (if equipped) before disconnecting the fuel line. On equipment without a shutoff valve, clamp the fuel line before removal to prevent spillage. Drain remaining fuel from the carburettor bowl.

**Step 2: Remove the carburettor.** Disconnect the throttle linkage, choke linkage, and fuel line. Note the position and routing of all linkages before disconnecting, a photo with your phone before disassembly is useful. Remove the carburettor mounting screws and pull the unit free.

**Step 3: Disassemble the carburettor.** Remove the float bowl (the bottom chamber that holds fuel). Inside, you will find the float, needle valve, main jet, and idle jet. Remove these components carefully and set them in your parts tray. Some carburettors have an emulsion tube or pilot jet as well, check your diagram.

**Step 4: Inspect the components.** Hold the jets up to light and look through the orifice. A clear passage is correct. A blocked or partially blocked jet needs cleaning. Check the needle valve for wear (a worn groove on the needle tip indicates it is not sealing properly).

**Step 5: Clean with carburettor cleaner.** Spray carburettor cleaner through all passages in the carburettor body and through each jet. Allow the solvent to soak for a few minutes on heavily varnished areas. Follow with compressed air through every passage. Repeat until the passages flow freely with no visible blockage.

**Step 6: Reassemble and reinstall.** Reassemble the carburettor in reverse order, checking that the float moves freely and the needle valve seats correctly. Reinstall on the engine, reconnect linkages and fuel line, and test.

## When DIY Cleaning Is Not Enough

Carburettor cleaning works when the problem is varnish and fuel deposits. It does not fix everything.

**Worn needle valves.** A needle valve that is not seating correctly causes fuel to overflow into the engine when the machine is not running (often evidenced by fuel running out of the air filter or engine flooding with raw fuel). The needle valve and seat need replacement, not cleaning.

**Damaged float.** A float that has absorbed fuel (floats are usually hollow brass or plastic) sinks rather than floating, causing a rich mixture. Replace rather than clean.

**Cracked or warped carburettor body.** Age and heat cycling can crack carburettor bodies. Cleaning a cracked body does not restore function.

**Carburettors with sealed or welded jets.** Some newer carburettors have pressed-in or sealed main jets that cannot be removed without damage. These require more careful cleaning or replacement.

If cleaning does not resolve the symptom, the next step is diagnosis at a shop. A technician can pressure-test the fuel system, inspect the carburettor under magnification, and determine whether the unit needs rebuild or replacement.

---

## When to Book It Instead

The DIY cleaning described above is appropriate for straightforward carburettor fouling on standard small engines. Consider booking at a shop when:

- The engine has not run in several years and has significant corrosion
- The cleaning attempt did not resolve the problem
- The equipment has any additional symptoms beyond the carburettor (compression loss, oil in air filter, excessive smoke)
- The carburettor is on a higher-value machine where incorrect reassembly has more consequence
