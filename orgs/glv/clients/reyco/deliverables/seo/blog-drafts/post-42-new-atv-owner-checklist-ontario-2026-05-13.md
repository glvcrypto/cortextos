Buying an ATV or UTV is the purchase decision. What you do in the first month determines how long the machine runs well and whether you are operating it safely in Northern Ontario's specific context. This checklist covers the registration requirements, the first-use checks, the break-in period, and the first service milestone that new owners often miss.

---

## Before the First Ride: Registration and Insurance

In Ontario, ATVs and UTVs that will be operated on public land, trails, or roadways require registration with the Ministry of Transportation and third-party liability insurance.

**MTO registration.** Register the vehicle through ServiceOntario. You will need the bill of sale, your licence plate (if transferring), and proof of insurance. The registration sticker goes on the machine. Operating an unregistered off-road vehicle on public land is a Highway Traffic Act violation.

**Insurance.** Third-party liability insurance is the minimum requirement for on-trail use in Ontario. For a machine stored at a property, comprehensive insurance that covers theft, fire, and collision is worth considering. Confirm with your insurance broker what coverage applies on private property versus on registered trail networks.

**OFATV trail permit.** If you plan to use Ontario Federation of All Terrain Vehicle Associations (OFATV) trails in the Algoma District, you need an annual trail permit in addition to the MTO registration. Permits are purchased through the OFATV and are separate from vehicle registration. The trail system in Northern Ontario is extensive but the permit requirement is enforced.

**Operator age and licence requirements.** The operator must be at least 16 years of age and hold a valid driver's licence to operate an ORV on public land in Ontario. Operators under 16 are limited to private property only.

## The First Physical Inspection

Before the first ride, walk the machine and confirm the following:

**Tyre pressure.** Check all four tyres against the specification in the owner's manual. Tyres that shipped low or lost pressure during transport create handling instability, particularly on ATVs where the rider's body position and the machine's balance are linked.

**Oil level.** Check the engine oil level on the dipstick with the machine on level ground. Some machines ship from the dealer with oil already at the correct level; confirm rather than assume.

**Brake function.** Test both front and rear brakes before operating at any speed. Squeeze the brake lever and push the machine to confirm the brakes engage firmly and release cleanly. Check that the parking brake (if equipped) holds on a slight slope.

**Fuel.** Fill with fresh, clean fuel. For a new machine, do not run the first tank from a container that has been sitting in a garage. Fresh pump fuel for the first use.

**Safety equipment.** A DOT-certified helmet is required for all ATV operators on Ontario public trails. Full face coverage is recommended for Northern Ontario trail use where branch and debris contact is common. Gloves, goggles, and sturdy footwear are standard.

## Break-In Period

Most ATV and UTV engines have a break-in period of approximately 10 to 25 hours, specified in the owner's manual. The break-in procedure varies by manufacturer, but the general principles are consistent.

**Vary the throttle.** Do not operate at full throttle or sustained high RPM during the break-in period. Varying the throttle allows the engine's internal components to seat gradually across the full operating range. Running continuously at one throttle position during break-in prevents even component seating.

**Avoid heavy sustained loads.** During break-in, avoid extended trailer towing at maximum weight, heavy uphill pulling, or sustained operation that keeps the engine near its maximum output. The engine needs the full break-in period under graduated load before it is ready for maximum sustained output.

**Check fasteners at 10 hours.** New vehicles vibrate enough in the first few hours of operation to loosen fasteners that were correctly torqued at the factory. At approximately 10 hours, check all accessible fasteners on the front suspension, rear axle, cargo rack, and any accessories for correct tightness.

## Trail Familiarisation in Northern Ontario

Northern Ontario trail systems are more demanding than trail systems in much of Canada. Algoma District bush trails involve exposed Shield rock, loose gravel over shale, mud, and sections where roots and ruts require active machine management.

For a new owner who is also new to off-road riding, start on accessible gravel roads and managed trail sections before progressing to technical terrain. ATV riding requires active weight management and instinctive response to terrain changes that develop with practice. A new machine and a new rider on challenging Northern Ontario terrain simultaneously is not the right starting point.

For UTV operators: side-by-sides are more forgiving to operate than ATVs for new riders because the passive stability does not require active balance management. However, ground clearance, vehicle width, and rollover risk on steep lateral slopes are still important to understand before attempting technical terrain.

## First Service Interval

Most ATVs and UTVs have a first service interval at 20 to 25 hours or one month, whichever comes first. This service includes:

- Engine oil and filter change (break-in oil removed and replaced with full-specification oil)
- Air filter inspection and cleaning
- Drive belt inspection (CVT belt condition after initial load)
- Brake inspection and adjustment
- All fastener check
- Lubrication of pivot points and suspension components

The first service interval on a new machine is not optional maintenance. It removes the break-in oil that has accumulated metal particles from the seating of engine components, and it documents the start of the service record for warranty purposes.
