The Toro Power Clear is the single-stage snowblower that comes up most consistently in conversations with Northern Ontario homeowners who have shorter driveways and lighter to moderate snowfall years. It is lighter than a two-stage unit, easier to manoeuvre, and quicker to store. It is also not the right machine for every Northern Ontario situation. ## What the Power Clear Is

The Toro Power Clear is a single-stage snowblower. Single-stage means the auger does the work of both picking up snow and throwing it. There is no separate impeller. The rubber auger paddles contact the ground surface directly and propel snow through the chute.

Toro's Power Clear is available in electric-start and pull-start configurations, in clearing widths from 18 to 21 inches depending on model year. Current models in the Power Clear lineup include the 518 ZE (18-inch, electric start) and the 521 E (21-inch, electric start). Engine sizes range from 99cc to 163cc across the lineup.

This is the machine for driveways up to approximately 20 metres and snowfall up to roughly 30 centimetres per clearing event. It is not a heavy snow machine.

---

## Where It Performs Well

**Light to moderate snowfall.** For the typical Northern Ontario snowfall event of 10 to 25 centimetres of dry or lightly packed snow, the Power Clear handles the clearing quickly and with minimal effort. The rubber auger paddles grip the pavement surface and propel the machine forward, meaning you are guiding rather than pushing.

**Short to medium driveways.** The Power Clear's clearing width and throw distance are appropriate for driveways up to a car length or two and sidewalks. Clearing a 30-metre driveway with a Power Clear takes longer than a two-stage unit, but the machine is light enough that the operator does not fatigue on a typical residential clearing.

**Pavement and packed surfaces.** The auger contacts the ground directly, which clears pavement to near-bare on each pass. On a stamped concrete or interlocking stone surface, the clearance can be adjusted to avoid dragging the auger across the surface.

**Storage and handling.** The Power Clear weighs significantly less than a two-stage snowblower. It stores vertically (Toro offers this as a marketed feature on some models), which reduces the footprint in a garage or shed. For a property where storage space is limited, this matters.

---

## Where It Falls Short for Northern Ontario Use

**Heavy snowfall events.** When snowfall exceeds 30 centimetres in a single event, or when the snow is wet and dense, the Power Clear's single-stage design is at its limit. Wet snow in particular is harder for rubber auger paddles to handle than a two-stage impeller system. The machine can clog and slow significantly in heavy wet snow.

**Large driveways.** A 40-metre driveway cleared with an 18 or 21-inch wide single-stage machine is a lot of passes. Clearing time and operator effort both increase significantly relative to a two-stage unit with a wider clearing path.

**Deep compacted snow at the end of the driveway.** The windrow left by the plough at the end of a driveway is often compacted and heavy. This is where the Power Clear's limitations are most apparent. The single-stage design handles fresh snow better than packed, ploughed windrows.

**Gravel driveways.** The rubber auger contacts the ground directly. On gravel, this picks up stones along with snow and throws them through the chute, which can damage the machine and create a hazard. Two-stage machines ride above the surface; single-stage machines do not. The Power Clear is designed for paved surfaces.

## Power Clear vs Two-Stage: The Northern Ontario Decision

For an SSM homeowner, the decision usually comes down to driveway length and typical snowfall intensity.

**The Power Clear makes sense if:** Driveway is 20 metres or less, snowfall events are typically dry and moderate (under 30cm), the surface is paved, and storage space is a factor.

**A two-stage makes sense if:** Driveway is longer than 20 metres, heavy snowfall events are typical, the driveway includes a compacted windrow from the plough, or the surface is gravel.

SSM's snowfall patterns vary. The city receives significant lake-effect influence in some years, which produces heavier snow events than inland areas at the same latitude. Buyers who have previously found smaller single-stage machines inadequate should consider whether a two-stage is the more appropriate purchase, even at a higher price.

## Auger Paddle Wear: The Maintenance Item Most Owners Miss

The rubber auger paddles on a Power Clear are the single component that determines whether the machine clears effectively. They are also consumables that wear and need periodic replacement.

New rubber paddles grip the pavement surface firmly and propel the machine efficiently through snow. As the paddles wear, the rubber thins. Worn paddles lose their grip on the surface, clear snow less completely, and eventually expose the metal backing of the paddle assembly. Metal contact with pavement is audible (scraping) and will damage the auger housing and the pavement surface.

The rate of wear depends on the surface being cleared and how often the machine is used. On smooth poured concrete, paddles wear slowly. On rougher asphalt or textured surfaces, wear is faster. On any surface with minor irregularities (cracks, settled edges, frost heaves), the paddles take additional contact load.

Inspect the paddles at the start of each season. The rubber should have consistent thickness from edge to edge. If one edge is significantly thinner than the other, the paddle is wearing unevenly and should be replaced. If the total thickness is less than about half the original, replace before the season. Replacement paddles for Power Clear models are a standard part available at Toro dealers.

Replacing auger paddles on a Power Clear is a DIY-accessible job on most models. The paddle assembly unbolts from the auger shaft with standard hand tools. It is the kind of service that takes less than an hour the first time and less than 30 minutes subsequently.
