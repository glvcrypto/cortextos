A new snowblower comes ready to operate, but operating it correctly in the first season and preparing it properly for storage at the end are what determine whether it runs as well in year five as it did in year one. Northern Ontario winters are demanding. A machine used heavily from November through March and then stored incorrectly will accumulate problems that are entirely avoidable.

This is what a new snowblower owner in Ontario needs to know for the first season and the end-of-season transition.

---

## Before the First Use

When you take delivery of a new snowblower, a few things are worth checking before you run it for the first time.

**Oil level.** Two-stage snowblowers with four-stroke engines require engine oil. The machine ships from the factory with oil, but it is worth confirming the level before first start. Check the dipstick with the machine on level ground.

**Fuel.** Fill with fresh, regular unleaded fuel. Use 87 octane or higher. Do not use fuel with more than 10 percent ethanol. If you are filling a two-stroke machine (some handheld units), confirm the correct oil-to-fuel mixture ratio for your specific engine, which is in the operator's manual.

**Shear bolts.** Two-stage machines ship with shear bolts installed in the auger shaft. Locate where the spares are stored on the machine (typically a small container attached to the housing) and confirm you have them. Shear bolts are the first line of defence when the auger hits a frozen object; having spares on the machine means you can clear a bolt failure on the spot.

**Skid shoe height.** Set the skid shoes to the correct height for your driveway surface before the first use. Shoes set too high leave snow on the pavement; shoes set too low scrape the surface and wear faster. For paved concrete or asphalt, shoes are set at or near the bottom of the housing. For gravel or uneven surfaces, raise the shoes slightly to clear the surface.

## Operating in the First Season

**Work with the snow, not against it.** Two-stage machines are self-propelled. You guide the machine; you do not push it. Trying to force a two-stage machine into deep or hard-packed snow by pushing will bog the engine and strain the drive system. Match the ground speed to the snow depth: in light snow, move at a brisk pace; in heavy or compacted snow, slow down and let the auger feed at its own rate.

**Manage wet snow differently.** Northern Ontario wet, heavy snow (common in November and March at freezing temperatures) has a tendency to pack in the discharge chute. Do not run a wet-snow clearing session at top speed. Slow down, and if the chute packs, stop the auger before clearing the blockage. Never clear a chute blockage with your hand; use the chute clean-out tool that shipped with the machine. The auger can restart suddenly even with the engine idling.

**Know where the buried edges are.** A new snowblower owner with a new machine sometimes drives the auger housing over a curb edge, a retaining edge, or a lawn transition. The shear bolts protect the drivetrain, but replacing multiple shear bolts in one clearing session is an indication that the machine is hitting something it should not. Walk the driveway perimeter before the first snowfall and note any edges or transitions that will be hidden by snow.

## Mid-Season Checks

Halfway through the season, run through a short inspection:

**Belt condition.** On a two-stage machine, the drive and auger belts are accessible under the housing. After a half-season of heavy use, check that the belts are not visibly cracking or glazed. A belt that has lost flexibility will slip or break in the second half of the season.

**Auger condition.** Inspect the rubber paddles (on single-stage machines) or the auger blades (on two-stage machines) for damage. A rubber paddle that has cracked or separated from the metal backing causes uneven clearing and increasing vibration.

**Chute and deflector.** Confirm the chute rotates freely and the deflector cable operates correctly. A chute that is stiff or binds means a lubrication service is needed.

---

## End-of-Season Storage

This is the step most first-year snowblower owners miss.

**Fuel:** Run the machine until the fuel tank is low, then add fuel stabiliser to the remaining fuel. Alternatively, drain the fuel completely and run the carburettor dry by operating the machine until it runs out of fuel. Fresh fuel with stabiliser is the preferred approach.

**Oil change (four-stroke engines):** Change the engine oil at the end of the season, not the beginning of the next one. Old oil is acidic and sitting on a four-stroke engine for six months over summer accelerates internal wear. Fresh oil goes into storage.

**Cleaning:** Remove packed snow and ice from the auger housing, chute, and impeller area before storage. Moisture trapped in packed snow causes corrosion on steel components over the summer.

**Storage location:** Store indoors if possible. A snowblower stored in an unheated garage through a Northern Ontario summer experiences large temperature swings, but this is acceptable. Outdoor storage under UV exposure accelerates rubber component degradation faster.
