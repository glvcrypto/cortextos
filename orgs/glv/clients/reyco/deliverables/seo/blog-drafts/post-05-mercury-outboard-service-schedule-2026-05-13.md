Most Mercury outboard owners know that service is required. Fewer know what Mercury's actual schedule requires, and fewer still follow it consistently. The gap between "I've had it serviced" and "it has been serviced at the correct intervals" is where most engine problems originate.

This is what Mercury's service intervals require for recreational four-stroke outboards, and why each item exists.

---

## The Two Service Triggers

Mercury uses two triggers for service intervals: hours run and calendar time, with the first to arrive winning.

**Every 100 hours or annually, whichever comes first.**

This is the primary service trigger for most Northern Ontario recreational boaters. In practice, a typical seasonal boater puts 50 to 80 hours on a recreational outboard in a season. That means calendar time (annually) drives the service schedule for most owners, not hours.

If you run significantly more than 100 hours in a season (charter use, heavy recreational use, fishing guide operations), the hours trigger fires first and you are looking at mid-season service.

A second service interval applies to the first season of a new engine:

**Initial 20-hour service on new engines.**

New Mercury outboards require a 20-hour break-in service. This is not a manufacturer suggestion. It is a warranty requirement on most models. During break-in, the engine runs in a slightly restricted mode and small amounts of metal from new components circulate in the oil. At 20 hours, the oil is changed and the break-in is complete. Skipping this service can affect warranty coverage.

---

## What the Annual Service Includes

The 100-hour/annual service covers the following on a typical Mercury four-stroke outboard:

**Engine oil and filter.** The oil change interval is not long in marine applications. Outboards run in a humid, salty (if used in brackish water), high-vibration environment. Contaminated oil shortens engine life.

**Gear oil (lower unit).** The lower unit is drained and refilled. What comes out of the lower unit tells a story. Clean gear oil is dark and consistent. Milky gear oil means water intrusion, which points to a failed seal somewhere in the lower unit. Catching this at service is far less expensive than catching it after a failure.

**Water pump impeller.** The impeller is the rubber component that drives water through the cooling system. Mercury recommends impeller replacement on a scheduled basis rather than waiting for failure, because a failed impeller at speed causes overheating in minutes. Standard practice at an authorised service centre is to inspect or replace based on condition and hours at each service.

**Spark plugs.** Inspected and replaced as needed. Fouled or worn plugs affect starting, fuel economy, and smooth running.

**Fuel system check.** Fuel filter inspection, lines checked for brittleness and cracking, primer bulb condition assessed.

**Corrosion inspection.** The lower unit, mounting hardware, and electrical connections are inspected for corrosion. Northern Ontario outboards run in freshwater, which is better than saltwater, but corrosion still develops at connection points and in unpainted crevices.

**Throttle and shift linkage.** Cable tension, condition, and operation are verified.

## Where Service Fits in the Season

Spring commissioning is the natural point to handle annual service. Most Northern Ontario owners who bring their boats to Reyco for spring commissioning are getting both the commissioning and the annual Mercury service done at the same time, because the procedures overlap.

If your engine reached 100 hours mid-season last year and you did not service it, that service should happen before this spring launch, not after.

Fall winterisation is a separate procedure. Some owners do a simplified service check in fall as part of storing the engine. Full service at spring commissioning is the more common approach in this region.

## Authorised vs Non-Authorised Service

Mercury's maintenance schedules specify OEM parts for items like impellers and filters. Using aftermarket parts for routine service is common at independent shops and rarely causes immediate problems, but it does create a documentation gap if a warranty claim arises.

More importantly, authorised Mercury service centres are required to have the diagnostic tools Mercury releases for their engine lineup. These tools catch issues that generic scanners miss, particularly in the ECM and fuel injection systems on current Mercury four-stroke engines.

For warranty coverage, service performed at a non-authorised facility does not automatically void the warranty, but service that skips required intervals or uses non-approved parts can affect claims on components related to those items.

## If You Are Behind on Service

An engine that is one service interval overdue is not a crisis. An engine that has run several seasons without a complete service is a different situation.

If you do not know when the gear oil was last changed, or you have never had a confirmed impeller replacement, those items should be addressed before the engine takes full-season load. Gear oil and impeller condition are inspectable in a morning.
