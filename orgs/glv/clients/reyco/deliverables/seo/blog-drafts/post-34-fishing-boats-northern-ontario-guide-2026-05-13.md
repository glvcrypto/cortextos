## Part One: What Kind of Boat Is Right for Northern Ontario

The answer depends on which Northern Ontario lakes you intend to fish and how you intend to use the boat.

### Shield Lakes vs Lake Superior vs Cottage Lakes

Northern Ontario has three distinct lake types, and each one pulls the buying decision in a different direction.

**Shield lakes** are the dominant lake type across the Algoma District and most of Northern Ontario. These are hard-rock Canadian Shield lakes: rocky shorelines, irregular depth profiles, cold and clear water, and populations of walleye, northern pike, bass, and lake trout depending on the latitude. Shield lakes range from small wilderness lakes accessible only by portage or float plane to large bodies of water reachable by gravel road and a gravel boat launch. Most Northern Ontario fishing happens on Shield lakes.

For Shield lake fishing, an aluminum fishing boat in the 14 to 19-foot range is the standard. The rocky shorelines and variable water depths require a boat that can absorb contact, run in relatively shallow water over rock, and be launched from informal ramps. Aluminum hull construction handles all of these conditions better than fibreglass.

**Lake Superior** is a different environment entirely. It is the largest freshwater lake on earth by surface area, and it behaves accordingly. Wave heights on Lake Superior in adverse conditions can exceed two metres. Running on Lake Superior requires a boat that was designed for open-water conditions: a deep-V hull with adequate freeboard, sufficient horsepower to maintain headway in following seas, and a boat length that provides enough hull speed to handle wave frequency. For Lake Superior day trips and island runs out of Sault Ste. Marie, a 17 to 19-foot aluminum fishing boat with a 115hp or larger four-stroke is the minimum practical platform for anything more than flat-calm morning runs.

**Cottage and resort lakes** in the Northern Ontario and Algoma District area vary from small sheltered lakes with controlled access to larger lakes with more wind exposure. For predominantly calm cottage lakes where the primary use is family fishing, swimming, and casual cruising, a smaller aluminum fishing boat or a pontoon boat may be appropriate depending on the use pattern. Cottage buyers who will also fish seriously typically choose a fishing boat over a pontoon for its versatility.

### Aluminum Dominates the Northern Ontario Market

Unlike southern Ontario and the lower Great Lakes region, where fibreglass fishing boats have a significant market share, Northern Ontario's fishing boat market is overwhelmingly aluminum. The reasons are practical: aluminum is lighter than fibreglass at equivalent dimensions, it handles rock contact better without catastrophic damage, it is easier to repair after impact, and it is less expensive to manufacture in the fishing boat size range.

A Northern Ontario buyer who chooses fibreglass over aluminum should have a specific reason that outweighs these factors. For most buyers in this region, aluminum is the correct starting point.

### Boat Size and Use Pattern

The most common fishing boat size in the Northern Ontario market is 16 to 18 feet. This size range handles two to three anglers comfortably, has enough hull for open water use on larger lakes, and is manageable on a standard trailer at typical Northern Ontario boat launches.

**14-16 foot boats** are suited to smaller lake systems, solo or paired fishing, and buyers for whom easy trailering and launch access on narrow gravel roads is a priority. These boats are capable of Shield lake fishing in typical conditions but have limited range and reduced rough-water capability compared to larger hulls.

**17-19 foot boats** are the workhorse segment. This is where the Princecraft Yukon, the most commonly chosen Northern Ontario multi-species fishing boat, sits. Adequate hull for Lake Superior day use, enough deck for two or three anglers fishing effectively, and self-propelled enough to handle a full load of gear and passengers without bogging down.

**20-foot and larger** boats are less common in the Northern Ontario recreational market outside of charter and commercial use. At this size, the boat is less manoeuvrable on the kinds of smaller access routes common in the Algoma District, and the cost premium is significant.

## Part Two: Aluminum vs Fibreglass in Northern Ontario

The aluminum-versus-fibreglass comparison comes up when buyers are considering moving into the upper-middle tier of the fishing boat market, where fibreglass models from some manufacturers compete with aluminum on features and finish. Here is what the comparison actually means in Northern Ontario conditions.

### Why Aluminum Handles Northern Ontario Better

**Rock contact tolerance.** Shield lake rock is unforgiving. An aluminum hull that contacts a submerged rock at low speed will typically dent or gouge. A fibreglass hull under the same circumstances can crack, delaminate, or fracture. A dented aluminum hull continues to float and function. A cracked fibreglass hull may not, and the repair is structurally complex. For buyers who fish rock-strewn Northern Ontario lakes (which is almost all Northern Ontario lake fishing), aluminum's rock tolerance is a genuine practical advantage, not just a sales point.

**Weight and draught.** Aluminum boats in the 17 to 18-foot range are significantly lighter than equivalent fibreglass boats. Lower weight means shallower draught, which matters when you are backing off a gravel ramp into two feet of water or running over the shallow rock humps that mark submerged points on Shield lakes. A lighter boat also accelerates more efficiently on the same horsepower, which matters for fuel economy and getting to the fishing spots at a reasonable pace.

**Repairability.** An aluminum hull can be repaired by a competent welder. Structural fibreglass repairs require gelcoat work, layup skills, and access to materials that are less available in Northern Ontario communities than welding equipment. For a boat used at a remote cottage or a remote lake system, aluminum's repairability with local tools is a real advantage.

**Maintenance.** Aluminum boats do not require the regular waxing, oxidation treatment, and gelcoat maintenance that fibreglass hulls need to maintain their appearance and structural integrity. In a Northern Ontario context where boats often sit on trailers in unheated spaces through long winters, this low-maintenance characteristic is significant.

### When Fibreglass Makes Sense

Fibreglass fishing boats have advantages in specific areas: they provide a quieter ride at speed because fibreglass dampens hull vibration better than aluminum, they offer a smoother finish for open-water performance on large bodies of water, and they allow more complex hull geometries that some manufacturers use to optimize hull speed and stability.

For Northern Ontario buyers who primarily fish large, open-water systems (Lake Superior, Lake Huron, or large inland lakes) where smooth water performance and ride comfort matter more than rocky shore resilience, a fibreglass boat in the appropriate size range may be worth considering. For buyers whose use includes frequent launches on rocky gravel ramps, lake fishing near rock structure, and access to smaller lake systems, the aluminum advantage holds.

The bottom line for most Algoma District fishing: aluminum is the appropriate choice for the conditions the boat will actually face.

## Part Three: Princecraft Boats

Princecraft is a Canadian boat manufacturer that produces aluminum fishing boats, pontoons, and utility boats specifically for the Canadian freshwater fishing market. Understanding the brand helps buyers evaluate what they are getting relative to alternatives.

### Who Makes Princecraft

Princecraft boats are manufactured in Princeville, Quebec, a small community in the Centre-du-Québec region with a long history of aluminum boat production. The brand is part of the Brunswick Corporation distribution network, which is the same parent company that owns Mercury Marine, Lund, and several other marine brands. Brunswick's scale gives Princecraft access to manufacturing infrastructure and distribution capability that smaller independent boat manufacturers cannot match.

The Canadian manufacturing location is not simply a marketing point. It means the boats are designed for Canadian freshwater fishing conditions, the production quality control is performed against Canadian regulatory standards, and the dealer network through which the boats are sold is structured for the Canadian market.

### Princecraft Hull Construction

Princecraft uses welded aluminum construction throughout their fishing boat lineup. Welded aluminum joints are structurally superior to riveted construction: the welds form a continuous metal bond rather than a mechanical joint, which means no individual rivet to loosen over time and no rivet seams where water can penetrate. Premium aluminum fishing boats have been welded rather than riveted since welding technology became cost-effective in marine production in the late 1980s and 1990s.

Welded aluminum also holds up better to the repeated impact and flex stress of rocky lake use. A riveted hull that contacts rock repeatedly may develop loosening rivets and developing seam leaks; a welded hull under the same conditions shows denting or wear on the exterior without compromising the structural joints.

## Part Five: Choosing the Right Outboard for a Northern Ontario Fishing Boat

The outboard motor is as important as the hull for a Northern Ontario fishing boat purchase. A correctly matched engine makes the hull perform as designed. An undersized engine is a constant limitation; an oversized engine wastes money and adds weight without benefit.

**Matching engine to hull rating.** Every fishing boat hull has a maximum horsepower rating on the capacity plate at the transom. This is the upper limit. The correct engine is typically in the range of 75 to 100 percent of the hull's maximum rating, depending on intended use and load.

**HP for typical Northern Ontario use.** For a 17 to 19-foot fishing boat used on inland Shield lakes with two to three passengers and gear, engines in the 90 to 115hp range are the most common choice. For any use that includes Lake Superior runs, a 115hp minimum is the practical floor, with 150hp providing more useful reserve power in changing conditions.

**Four-stroke is the standard.** Modern Mercury four-stroke outboards dominate the current Northern Ontario market. They are quieter than two-stroke engines, more fuel-efficient at cruise, and cleaner in operation. The older assumption that two-stroke engines are more powerful has been overtaken by current four-stroke designs, which produce comparable power at competitive weight.

**Break-in matters.** A new Mercury outboard requires a 20-hour break-in period with varied throttle operation. Buyers who put a new engine directly into heavy use without following the break-in procedure risk internal engine wear patterns that affect performance and longevity. The break-in is covered in the owner's manual and at the first service appointment with the authorised dealer.

For a Northern Ontario fishing boat purchase, the hull and engine are both decisions that benefit from a dealer conversation rather than an online configuration. The right combination for a specific buyer depends on use, load, and the specific water the boat will operate on.
