Spring commissioning is a defined process, not a general tune-up. It is the systematic reversal of everything done to put a boat into winter storage, combined with the service work that the engine and hull require at the start of a new season. Understanding what commissioning covers helps you evaluate what is being done to your boat and why each element matters.

---

## Why Northern Ontario Commissioning Is Not Optional

Northern Ontario winters are long. A boat stored from October to May sits for seven months. Fuel degrades. Rubber components age. Electrical connections corrode. Water that was not fully drained from the engine block or raw water cooling circuit can cause damage in below-zero temperatures. The interval between the end of last season and the beginning of this one is enough time for a properly winterised boat to develop issues that go undetected until the engine is started, or worse, until the boat is already on the water.

Spring commissioning is the gate between storage and use. It confirms the boat is actually ready rather than assumed to be.

---

## What Spring Commissioning Covers

### Engine Service

The outboard receives the most attention during commissioning. The core engine service includes:

**Oil change (four-stroke engines).** Engine oil that sat in the crankcase over winter has absorbed moisture and begun to acidify. Fresh oil goes in before the season starts.

**Fuel system flush and inspection.** Even with fuel stabiliser added at winterisation, carburettors and fuel lines are inspected for varnish buildup, cracking, or blockage. An engine that ran cleanly at the end of last season may have developed carburettor deposits during a long storage interval.

**Water pump impeller replacement.** The rubber impeller in the water pump circulates cooling water through the engine. Impellers are replaced annually on most service schedules because they degrade with heat cycling and can fail without warning. A failed impeller at speed produces engine overheating in minutes. This is one of the few service items where preventive replacement is cheaper than the downstream repair.

**Spark plugs.** Inspected and replaced if at service limit.

**Gear lube change.** The lower unit gear lube is drained and replaced. Gear lube that has turned milky indicates a seal leak that needs repair before the boat runs.

**Belts and cables.** Throttle and shift cables are inspected for binding or fraying. Steering cables are checked for stiffness or corrosion at the pivot points.

### Fuel System and Tank

The fuel tank is inspected for sediment or water contamination. On boats where the winterisation procedure included running the engine dry, fresh fuel is added and the system is primed before first start. Fuel line fittings and primer bulbs are inspected for cracking or hardening, which is a common failure point on rubber components that sit for extended periods.

### Electrical System

**Battery condition.** A battery that was properly maintained on a trickle charger through winter typically shows good capacity. A battery that was left discharged or in cold storage without maintenance may no longer hold a full charge. Battery state of health is tested before the boat leaves the shop.

**Bilge pump.** The bilge pump float switch is tested for correct operation. A bilge pump that does not activate automatically is a safety risk that may not be apparent until the boat is in the water.

**Navigation lights.** All navigation lights are tested for function. Running lights that fail an inspection are replaced at commissioning rather than on the water after dark.

**Connections and terminals.** Salt, moisture, and vibration corrode electrical connections over time and over winter. Critical connections are inspected and cleaned where needed.

### Hull and Hardware

**Hull inspection.** The hull is examined for impact damage from the previous season. Aluminium boats develop dings and stress points from contact with rocky lake bottoms and launch ramps that are not always visible until the hull is out of the water and clean.

**Through-hulls and fittings.** All through-hull fittings, drain plugs, and hull penetrations are inspected and confirmed seated. A drain plug that is loose or missing is the most common preventable cause of a sinking at launch.

**Trailer and bunks.** The trailer is inspected for bearing condition, tyre pressure, and bunk alignment. A boat that sits on misaligned bunks over winter can develop hull distortion.

### Safety Equipment Check

**Transport Canada minimum requirements** for the boat's length and waterway apply at all times. Spring commissioning is when safety equipment is inventoried and replaced as needed: throwable flotation, fire extinguisher date, flares expiry, sound-signalling device, bailer or bilge pump presence. Equipment that expired or was not restocked after last season gets addressed before the first trip.

---

## What Commissioning Is Not

Commissioning is a systematic inspection and service process. It is not a repair estimate. Items discovered during commissioning that require repair work beyond routine service are quoted separately and discussed with the owner before work proceeds.

A boat that was improperly winterised, stored outdoors on inadequate supports, or ran into a mechanical problem at the end of last season may have significant work required before it is ready to use. Commissioning identifies those items. The cost of commissioning is separate from the cost of any repair work discovered during it.

## When to Book in Sault Ste. Marie

In SSM, boats come out of storage from late April through May. Service demand peaks in mid-May as buyers aim to be on the water for the long weekend. Booking commissioning in April means the work is completed before the rush and the boat is ready when the weather cooperates.
