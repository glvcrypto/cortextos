A lawn mower or tractor that sat through a Northern Ontario winter without proper preparation will spend the first weeks of spring in the shop rather than on the lawn. The off-season runs six months in this region. Fuel degrades completely in that interval. Rubber components crack. Batteries discharge. The machines that start reliably in April are the ones that were put away correctly in October.

---

## Why Northern Ontario Winterization Is a Distinct Problem

Lawn equipment in warmer climates may sit for two or three months between seasons. Northern Ontario equipment sits for six. That difference is not marginal.

Ethanol-blended gasoline, which is the standard at most Canadian fuel stations, has a shelf life of approximately 30 days before it begins to degrade. After six months in a fuel tank and carburettor, ethanol-blended fuel will have separated, varnished the carburettor bowl and jets, and left deposits that prevent normal fuel flow. A machine stored with untreated fuel and a full carburettor in October will not start cleanly in April. It will require at minimum a carburettor cleaning, and often a full rebuild if the varnish has hardened.

Beyond fuel, the six-month interval is enough time for battery self-discharge, blade and deck corrosion, belt cracking, and tyre pressure loss to compound to the point where the machine requires significant attention before it is ready to work. None of this is wear. All of it is preventable.

---

## Fuel System Preparation

The fuel system is the single most important item in lawn equipment winterization. There are two approaches:

**Run the fuel system dry.** Start the machine after the last mowing session and run it until it stops from fuel starvation. Then drain any remaining fuel from the tank. A dry fuel system cannot varnish. This is the cleanest approach for a four-stroke engine, though it leaves internal fuel system components dry during storage, which is not a problem for engines of normal age and condition.

**Add fuel stabiliser and run it through.** Add a quality fuel stabiliser to the remaining fuel at the manufacturer's recommended ratio. Run the engine for five to ten minutes to circulate stabilised fuel through the carburettor and fuel lines. This protects the fuel system without running the system dry. For a fuel-injected engine or any engine where running it dry is impractical, stabiliser treatment is the standard approach.

For either approach: do not leave unstabilised, untreated ethanol-blended fuel in the system through a Northern Ontario winter.

## Engine Preparation

**Oil change.** Four-stroke lawn tractor engines should have their oil changed at end of season, not beginning of next season. Used engine oil contains combustion byproducts and trace acids. Oil that sits in contact with engine surfaces for six months accelerates internal corrosion compared to fresh oil. Change the oil in October; the engine goes into storage with clean oil protecting the internals.

**Spark plug inspection.** Remove and inspect the spark plug. A plug that is worn, fouled, or cracked should be replaced now rather than discovered next spring when the machine fails to start. While the plug is out, spray a small amount of fogging oil or light machine oil into the cylinder through the plug hole, then turn the engine over by hand once to distribute the oil before reinstalling the plug. This protects the cylinder wall and piston rings during storage.

**Air filter.** Clean or replace the air filter. A clogged or fouled air filter that goes into storage will be worse in spring after six months of sitting. Clean filters also prevent the debris and nest material that insects and rodents pack into stored equipment over winter.

---

## Battery Preparation

Lawn tractor batteries self-discharge during storage. A 12-volt battery left in the tractor through a Northern Ontario winter without a maintenance charger will typically be at 40 to 60 percent capacity by spring, and may have sulfated to the point where it cannot be fully recovered.

The correct approach is to either remove the battery and store it in a warmer space on a battery maintenance charger (trickle charger), or leave it in the machine and connect a maintenance charger that keeps it at full charge without overcharging.

A battery that has been properly maintained through storage starts the machine on the first attempt in April. A battery that self-discharged all winter may start the machine a few times before failing, or may not start it at all.

## Blade, Deck, and Drive System

**Blade removal and inspection.** Remove mowing blades before storage. Inspect for cracks, impact damage at the tips, or significant wear. A cracked blade is a safety risk that should not go back on the machine. While the blades are off, inspect the blade mounting spindles and spindle housings for damage.

**Deck cleaning.** Grass clippings packed on the underside of the deck retain moisture and accelerate rust through the winter. Remove packed clippings with a scraper, then rinse and dry the deck before storage. A light spray of penetrating oil on bare steel areas reduces rust formation during storage.

**Drive belts.** Inspect the mowing deck drive belt and traction drive belt for cracking, glazing, or fraying. Belts that showed these signs through the season should be replaced now. Belts that fail in spring are inconvenient; belts replaced in fall are handled on your schedule.

**Tyres.** Set tyre pressure to the manufacturer's specification before storage. Lawn tractor tyres that store at low pressure develop flat spots that cause vibration when the machine is first used. Correct pressure before storage prevents this.

## Storage and Service in Sault Ste. Marie

Store lawn equipment in a covered space. Outdoor storage under a tarp through a Northern Ontario winter exposes rubber, plastic, and painted components to UV, freeze-thaw cycling, and moisture in ways that accelerated deterioration compared to indoor storage.
