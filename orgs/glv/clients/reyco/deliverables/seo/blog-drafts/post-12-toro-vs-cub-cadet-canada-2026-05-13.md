If you are buying a new lawn mower or outdoor power unit in Northern Ontario, Toro and Cub Cadet will both come up. They occupy similar market positions, share some distribution overlap, and appear to serve the same buyers on paper. The decision between them is not obvious from a distance, but it becomes clearer when you look at what each brand actually builds and where each one performs better.

---

## Who Makes These Brands

Both Toro and Cub Cadet are now part of large equipment conglomerates, but they have distinct manufacturing and design identities.

**Toro** is an American manufacturer headquartered in Bloomington, Minnesota, founded in 1914. Toro focuses heavily on the professional and commercial landscape market, and their residential lineup reflects that heritage. The residential product lines (TimeCutter zero-turns, Recycler walk-behinds) are designed by an organisation that understands high-use equipment and durability because their commercial customers demand it.

**Cub Cadet** is also an American brand, founded in 1961 as a product of International Harvester. It is now part of MTD Products (a Stanley Black & Decker company). Cub Cadet has historically focused on the homeowner market, which has produced a broad product lineup, strong attachment compatibility, and a product development direction oriented toward what residential owners want rather than what commercial landscapers need.

Neither brand is manufactured in Canada, but both have well-established Canadian dealer networks and both are designed for the climate ranges that Northern Ontario buyers will subject equipment to.

---

## Walk-Behind Mowers

In the walk-behind category, Toro and Cub Cadet both produce well-regarded residential models, and the differences are subtle.

Toro's Recycler series is one of the most widely recognised residential walk-behind lineups in North America. The Personal Pace self-propel system, which adjusts drive speed to match the operator's walking pace, is a Toro innovation and is generally considered the benchmark for residential self-propelled mowers. Toro's blade designs for mulching (the Recycler blade system) are effective and well-documented.

Cub Cadet's walk-behind lineup is competent but less differentiated. The brand's walk-behind models perform well in standard residential conditions and are priced similarly to comparable Toro models. For buyers whose priority is a reliable mower without specific interest in features, either brand at comparable specifications will serve roughly equally.

For most Northern Ontario walk-behind buyers, Toro's self-propel system and mulching performance give it a slight advantage for buyers who prioritise those features.

## Riding Mowers and Lawn Tractors

This is where the two brands diverge more meaningfully.

**Cub Cadet's XT series** lawn tractors are purpose-built for attachment use. The front and rear attachment points, the PTO configurations, and the structural design of the XT series assume that the machine will be used for more than mowing. If you want a snow blower attachment for fall, a front blade for gravel driveway maintenance, or an aerator for spring, the Cub Cadet XT platform supports that more comprehensively than Toro's residential riding lineup.

For Northern Ontario properties where one machine needs to handle both summer mowing and fall supplemental snow or property maintenance, Cub Cadet's tractor platform is the more versatile choice.

**Toro's residential riding mowers** are well-built and simple to operate, but they are designed primarily for mowing. The product line is less focused on attachment versatility than Cub Cadet's. For buyers whose use is predominantly mowing with no expectation of attachment use, Toro's riding mowers are straightforward and reliable.

---

## Zero-Turn Mowers

Both brands produce residential zero-turn lineups, and this is where the comparison is closest.

**Toro's TimeCutter series** is a mature product line with strong reviews and a reputation for straightforward operation and good cut quality. The TimeCutter is designed for residential use and performs well on flat, open properties.

**Cub Cadet's RZT and Ultima series** zero-turns span entry to near-commercial specifications. The Ultima series in particular is built to a higher standard than typical residential zero-turns, with stronger deck and frame construction that approaches light commercial duty. For buyers who want a zero-turn that will last on larger properties or under more intensive use, the Cub Cadet Ultima series is worth considering.

For straightforward residential zero-turn use, both brands are competitive. Cub Cadet's higher-end zero-turn lineup has more upside for buyers who are mowing larger or more demanding properties.
