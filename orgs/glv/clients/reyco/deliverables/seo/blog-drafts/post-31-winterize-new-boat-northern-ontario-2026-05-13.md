Most boat dealers are thorough at the point of sale and at the point of delivery. What gets covered less consistently is the end-of-season conversation: the specific steps that determine whether a Northern Ontario boat emerges from seven months of storage ready to run, or arrives at spring commissioning with problems that were preventable.

This is the winterization information that does not always make it into the conversation at the dealership.

---

## The Northern Ontario Storage Interval Is Longer Than Most Guides Assume

Published winterization guides are often written for a general North American audience. They assume a four to five month off-season. Northern Ontario is not that.

In Sault Ste. Marie, the boating season typically ends in October and restarts in May. That is a seven-month interval. Fuel has seven months to degrade. Rubber components have seven months to age. Water that was not completely cleared from the cooling system has seven months of freeze-thaw cycling. The stakes on getting winterization right are higher here than in markets with a shorter winter.

Everything in this article should be read against that seven-month reference point.

---

## Check the Lower Unit Gear Lube Before Storage

Dealers mention gear lube changes at scheduled service intervals. What they do not always emphasise is why it needs to happen before storage specifically.

Lower unit gear lube should be drained and inspected every year at winterization. The specific reason is this: the lube you drain tells you whether the lower unit has a water seal leak. If the drained lube is milky white or grey rather than its original amber colour, water has entered the gear case through a damaged seal. A small seal leak that was not causing obvious problems during the season will cause serious damage if the water-contaminated lube sits in the gear case through a Northern Ontario winter. The water freezes, expands, and damages the bearings, gears, or the housing itself.

Catching a milky gear lube in October costs a seal replacement and a lube refill. Missing it until spring commissioning the following year may cost a lower unit overhaul.

Drain the gear lube before storage, look at the colour, and act on what you find.

## Fog the Engine Cylinders

Engine fogging is the step most often left off dealer winterization briefings. It is straightforward and takes a few minutes.

Fogging oil is a light corrosion-inhibiting oil sprayed into the engine's air intake or directly into the spark plug holes before the engine is stored. The oil coats the cylinder walls and piston rings, preventing corrosion during the months the engine is not running.

Cylinder wall corrosion during storage is one of the contributors to increased oil consumption and reduced compression in engines that have been stored repeatedly without fogging. It is not always visible as a problem in the first winter, but it accumulates. Engines that are fogged correctly for several winters in a row show meaningfully better internal condition at high hours than engines that were not.

The fogging oil and procedure are in the Mercury owner's manual. It takes under ten minutes. Do it at the end of every season.

## Ethanol in Fuel Is a Storage Problem

Most service stations in Northern Ontario supply fuel blended with up to ten percent ethanol. Ethanol has specific behaviour in stored fuel systems that is worth understanding.

Ethanol is hygroscopic: it absorbs water from the air. Over a seven-month storage interval, ethanol-blended fuel in a marine tank can absorb enough moisture to cause phase separation, where the ethanol and water separate from the gasoline and settle to the bottom of the tank. That phase-separated layer is corrosive to carburettor components and fuel lines, and it does not burn correctly.

The options:
- Use marine-grade ethanol-free fuel for the last fill of the season if it is available in your area
- If using regular pump fuel, add a quality marine fuel stabiliser and run the engine long enough to circulate stabilised fuel into the carburettor before storage
- For carburetted engines: running the fuel system dry (running the engine until it stops from fuel starvation) eliminates the storage fuel problem entirely, though it leaves the carburettor components unprotected from dry corrosion unless fogging oil was used beforehand

Fuel stabiliser is not a cure for ethanol-blended fuel over seven months. It slows degradation; it does not stop it. Combined with ethanol-free fuel for the final fill, it is effective. Used alone with ethanol-blended fuel over a full Northern Ontario winter, results vary.

## How the Canvas Is Stored Matters

Canvas storage is the cosmetic step. It is also the one that new boat owners get wrong most often because no one explains what happens if it is done incorrectly.

Boat canvas stored with water trapped in folds or depressions will develop mould over a Northern Ontario winter. Canvas that pools water in winter conditions will have ice form in the pooled areas, which stresses seams and snap attachments over multiple freeze-thaw cycles. Canvas left on the boat without support may sag and hold water in ways that the original design did not intend.

The correct approach: clean the canvas before storage, ensure it is dry, and if it goes over the boat, support it so water does not pool. Many Northern Ontario boat owners store canvas off the boat entirely and cover with a tightly fitted tarped frame that sheds water without allowing pooling.
