A snowblower that worked without issue last winter is not necessarily ready for this one. The Northern Ontario off-season runs from April to October: six months of sitting, fuel degradation, belt aging, and rubber deterioration. The machines that fail in November are almost always ones that went into storage without service and came back out without preparation.

Here is what snowblower service covers in Sault Ste. Marie, when to book, and what to expect.

---

## Spring Service: What Happens After the Season

Spring is the right time to service a snowblower, not fall. The reasoning is straightforward: you know exactly what the machine did during the season, any damage or wear is visible immediately, and you have the entire off-season to have work completed without any scheduling pressure.

**What spring service covers:**

**Fuel system drain or stabilise.** Fuel left in the carburettor over the summer months degrades and varnishes. Spring service either drains the carburettor and fuel tank or treats the remaining fuel with stabiliser before storage. Both approaches prevent the varnish buildup that causes hard starting and rough running when the machine is needed in November.

**Belt inspection.** Drive and auger belts stretch and crack over time and with cold-weather cycling. Spring service is when belts are inspected for cracking, glazing, and correct tension. A belt that shows cracking or surface hardening is replaced at service rather than at the start of next season when failure is more inconvenient.

**Auger shear bolt check.** Two-stage machines use shear bolts designed to break when the auger strikes a hard object, protecting the drivetrain. Spring service confirms shear bolts are present and in good condition. Missing or damaged shear bolts are a safety concern, as the drivetrain is no longer protected against sudden auger stoppage.

**Skid shoe and scraper blade inspection.** The skid shoes and scraper blade on the bottom of the housing take direct contact with the pavement. After a full season of use on Northern Ontario driveways, these wear items are checked and replaced if below service limit.

**Lubrication.** Auger gear box, impeller bearings, and chute pivot points are lubricated per the manufacturer's schedule. Components that run dry over the summer show accelerated wear in the first heavy use of the following season.

**Spark plug replacement.** Two-stroke and four-stroke small engines in snowblowers should have plugs replaced or inspected annually. A worn or fouled plug on a cold November morning when the machine needs to start reliably is not a position to be in.

## Fall Pre-Season Check: Getting Ready for November

Fall service at the start of the snowblower season (September or October, before first snowfall) covers a narrower set of items than full spring service.

**Fuel check.** Confirm the machine was stored with stabilised fuel or a dry fuel system. If it was not, a carburettor clean or fuel system flush is required before the machine runs.

**Test start.** Run the machine before the first snow falls, ideally on a test surface. A machine that starts cleanly and runs at operating temperature confirms the fuel system is clear and the engine is healthy.

**Belt tension check.** Belt tension that was correct in spring may have changed over storage. Checking and adjusting if needed takes minutes and avoids a belt slip or break mid-clearing.

**Chute and deflector function.** Verify chute rotation and deflector operation are smooth. A chute that seized over the summer due to lack of lubrication is a nuisance in use; a minute of attention in fall prevents it.

**Skid shoe height.** Set skid shoe height for your driveway surface before the season starts, not after the first storm.

## When Booking Matters

In Sault Ste. Marie, snowblower service demand concentrates at two points in the year: the week before the first significant fall snowfall and immediately after the season ends in spring. Both of these windows can be busy.

Booking spring service in April or May means work is done during the slow period between snow and marine seasons. Turnaround during this window is typically faster.

Booking fall service in September, before the October rush, similarly improves turnaround. A machine dropped off in October with a carburettor problem during the first week of snow has a longer queue than the same machine dropped off in September for scheduled maintenance.

## Common Repairs and What Causes Them

Beyond routine maintenance, several repairs come up consistently in snowblower service:

**Belt replacement.** Drive belts and auger belts are the most common snowblower repair. Belts that were not replaced at the end of last season often fail mid-season under load. Keeping a spare belt set on hand is reasonable for machines used heavily in Northern Ontario winters. Reyco stocks belts for common Cub Cadet and Toro models.

**Carburettor service.** As described above, unstabilised fuel over summer is the primary cause. A carburettor cleaning takes less time than a full rebuild; a badly varnished carburettor that sat for two or more seasons without stabiliser may need a rebuild.

**Shear bolt replacement.** Shear bolts are consumable items that fail when the auger hits a solid object. Replacing them is a few minutes of work. If shear bolts are failing repeatedly on the same clearing run, the auger housing is likely contacting a pavement edge or a buried obstacle that needs to be identified.

**Wheel drive failure.** On machines with friction-disc wheel drive, the friction pad wears down over several seasons. A machine that drives poorly in reverse, or drives at reduced speed, typically has worn friction disc components. This is a straightforward repair at service.
