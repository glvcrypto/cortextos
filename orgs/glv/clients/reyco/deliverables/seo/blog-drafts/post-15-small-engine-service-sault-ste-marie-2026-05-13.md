A lawn mower, riding tractor, string trimmer, or leaf blower that was running at the end of last season is not guaranteed to start this spring. Small engines that sit unused through a Northern Ontario winter experience fuel degradation, carburettor varnishing, and component deterioration that does not show up until you pull the cord on the first warm day.

This is what small engine service covers for lawn equipment in Sault Ste. Marie, and when to bring equipment in rather than trying to troubleshoot it at home.

## The Spring Start-Up Problem

The most common small engine service call in spring is an engine that will not start, runs rough, or starts and then stalls under load. In the majority of these cases, the root cause is the same: fuel left in the carburettor or fuel system over winter without stabiliser.

Modern pump gasoline contains up to 10 percent ethanol. Ethanol-blend fuel absorbs moisture from the air and phase-separates over a storage period of 30 to 60 days. The ethanol and water settle to the bottom of the tank and carburettor, leaving a gum and varnish residue in the carburettor passages when the volatile components evaporate.

This varnish restricts carburettor fuel passages without fully blocking them, which produces symptoms like hard starting, rough running at low RPM, and stalling under load. The engine has fuel, but the flow is restricted enough that it cannot maintain consistent operation.

The fix is a carburettor cleaning or rebuild, depending on how severe the varnishing is and whether any of the jets or internal passages are corroded.

## What Small Engine Service Covers for Lawn Equipment

A standard tune-up and service for lawn equipment at Reyco Marine covers:

**Spark plug inspection and replacement.** A fouled or worn spark plug is one of the most common causes of hard starting in both two-stroke and four-stroke small engines. Spark plugs are inexpensive and replacing a worn plug often restores starting and smooth running without further diagnosis.

**Air filter check.** A clogged air filter restricts airflow and causes rich running. On small engines, paper air filters should be inspected and replaced annually. Foam pre-filters are cleaned separately.

**Fuel system inspection.** Fuel lines are checked for brittleness and cracking. The primer bulb on two-stroke engines is inspected for hardness and cracking. The fuel filter (where present) is checked and replaced. Carburettor condition is assessed based on the engine's symptoms.

**Drive system check.** On self-propelled mowers and tractors, drive cables and belts are inspected for condition, tension, and wear. A self-propelled mower that drives sluggishly or not at all typically has a worn drive cable or a stretched belt. These are wear items that are straightforward to address at service.

**Blade condition (push and riding mowers).** Blades are inspected for impact damage and sharpness. A dull or bent blade produces an uneven cut and puts stress on the deck spindle bearing. Sharpening or replacing blades at the start of the season is a cost-effective part of annual service.

**Oil change (four-stroke engines).** Walk-behind and riding lawn mower four-stroke engines have engine oil that should be changed annually. Old oil in a small four-stroke engine does not cause immediate problems but contributes to bearing and piston wear over time.

## When to Bring It In vs Handle It at Home

**Bring it in:**

- Engine will not start after checking fuel and spark at home
- Engine starts but stalls under load or when throttle is increased
- Self-propel or drive system is not working
- Deck vibration or unusual noise from the blade area
- Oil leak from the engine or gear case
- Any electrical issue on a riding mower (starting, charging, gauges)

**Serviceable at home:**

- Replacing the air filter
- Replacing the spark plug (straightforward on most walk-behind engines)
- Refilling engine oil
- Sharpening blades with the right tools and safety procedures
- Replacing a worn pull-cord on simple two-stroke engines

The carburettor is where most home-service attempts stall. Carburettor cleaning requires removing the unit, disassembling the internal components, cleaning with carb cleaner, and correctly reassembling including small rubber components that are easily damaged. For owners without mechanical experience, a failed carburettor clean attempt often results in a no-start engine that is more difficult to diagnose than the original problem.
