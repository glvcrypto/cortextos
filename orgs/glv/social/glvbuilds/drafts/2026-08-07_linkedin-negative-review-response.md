## LinkedIn Post

A negative Google review isn't a crisis.

Responding badly to it is.

Here's the 3-sentence formula I give every local client:

**Sentence 1: Acknowledge — without admitting fault.**
"Thank you for taking the time to leave a review. I'm sorry to hear your visit didn't meet your expectations."
You're not agreeing with the complaint. You're acknowledging that the experience fell short of what they hoped for.

**Sentence 2: Take it offline.**
"I'd like to learn more about what happened — please reach out to us directly at [phone] or [email] so we can make this right."
A specific contact point. Not "contact us through our website." A phone number or email address. This signals you're serious about resolving it, not just performing concern for the audience.

**Sentence 3: Invite resolution — briefly.**
"We stand behind our work and want every customer to leave satisfied."
One sentence. No over-explaining. No excuses. No counterattack.

**The three things that make negative review responses worse:**

1. **Arguing publicly.** Even when the review is factually wrong. Every word you type is now visible to every future potential customer reading that profile. The optics of the argument matter more than winning it.

2. **Offering refunds or compensation in the comments.** This creates a public record of your resolution policy and invites bad-faith complaints from people who read it later.

3. **Generic copy-paste responses.** "We strive to provide excellent service and are sorry to hear this" — no customer reference, no offer to follow up. Readers can tell. It's worse than no response.

The goal of a response to a negative review isn't to win back the reviewer. It's to show every other potential customer reading the profile that you're professional, accountable, and reachable.

That's the audience that matters.
