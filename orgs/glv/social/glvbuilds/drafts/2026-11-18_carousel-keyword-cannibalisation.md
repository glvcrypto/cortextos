## Slide 1 — Hook

**"A client had three pages all targeting '[service] in [city].' None of them ranked. This is what keyword cannibalisation looks like — and how I fixed it."**

When multiple pages compete for the same query, Google gets confused and often ranks none of them well.

---

## Slide 2 — What cannibalisation actually is

**Multiple pages targeting the same keyword = Google doesn't know which one to show.**

Google can only rank one page per domain for a given query. When two or more pages compete, Google either:
- Rotates between them unpredictably
- Ranks a weaker page over the stronger one
- Drops both in favour of a competitor's single focused page

The symptom: impressions spread across multiple URLs for the same keyword, with none getting consistent top-5 placement.

---

## Slide 3 — How to find it in GSC

**GSC → Performance → Pages tab → search for your target keyword.**

Filter the query you're worried about. If two or more URLs show significant impressions for the same query, you have cannibalisation.

Cross-check: open GSC → Performance → Queries → filter for the keyword → click through to Pages tab below the chart. You'll see every URL Google associated with that query.

---

## Slide 4 — Step 1: Pick the winner

**One page gets to rank. Choose deliberately — don't let Google choose randomly.**

The winner is usually the page with:
- Highest impressions over the last 90 days
- Best average position
- Most internal links pointing to it
- Strongest external links (if any)

That page becomes the canonical destination. Everything else gets fixed against it.

---

## Slide 5 — Step 2: Fix the losers

**Two options for losing pages — 301 or differentiate.**

**301 redirect** (if the page has no unique value): forward the URL to the winner permanently. Best when the loser is a near-duplicate with no distinct audience.

**Differentiate** (if the page serves a distinct intent): rewrite it to target a different, adjacent query. "[service] near me" vs "[service] for [specific situation]." Give each page its own query to win.

Don't delete pages that have external links — 301 preserves that link equity.

---

## Slide 6 — Step 3: Tighten internal links

**Internal links are votes. Make them all point to the winner.**

After picking the winner, audit every internal link across your site. Any anchor text or link that used to point to a losing page should now point to the winner.

This reinforces to Google which page you've decided is authoritative for that query. It also consolidates any link equity the losers had built through internal linking.

Check your navigation, footer, related posts/services blocks, and any in-body links.

---

## Slide 7 — After the fix

**Request indexing. Wait 21 days. Check GSC.**

After fixing:
1. GSC → URL Inspection → paste winner URL → Request Indexing
2. Also request indexing on any redirected losers (so Google processes the 301 quickly)
3. Wait 21 days before judging results — Google's re-ranking takes time
4. Check GSC Pages tab: winner should start consolidating impressions

## LinkedIn Caption

When I pulled the GSC data for a new client, I found three service pages — all targeting the same "[service] in [city]" keyword. Each had impressions. None had consistent rankings.

That's keyword cannibalisation. And it's one of the most common silent ranking killers I find on local service sites that have been adding content without a strategy.

Here's what it looks like in GSC: open Performance → Pages tab, filter for your target keyword. If two or more URLs show significant impressions for the same query, they're competing with each other instead of competing with your actual competitors.

The fix has three steps.

**Step 1: Pick the winner.** Look at impressions over 90 days, average position, and how many internal links each page has. One page becomes the canonical destination. The others get dealt with.

**Step 2: Fix the losers.** If a losing page is a near-duplicate with no unique value — 301 redirect to the winner. If it genuinely serves a different search intent, rewrite it to target a distinct adjacent query. Don't delete pages with external links. Redirect them.

**Step 3: Tighten internal links.** Every link across your site that points to a losing URL should now point to the winner. Navigation, footer, in-body links, related services blocks. Internal links are votes — make sure all the votes go to the right page.

After the fix: GSC → URL Inspection → Request Indexing on the winner (and the redirected losers). Wait 21 days. Then check the Pages tab again and confirm impressions are consolidating.

The client who had three cannibalised pages was ranking in position 14, 16, and 22 for the same query. After consolidating to one page and redirecting the others, they were at position 6 within a month.
