## LinkedIn Post

Traffic to a service page means nothing if visitors don't trust you enough to call.

I've audited a lot of local service pages that rank well and convert badly. The ranking problem is solved. The trust problem isn't.

Here are the five elements I add to every service page that's getting clicks but not calls:

**1. Review count near the top — not buried in the footer**
Your star rating should be visible before the visitor scrolls. A widget showing "4.8 ★ (62 reviews)" above the fold converts better than the same information at page bottom. Visitors decide whether to keep reading based on that number.

**2. One named testimonial with a full name and specific service**
"Great service!" — Anonymous does nothing. "Lee had my boat winterised and ready for pickup the same day. Best marina service I've used in 10 years. — Sarah M., Barrie" — that works. Specificity is the signal of authenticity.

**3. A concrete trust anchor in the copy**
Not "we've been around for years" — something specific. A founding year, a local landmark reference ("we've been on Dunlop Street since 1987"), a number of completed jobs. Specificity earns trust. Vagueness signals nothing.

**4. A real team photo above the fold**
Stock photography performs worse than a genuine photo of the people doing the work — even if the phone photo is imperfect. Real faces build faster trust than polished generic imagery. If you have one, use it.

**5. A certification or accreditation logo where it applies**
TSSA, WSIB clearance, Better Business Bureau, manufacturer dealer certification — if you have a credential that customers care about, it belongs near the top of the page, not on a separate About page nobody visits.

None of these are SEO plays. They're conversion plays. But they answer the question a visitor asks the moment they land: "Can I trust these people?"

Get the trust answer right and the phone rings more often from the same traffic you already have.
