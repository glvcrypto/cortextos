## Slide 2 — Step 1: Claim and Complete Your GBP

**Headline:** Step 1: Claim your Google Business Profile and fill in every field.

**Body:**
Free. Available to every local business.
What "complete" means in practice:
→ Primary category: the most specific option available for your business type
→ Service area or storefront address: one, not both unless you genuinely operate both ways
→ Hours: accurate, including special holiday hours
→ Services: every service listed with a short description
→ Business description: 750 characters, lead with service + city
→ 10 photos at minimum: exterior, interior, team, work in progress
This is the foundation. Everything else in local SEO builds on it.

## Slide 3 — Step 2: Fix NAP Consistency on the Top Directories

**Headline:** Step 2: Fix your business name, address, and phone on the top 10 directories.

**Body:**
Free (most directories).
NAP inconsistency across directories is one of the most common local SEO problems and one of the most quietly damaging.
The 10 to check first: Google Business Profile, Yelp, Facebook, Apple Maps, Bing Places, Yellow Pages, Foursquare, BBB, your local Chamber of Commerce, and any industry-specific directory.
Search your business name + city in Google and audit the first three pages of results.
Update stale addresses, phone numbers, or trading names. Request duplicate listing removal where they exist.

## Slide 4 — Step 3: Ask for Reviews Personally

**Headline:** Step 3: Ask for reviews in person, with a direct link.

**Body:**
Free.
The most effective review-generation method for local businesses isn't an automated email sequence. It's a direct, personal ask from the person who served the customer — followed immediately by a one-tap link.
How to get the link: GBP dashboard → Get more reviews → copy the review link.
Save it as a text shortcut on your phone. Send it as a follow-up text immediately after a successful job.
"It was great working with you today — if you've got a minute, a Google review helps us out a lot. [link]"
That's it. No email drip. No third-party tool. No budget.

## Slide 5 — Step 4: One Page Per Service

**Headline:** Step 4: One dedicated page for each service you offer.

**Body:**
Free (if you can edit your website yourself).
A single "Services" page with a bullet list does not rank for individual service queries.
A dedicated page for each service — with its own URL, H1, and first paragraph — is how Google understands what you offer and when to show you.
Minimum viable page: 300 words, H1 includes service name + city, first paragraph answers what the service is and who it's for, contact CTA at the bottom.
If you offer 6 services and have only 1 services page, that's 5 pages you're not ranking for.

## Slide 6 — Step 5: One GBP Post Per Week

**Headline:** Step 5: Post to your Google Business Profile once a week.

**Body:**
Free.
GBP posts appear in your knowledge panel and signal to Google that the profile is actively managed.
Post formula: service + city + one specific differentiator + CTA.
"Small engine tune-ups in Sault Ste. Marie. We're booking spring lawn mower service now — book by [date] for priority scheduling. Call Damian at [number]."
90–150 words. A relevant photo. A booking or call CTA.
Once a week, every Monday. Schedule it so it doesn't slip.

## LinkedIn Caption

New local SEO clients sometimes ask what I'd prioritise if they had no budget at all.

Here's the answer. Five steps. About 3 hours of work. All of it free.

**1. Claim and complete your Google Business Profile.**
Every field. Primary category as specific as possible. All services listed with descriptions. 10 photos minimum. Accurate hours including holidays. Business description with service + city in the first sentence.
This is the foundation. Nothing else works well without it.

**2. Fix your business name, address, and phone across the top 10 directories.**
NAP inconsistency — different phone numbers, old addresses, slightly different trading names — is one of the most common and quietly damaging local SEO problems. Search your business name and audit the first few pages of Google results. Fix what's wrong. Request removal of duplicates.
Free. Takes about 45 minutes.

**3. Ask for reviews in person, with a direct link.**
Get your Google review link from the GBP dashboard. Save it as a text shortcut. Send it immediately after a successful job.
"It was great working with you — if you have a minute, a Google review helps us out a lot. [link]"
No automated email sequence. No third-party tool. No budget. Personal + immediate + frictionless = more reviews.

**4. Build one dedicated page per service.**
A single "Services" page with a bullet list doesn't rank for individual service queries. Each service needs its own URL, its own H1, and its own 300-word-minimum page.
If you offer 6 services and have one services page, you're not ranking for 5 of them.

**5. Post to your GBP once a week.**
Formula: service + city + one specific differentiator + CTA. 90–150 words. A relevant photo. Every Monday.
Weekly posts keep the profile active and give Google a consistent signal that the business is operating.

This is the baseline. When it's solid, more advanced tactics build on a base that actually works.
