## Slide 2 — GBP Category: You Have More Options Than "Nail Salon"

"Nail Salon" is the primary GBP category for most nail businesses. But depending on your services, secondary categories matter.

A nail spa that offers spa pedicures, paraffin treatments, and gel overlays is a different Map result than a nail bar doing express manicures. A studio with a certified nail artist specialising in nail art is different again.

Secondary categories to review: "Nail Care," "Beauty Salon," "Manicurist." Add the ones that match your actual service mix. The category affects which searches your listing appears in and which attributes Google displays on your profile.

Check your primary category first. Many inherited listings say "Beauty Salon" when "Nail Salon" is more accurate — and that one change affects which filters your listing shows up under in Google Maps.

---

## Slide 3 — Service Pages: One Page Per Service Type

A single "Services" page listing gel, acrylic, pedicure, nail art, and removal as bullet points ranks for none of them specifically.

The clients searching for "gel manicure [city]," "acrylic nails [city]," and "pedicure [city]" are at different stages with different expectations. A dedicated page for each answers their specific questions: what the service involves, how long it takes, how much it costs (or "call for current pricing"), and how to book.

Minimum page set for a full-service nail salon:
- Gel manicure (gel overlay, gel extensions)
- Acrylic nails (full set, fills, removal)
- Pedicure (classic, spa, gel)
- Nail art (custom, French, seasonal — one page that can be updated)
- Nail removal and infill

Each page: service description, duration, booking link, photos of your actual work.

---

## Slide 4 — Technician Bios Build Trust Before the Booking

A client searching for a nail salon is choosing who is putting their hands on their nails. Technician bios — a photo, the services they specialise in, years of experience, products they work with — reduce the uncertainty that stops new clients from booking.

What to include:
- Name and photo (real, not a stock headshot)
- Training background (where they studied, courses completed)
- Specialisations: gel, acrylics, nail art, specific techniques
- Products they use (OPI, CND Shellac, Gelish, Bio Sculpture Gel — clients search for these by name)
- Languages spoken (significant for markets with multilingual clientele)

No formal licensing body exists for nail technicians in most Canadian provinces. Do not invent credentials that do not exist. Training completion and product expertise are legitimate and searchable.

---

## Slide 5 — Product Signals: Clients Search by Brand

"OPI salon [city]." "CND Shellac [city]." "Gelish [city]."

Clients who know the products they want search with the brand name. A salon that uses CND Shellac and does not mention it anywhere on the site is invisible to those searches.

List the products you use on your services pages and GBP profile. Include them in your GBP description. Add them to your services tab.

This applies to nail lamps (UV vs LED), topcoats, base coats, and any speciality brands your technicians are trained in. These are searchable signals that competitors who only list "gel manicure" are not capturing.

---

## Slide 6 — Photos: Real Work, Regularly Updated

GBP photo categories for nail salons that matter:
- At Work: technician working on a client's nails (the clearest trust signal)
- Your Work / Portfolio: finished nails — close, well-lit, real client results
- Interior: the space itself (cleanliness signals matter in this category)

Do not use manufacturer stock photos of products. Do not use generic nail art stock imagery. Clients know the difference, and Google knows which photos get engagement.

A fresh set of real work photos uploaded monthly keeps the listing active and gives Google new content to surface in photo searches.
