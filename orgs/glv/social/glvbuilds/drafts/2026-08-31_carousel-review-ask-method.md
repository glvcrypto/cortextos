# Getting More Google Reviews — The Order That Actually Works

## Slide 1 — Hook

Most businesses ask for reviews in a follow-up email.

That's the third-best option.

Here's the order I use — and why timing is the whole game.

---

## Slide 2 — Why Timing Matters

Review intent drops off fast.

Someone who just had a great experience will review you.
Someone who had a great experience three days ago — and then got a generic email — might not.

The goal: ask before the moment passes.

---

## Slide 3 — Option 1: In-Person Ask (Best)

Ask face-to-face at checkout or job completion.

Script: "If you're happy with the work, a Google review would mean a lot to us."

Then: hand them a QR code card or pull up the review link on your phone.

Highest conversion of any method — because the positive emotion is still live and you've made it effortless.

---

## Slide 4 — Option 2: QR Code Card

Print your Google Review QR code on a small card.
Leave it at checkout, with the invoice, or on the job site.

No email required. No app required.
Customers scan and review before they leave.

Works especially well for retail, service counters, and any business with a physical touchpoint.

---

## Slide 5 — Option 3: Same-Day Text

Text the direct review link within 1 hour of service completion.

One message. One link. No marketing copy.
Example: "Hi [Name] — here's a link to leave us a Google review if you're happy with today's work: [link]"

Higher conversion than email because texts get read.
Use only if you have permission to text the customer.

---

## Slide 6 — Option 4: Follow-Up Email

Lowest conversion of the four — but still worth having as the fallback.

Rules:
— Send within 24 hours of service
— One link only — no attachments, no design, no long copy
— Subject line: "Would you mind leaving us a review?"
— Don't ask twice (one follow-up maximum)

---

## Slide 7 — The Setup Checklist

Before you can ask for reviews, you need:

1. Your Google Review shortlink set up (search "Google review link generator" — it's free)

## LinkedIn Caption (300–600 words)

The number one question I get from local SEO clients after we've optimised their GBP: "How do we actually get more reviews?"

Most of them are already sending follow-up emails. The email is not the problem — it's the timing, and it's the fact that they're relying on the lowest-conversion method.

Here's how I explain the review ask hierarchy.

**In-person, at the moment of service: highest conversion by far**

When someone has just had a good experience — they paid, they're happy, they're standing right in front of you — that's the moment to ask. A single sentence: "If you're happy with the work, a Google review would mean a lot to us." Then make it effortless: hand them a QR code card or pull up the link on your phone.

This works because the positive emotion is still active and the friction is near zero.

**QR code card: lowest friction, no human required**

Print the Google Review QR code on a small card. Leave it at checkout, with invoices, in service vehicles, at reception.

The customer scans it in 10 seconds. No app, no searching, no finding the right Google listing. The friction is gone.

This is the most underused method. Most businesses either don't have a QR code or never put it in front of customers.

**Same-day text: beats email every time**

A text sent within an hour of service completion, with just the link and one sentence, will outperform a well-designed email sent 48 hours later.

Texts get read. The experience is still fresh. And there's no inbox competition.

**Email: still worth it, just not first**

I still set up a follow-up email template for every client — because not every customer will be reached in person or by text. But I build it as the fallback, not the primary strategy.

One link. Plain text. Sent within 24 hours. One follow-up maximum. That's it.

Before any of this can work: set up your Google Review shortlink (free, takes 5 minutes), print QR code cards, and make sure your team knows the one-sentence in-person ask.
