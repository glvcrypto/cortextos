## Slide 2 — GBP Category: Orthodontist ≠ Dentist

"Orthodontist" and "Dentist" are separate Google Business Profile categories with different filter behaviour in Maps.

A patient filtering for orthodontists near them will not see a listing categorised as "Dentist" in that filtered view — even if the practice offers full orthodontic services. Most orthodontic practices I audit have their GBP set to "Dental Clinic" or "Dentist" by default. The fix takes two minutes and changes who your listing appears for.

If your practice is an orthodontist-only clinic, "Orthodontist" should be your primary GBP category, with "Dentist" as a secondary only if you offer both.

## Slide 3 — Treatment Pages

"Invisalign [city]." "Braces for adults [city]." "Clear braces [city]." "Teen orthodontics [city]." "Retainer replacement [city]."

Each of these is a separate search made by a patient with a specific treatment in mind. One "treatments" page listing all options ranks for none of them specifically.

Treatment-specific pages — covering what the treatment involves, approximate timeline, cost range or "call for pricing," candidacy (who is a good fit), and what the first appointment looks like — answer the questions that move a patient from search to consultation booking.

---

## Slide 4 — Referring Dentist Page

A significant share of orthodontic patients come via GP dentist referral. A page for referring dental professionals — outlining the referral process, what information to include, expected timeline for a new patient consultation, and a direct referral contact — signals that your practice actively supports GP referrals.

Most orthodontic websites have no page for this. A referring dentist searching "orthodontist accepting referrals [city]" or looking for a practice to recommend to patients finds nothing. A dedicated page captures that search and builds a referral pipeline that doesn't depend on existing relationships.

---

## Slide 5 — Adult Orthodontics Specifically

"Adult orthodontics" and "braces for adults [city]" are among the highest-growth orthodontic search segments in Canada. Adults considering orthodontic treatment have specific concerns that teen-focused content doesn't address: treatment duration relative to work and social obligations, whether clear aligners are suitable for their case, cost and payment options for an out-of-pocket expense, and what wearing braces looks like professionally.

A dedicated adult orthodontics page that addresses these concerns — and explicitly states that adults make up a significant portion of current patients — converts better than a general treatments page that leads with teen or child imagery.

---

## Slide 6 — Patient Lifecycle Content

The orthodontic patient relationship is long — often 18–30 months. The search journey spans multiple stages: initial research, consultation, treatment start, mid-treatment questions, and retention.

Content that maps to mid-treatment and retention questions — "how to clean Invisalign aligners," "retainer after braces — what to expect," "orthodontic emergency [city]" — creates touchpoints that keep the practice visible during treatment and generates the kind of specific, recent reviews that drive new patient inquiries.
