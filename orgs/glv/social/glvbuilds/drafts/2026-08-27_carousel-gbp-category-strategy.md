# GBP Category Strategy — The Signal Most Local Businesses Set Once and Forget

## Slide 1 — Hook

Most local businesses pick a Google Business Profile primary category
and never touch it again.

Categories are one of Google's strongest local pack signals.
Here's how I set them up for every new client.

---

## Slide 2 — Why Categories Matter

Your primary GBP category tells Google what searches to show you for.

Wrong category = showing up for the wrong searches.
Or not showing up at all.

It's not a label for customers. It's a signal for Google.

---

## Slide 3 — Primary Category: Be Specific

Your primary category should match your single most important service.

"Plumber" outranks "Contractor" for plumbing searches.
"Marine Service Station" outranks "Auto Repair Shop" for boat service searches.
"Physiotherapy Clinic" outranks "Health and Wellness Centre" for physio searches.

The more specific your primary category, the less competition you face for it.

---

## Slide 4 — Secondary Categories: Add Them, But Don't Dilute

Secondary categories cover additional real services you offer.

Rules:
— Only add categories for services you actually provide
— Keep it to 3–5 secondaries maximum
— More is not better — too many secondaries dilute the primary signal

Example: Primary = "HVAC Contractor". Secondaries = "Furnace Repair Service", "Air Conditioning Contractor", "Heating Contractor".

---

## Slide 5 — The Common Mistake: Choosing Broad Over Specific

Most businesses default to the broadest category that applies.

If you specialise, go specific:
— Broad: "Restaurant" → Specific: "Italian Restaurant"
— Broad: "Auto Repair Shop" → Specific: "Transmission Shop" (if transmissions are your main service)
— Broad: "Contractor" → Specific: "Roofing Contractor"

Specific categories have less competition in the local pack.

---

## Slide 6 — How to Research Competitor Categories

Open Google Maps.
Search for your main service + city.
Click a top competitor's profile.
Scroll to "More about this place" → categories are listed there.

Do this for your top 3 competitors.
Note which primary category they use.
Look for gaps — categories they're using that you're missing, or categories they've missed.

---

## Slide 7 — The Checklist

1. Is my primary category the most specific match for my main service?
2. Do my secondary categories reflect real services I offer?
3. Have I checked what categories my top 3 competitors use?
4. Am I using fewer than 5–6 secondary categories total?
5. Have I reviewed categories in the last 12 months? (Google adds new ones regularly.)

## LinkedIn Caption (300–600 words)

The GBP primary category is one of the strongest local pack signals Google uses — and most local businesses pick one during setup and never look at it again.

I audit GBP categories on every new client. It's one of the first things I check. And it's one of the most common places I find easy wins.

Here's how I think about it.

**Primary category: be specific**

Your primary category tells Google what searches to put you in front of. The more specific, the less competition you face for it.

I've seen businesses ranked "Contractor" competing with every trades business in the city when they were really a plumbing-only shop. Switch to "Plumber" and the local pack gets dramatically more targeted — and easier to rank in.

The rule I follow: the primary category should match your single most important service. Not your broadest description of what you do.

**Secondary categories: add real ones, not aspirational ones**

Secondary categories let you show up for additional relevant searches. They work — but only if they reflect real services you offer.

Adding categories for things you don't actually do (hoping to capture searches) is a waste of a signal slot. Google learns quickly from user behaviour. If people click your profile looking for a service and don't find it, that's a negative engagement signal.

I keep secondaries to 3–5 maximum. More than that and you risk diluting the primary category signal.

**The competitor research step**

Before I finalise categories for any client, I look at the top 3 competitors in the local pack.

Open Google Maps → search for their main service + city → click their profile → scroll to "More about this place." Their categories are listed there.

This tells you: what categories the ranking competitors are using, whether there's a more specific category they're missing, and whether there's a secondary category you haven't considered.

Five minutes of research. Often surfaces a quick category win.

**One last thing:** Google regularly adds new GBP categories. A category that didn't exist a year ago might be available now — and might be a better fit than what you're currently using. I review client categories every 12 months for this reason.
