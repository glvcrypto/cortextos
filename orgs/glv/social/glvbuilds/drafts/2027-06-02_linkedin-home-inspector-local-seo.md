## LinkedIn Post

I run local SEO audits for home inspectors regularly. The challenge with this niche is a timing problem: the buyer who needs an inspector doesn't know they need one until the moment they make an offer. At that point, they have 5–7 days, their realtor is suggesting three names, and they're Googling the first one to verify it before calling.

Here's what an audit finds almost every time:

**GBP category**
"Home Inspector" is the correct primary category. Not "Building Inspector," not "Property Management Company." The category signals the search match before the client even clicks your profile.

**One page per inspection type**
A single "our services" page covering everything ranks for nothing specifically. Pages I look for:

- **Pre-purchase home inspection** — the most common search; walk through the scope of assessment, timeline (typically 2–4 hours for a detached home), what's included and what falls outside a standard inspection (e.g., WETT certification is a separate service, not bundled in most provinces)
- **Pre-listing inspection** — sellers use this to identify issues before listing; different client, different motivation, different objection set
- **New construction inspection** — timing-sensitive (different phases: pre-drywall, final walkthrough), Ontario New Home Warranty Act / BC NHP context
- **Maintenance / annual inspection** — existing homeowners; not tied to a transaction, so lower urgency — the page needs to justify why you'd do this outside a sale context
- **Radon testing** — Health Canada recommends testing in all homes; "radon testing [city]" is a real search; whether you offer it in-house or as an add-on, it warrants its own page
- **Thermal imaging / infrared inspection** — a service that distinguishes inspectors who offer it; moisture detection, insulation gaps, electrical hot spots

**Certification and designation trust signals**
OAHI designation (Ontario), CAHPI membership, InterNACHI membership — these are verifiable and meaningful to a buyer who is handing over access to what is usually their largest purchase. List the designation, link to the public register where possible, and explain what it requires. Parents who would research a babysitter will research a home inspector.

**The realtor referral page**
A significant portion of home inspector clients come through realtor referrals. A page specifically for realtors — how you handle short turnaround requests, your report format, your availability window — is a B2B page that the average inspector website doesn't have. Realtors who refer frequently want to know your systems before they recommend you.

**Seasonal timing**
Spring real estate markets (April–June) and fall markets (September–October) are the two peaks for home inspection demand. GBP posts and content updates should be in place at least six weeks before peak — a site that posts its "spring home inspection checklist" in April has missed most of the search window.

**One page per inspection type.** Pre-purchase, pre-listing, new construction, maintenance, radon, and thermal imaging are six separate services with six different client moments. A single services page covers the inspection scope generally but wins none of the searches specifically.

**Certification displayed prominently.** OAHI, CAHPI, InterNACHI — buyers who are about to make the largest purchase of their life will verify credentials. The designation should be on the homepage, the About page, and each service page — linked to a verifiable source where possible.

**A radon testing page.** "Radon testing [city]" is a real search. Health Canada recommends testing in all homes. Whether you offer it in-house or as an add-on, a standalone page answers the question and captures the traffic.

**A page for realtors.** A meaningful share of inspector work comes through realtor referrals. A page that explains your turnaround time, report format, and scheduling process is a B2B page that most inspector sites don't have — and it earns the referral before the call.
