## Slide 2 — The two structures that work

**For a local service page, you have two URL patterns that work:**

`/service/city/` — e.g. /furnace-repair/toronto/

`/city/service/` — e.g. /toronto/furnace-repair/

Either works. What doesn't work:

`/our-services/?location=toronto`

`/page?id=482`

`/services/1234/furnace-repair-toronto-on-canada`

The first two patterns are clean, crawlable, and readable. The last three are not.

---

## Slide 3 — Subfolder vs subdomain

**The most common mistake I see on multi-location or multi-service sites:**

Using a subdomain instead of a subfolder.

`toronto.yourdomain.com` — Google treats this as a separate site

`yourdomain.com/toronto/` — Google treats this as part of your main site

Subdomains split your domain authority. For almost every local service business, subfolder is the right call.

---

## Slide 4 — Consistency matters as much as structure

**Two things that create duplicate content problems:**

1. Trailing slash inconsistency — `yourdomain.com/service/` and `yourdomain.com/service` are technically two different URLs. Pick one. Use a 301 redirect to enforce it.

2. www vs. non-www — same issue. `www.yourdomain.com` and `yourdomain.com` must be canonicalised to one version.

Check your canonical tags. Check your XML sitemap. They should all point to the same version.

---

## Slide 5 — Keep URLs clean

**One service, one city, one page. The URL should reflect that.**

Good: `/window-cleaning/north-york/`

Not good: `/window-cleaning-north-york-toronto-ontario-canada-residential-commercial/`

Keyword-stuffed URLs don't rank better. They look spammy, they're hard to share, and they're a pain to manage when service areas change.

Aim for 3–5 words in the URL slug. Descriptive, not exhaustive.

---

## Slide 6 — The redirect cost

**Every URL change is a ranking risk.**

A 301 redirect passes most link equity — but not all. A chain of 301 redirects (A → B → C) passes even less.

Before you restructure URLs on a live site, audit how many inbound links, internal links, and GBP citations point to the old URLs. The longer the site has been live, the higher the cost of changing them.

Rule: clean up genuinely broken patterns (parameter URLs, session IDs, CMS junk). Don't change clean URLs just to match a new preference.

---

## Slide 7 — Quick audit checklist

**Five things to check in the next 20 minutes:**

1. Are your service+city pages using clean, readable URL patterns?
2. Is your site on subfolder structure, not subdomain?
3. Are trailing slashes consistent? (Check with Screaming Frog or a redirect checker)
4. Are www and non-www versions canonicalised to one?
5. Are there any parameter-based or session ID URLs being indexed in GSC?

Fix the worst offenders. Leave clean URLs alone.

## LinkedIn Caption

When I start a local SEO audit, the URL structure tells me a lot about whether the site was built by someone who thought about search — or someone who just built a nice-looking website.

Most clients don't think about URL structure until something breaks. Here's what I look for.

**The two patterns that work for local service pages.**

`/service/city/` or `/city/service/` — either is fine. What's not fine: parameter URLs (`?location=city`), session ID strings, or WordPress auto-generated slugs that look like `/p=1234`. Those are crawlable in theory but rarely rank well in practice.

**Subfolders, not subdomains.**

If you serve multiple cities or have multiple service categories, the instinct is sometimes to put them on subdomains. `toronto.yourdomain.com`, `hvac.yourdomain.com`. The problem: Google treats subdomains as separate websites. Your Toronto subdomain doesn't inherit authority from your main domain the way a subfolder does. For local service businesses, `/toronto/` beats `toronto.` almost every time.

**Consistency matters more than people realise.**

Trailing slashes, www vs. non-www — these seem minor until they create duplicate content issues. `yourdomain.com/service/` and `yourdomain.com/service` are technically different URLs. If both are indexed and neither is canonicalised, you're diluting signals across two versions of the same page.

Pick one. Use 301 redirects to enforce it. Make sure your canonical tags and XML sitemap point to the same version.

**Don't change clean URLs.**

Every URL change carries a cost. A 301 redirect passes most link equity — but not all. A chain of redirects passes less. Before you restructure anything on a live site, audit how many inbound links, GBP citations, and internal links point to the old URLs. The longer the site has been live, the more disruptive a URL change becomes.

Fix genuinely broken patterns — parameter URLs, session IDs, keyword-stuffed slugs. Leave clean URLs alone, even if you'd choose differently today.
