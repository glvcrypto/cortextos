# GSC Zero-Impressions Pages — Thin Content or Cannibalisation? Here's the 5-Minute Test

## Slide 1 — Hook

If you find a page with zero impressions in Google Search Console,
don't delete it yet.

It's either thin content or keyword cannibalisation.

Here's how I tell the difference in 5 minutes.

---

## Slide 2 — What Zero Impressions Actually Means

Zero impressions = Google is not showing the page for any query.

Two causes:

1. The page isn't being crawled or indexed properly
2. The page IS indexed — but it has nothing worth ranking

Before diagnosing content, confirm the page is indexed.

---

## Slide 3 — Check 1: Is the Page Indexed?

Open Google Search Console → URL Inspection → paste the page URL.

Look for: "URL is on Google" ✓ or "URL is not on Google" ✗

If not indexed: check for noindex tags, blocked by robots.txt, or crawl errors first.

Fix indexation issues before touching content.

---

## Slide 4 — Check 2: Is It Thin Content?

If the page IS indexed but has zero impressions, ask:

- Does this page have fewer than 300 words of original content?
- Does it say anything a competitor page doesn't already cover?
- Does it have a clear, specific target query?

If the answer to any of those is no — it's thin content.

Fix: Expand, rewrite, add an FAQ block. Or consolidate into a stronger page.

---

## Slide 5 — Check 3: Is It Cannibalisation?

Open an incognito browser.
Search for your target keyword.

Does a different page from your site rank instead of this one?

That's cannibalisation — two pages competing for the same query.
Google picks the one it thinks is stronger. The other gets zero impressions.

---

## Slide 6 — The Fix for Cannibalisation

Option A — Merge: Move the best content onto one page. 301-redirect the other.
Option B — Differentiate: Change the target keyword so the pages don't compete.
Option C — Consolidate: If both pages are thin, merge them into one strong page.

Do not leave two thin pages competing for the same query. Neither will rank.

---

## Slide 7 — The Checklist

1. Check indexation first (URL Inspection tool)
2. If indexed + zero impressions → check for thin content
3. If not thin → check for cannibalisation (incognito search)
4. Fix thin content: expand + FAQ + sharper target keyword
5. Fix cannibalisation: merge or differentiate

## LinkedIn Caption (300–600 words)

If a page has zero impressions in Google Search Console, most people assume it's broken or irrelevant and delete it.

That's usually the wrong call.

Before you touch it, you need to know why it has zero impressions. There are two causes — and the fix is different for each.

**Cause 1: Thin content**

The page is indexed. Google has crawled it. But it doesn't say anything worth ranking for. It might be a generic location page ("We serve [City]!") or a short service blurb with no original detail. Google finds nothing useful for searchers and leaves it out of results.

Fix: Expand the page with specific, useful content. Add a FAQ block targeting the questions people actually ask about that service. Give Google something to match to a search query.

**Cause 2: Keyword cannibalisation**

You have two pages targeting the same query. Google has to pick one. The other gets suppressed — zero impressions, even if it's a well-written page.

The test: open an incognito browser, search for your target keyword. If a different page from your site ranks, that's your answer.

Fix: Merge the two pages into one strong page with a 301 redirect from the weaker one. Or differentiate them — change the target keyword for one so they're no longer competing.

The 5-minute diagnostic process I use:

1. Open URL Inspection in GSC — confirm the page is indexed
2. If not indexed — fix crawl issues before touching content
3. If indexed — count the words and ask: does this page say something a competitor doesn't?
4. If thin — expand it
5. If not thin — do the incognito cannibalisation check
6. If cannibalised — merge or differentiate

Zero impressions is almost never a sign that the page should be deleted. It's usually a sign it needs to be fixed or consolidated.
