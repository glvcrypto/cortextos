## Slide 1 — Hook

**"Google's quality raters are trained to ask one question about your service page: could the person whose name is on this page actually do this work?"**

Most local service pages aren't written by the technician. Google knows the difference.

---

## Slide 2 — What E-E-A-T actually is

**E-E-A-T: Experience · Expertise · Authoritativeness · Trustworthiness**

It's not a ranking factor you can install. It's how Google's quality raters evaluate whether a page deserves to rank for a given query.

For local service businesses, it comes down to one thing: does the page show that a real, qualified person with real-world experience produced it?

---

## Slide 3 — Named authors

**No author byline = no E-E-A-T signal**

Add a named author to every service page. Not "the team at [business name]." A real person: Damian, who's been doing small engine repair for more than a decade. Lee, who winterises outboards every fall.

Link the author name to a bio page. One sentence on the bio: what they do, how long they've done it, where they're based.

---

## Slide 4 — The credentials page

**About Us is not enough. You need individual bios.**

Each bio should include:
- Specific trade certifications or manufacturer training (if applicable)
- Years doing this specific type of work
- Service area city/region
- Real photo — not a stock headshot

Link each service page to the relevant technician or specialist's bio. That link is the E-E-A-T signal.

---

## Slide 5 — Review diversity as a trust signal

**Reviews on one platform = one data point. Reviews across platforms = pattern.**

Google's quality raters look for evidence that real customers have had real experiences. Reviews spread across Google, HomeStars, Houzz, Facebook, and the BBB carry more weight than 200 reviews on Google and nothing anywhere else.

You don't need to be everywhere. Pick two platforms your customers actually use.

---

## Slide 6 — Experience signals on the page itself

**"Experience" in E-E-A-T means first-hand experience with the thing you're writing about.**

On a service page, that looks like:
- One sentence written from first-person experience ("I check X before I do Y")
- Before/after photos of real work — no stock imagery
- A specific outcome a customer saw — stated carefully, without fabricated numbers

If the page reads like it could have been written by someone who has never done the job, it probably won't rank for customers who are about to hire someone to do it.

---

## Slide 7 — Three quick wins

**You can add E-E-A-T signals to a service page in under an hour.**

1. Add an author byline with a link to a bio page
2. Add one first-person sentence about doing this work in your city
3. Add one real-work photo with a descriptive alt text (not "IMG_4521")

These don't require a site rebuild. They require knowing who on your team actually does the work.

## LinkedIn Caption

Google trains human quality raters to evaluate whether the person whose name is on a service page could plausibly have written it.

That's E-E-A-T: Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness. It's not a technical setting. It's a question Google is asking about every page it considers ranking.

For a local service business, the signals are simple — but most sites I audit are missing all of them.

**Named authors.** Not "the team." A real person's name on every service page, linked to a bio that says what they do, how long they've done it, and where they're based. One real photo.

**Credentials that are specific.** "Years of experience" is not a credential. A manufacturer training certificate is. A trade licence number is. The name of the apprenticeship programme is. If you have specifics, put them on the bio page. If you don't, stick to what's defensible — "more than X years" is fine; a number you can't back up is not.

**Review diversity.** A Google review is a trust signal. Google reviews plus HomeStars reviews plus a few Facebook recommendations is a pattern. Patterns carry more weight than a single large number on one platform.

**First-person experience on the page.** One sentence written from direct experience of doing the work. "I always check X before I do Y — here's why." Not AI-generated filler that could describe any business in any city.

**Real photos.** Before/afters. Team on the job. The actual van, the actual shop, the actual piece of equipment being serviced. Google's quality raters are checking whether the page looks like it was produced by someone who has done this work.

None of this requires a site rebuild. It requires knowing who on your team actually does the work — and putting their name and face on the page that ranks for it.

Three wins you can do today: add an author byline with a bio link, add one first-person sentence, add one real work photo with a proper file name and alt text.
