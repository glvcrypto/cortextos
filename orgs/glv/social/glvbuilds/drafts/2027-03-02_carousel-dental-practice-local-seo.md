**Slide 3 — Individual practitioner bio pages**

A standalone bio page per practitioner: full name + professional title + areas of focus + provincial dental college registration number + real headshot.

Not a team grid. A dedicated page.

**Slide 4 — Patient-language service pages**
**Most dental site copy describes what procedures are. Patients search for what their problem is.**

GSC Performance → Queries shows what people actually typed before landing on your pages.

Match your H1 and first paragraph to that language — not clinical terminology.

"Cracked tooth repair in [city]" and "tooth pain [city]" are both different pages from "Restorative Dentistry."

**Slide 5 — Advertising guidelines**
**Before publishing before/after photos or specific outcome claims, check your provincial dental advertising regulations.**

In Ontario, the RCDSO publishes advertising guidelines for member dentists.
Guidelines vary by province and content type.

Defensible framing:
— "many patients experience" not "you will get"
— "our approach includes" not "guaranteed results"
— Before/after photos: confirm what's permitted before publishing

This protects the practice and keeps content durable.

Compliant framing: "If you had a good experience at our practice, a Google review goes a long way for our team."

Ask in person at the moment of satisfaction — not via an email that names the patient or references their treatment.

Consistent monthly collection beats a one-time burst.

## LinkedIn Caption

Local SEO advice for dental practices usually reads like advice for every other local service business: fix your GBP, get more reviews, build some service pages.

That's not wrong. But dental has a layer that generic advice misses.

Here's the four-part framework I use when auditing a dental practice:

**1. Category precision**
Google treats "Dentist," "Cosmetic Dentist," "Orthodontist," "Pediatric Dentist," and "Emergency Dental Service" as distinct categories in its index. If your practice offers teeth whitening and Invisalign but your only GBP category is "Dentist," you're invisible for those specific service searches.

Check what the top 3 map pack competitors in your city are using as their primary category. Matching or going more specific is almost always the right move. Secondary categories can stack the others.

**2. Individual practitioner bio pages**
Each dentist is a distinct local search opportunity. A bio page with the dentist's full name, provincial dental college registration number, specific areas of focus, and a real headshot does two things: it satisfies the E-E-A-T signals Google's quality raters look for on health-related queries, and it lets each practitioner rank independently for [Dr. Name] + [city] searches.

This is not a team grid page with three names. It's a standalone page per practitioner.

**3. Patient-language service pages**
Most dental site copy describes what a procedure is. Patients search for what their problem is. GSC Performance → Queries shows the actual search language arriving at your site before you've done anything to optimise it.

Match your H1 and first paragraph to that language. "Cracked tooth repair in [city]," "tooth pain [city]," and "teeth whitening [city]" are different pages with different intent — and they're often not what's written on a generic "Restorative Dentistry" page.

**4. Advertising guidelines**
Before publishing before/after patient photos, specific outcome claims, or comparative language, check your provincial dental advertising standards. In Ontario, the RCDSO publishes specific advertising guidelines for member dentists. Other provinces have equivalent bodies with their own requirements.

Defensible framing: "many patients experience" rather than "you will get." Before/after photos: confirm what's permitted in your province before publishing. Content that skips this step creates risk and often gets revised anyway.

The starting point for any dental practice audit: private browser, search your top service categories in your city, and see what GBP profiles appear. What's their primary category, review count, and Services tab content? That gap tells you where to start.
