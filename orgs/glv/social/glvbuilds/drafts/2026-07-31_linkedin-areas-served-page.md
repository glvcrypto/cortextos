## LinkedIn Post

Most local SEO audits I run turn up the same missing page.

Not a blog post. Not another service page.

An areas-served page.

Here's why it matters and when to build one:

**What it is:**
A page (or set of pages) that tells Google exactly which cities or towns a business serves — with service context, not just a list of place names.

**Why it works:**
Google uses geographic signals to rank a business outside its immediate address location.
A well-structured areas-served page gives Google: city names, service context, and a reason to show the business to searchers in that location.
For service-area businesses without a public storefront address — plumbers, landscapers, mobile techs — this page does a lot of the geographic ranking work that a physical address can't.

**When to build individual city pages vs. one hub page:**

Individual city pages make sense when:
- The business serves 6+ distinct locations
- Each location warrants genuinely different content (different services available, different seasonal context, local landmarks worth referencing)
- The goal is to rank for "[service] + [city]" queries in each market

One consolidated hub page makes sense when:
- The business serves 2–4 nearby towns
- The content differences between towns would be thin
- The business wants one authoritative page to rank for the regional cluster

**The one thing I tell every client:**
Don't create 20 city pages that are identical except for the city name swapped in.
Thin location pages that duplicate content are at best useless and at worst a footprint signal that works against you.
Each page — or each section of the hub — needs something genuinely local: a specific service note for that area, a relevant landmark or travel reference, or a FAQ that maps to what that market actually searches for.

If you serve more than one city and you don't have a page for it, that's usually the fastest geographic ranking fix available.
