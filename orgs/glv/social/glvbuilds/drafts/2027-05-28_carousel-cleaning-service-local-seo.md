## Slide 1 — Hook

A residential cleaning service and a commercial cleaning service are two different businesses.

## Slide 2 — Two GBP Listings If You Do Both

Google has separate categories for "House Cleaning Service" and "Commercial Cleaning Service." These categories surface in different searches, to different searchers, with different intent.

A property manager searching "commercial cleaning [city]" is not looking at the same results as a homeowner searching "house cleaner [city]."

If your business serves both markets, you need two separate GBP profiles — one per category, each with its own address or service area, its own phone number (or at minimum a tracked extension), its own set of reviews and photos. A single listing trying to serve both segments ranks poorly for both.

If you only serve one market, your primary category should match it exactly. "Commercial Cleaning Service" for B2B only. "House Cleaning Service" for residential only. Not "Cleaning Service" as a catch-all.

---

## Slide 3 — Residential: Four Pages, Not One

A homeowner searching for a cleaner has different needs depending on where they are in the process.

Four pages for a residential cleaning business:

- **Recurring cleaning:** weekly, biweekly, monthly — the recurring client is your highest-value acquisition; this page answers the routine-client questions (what's covered, how to schedule, what to leave out)
- **Deep cleaning:** first clean for a new client, or pre-sale; explains the difference from a recurring clean
- **Move-in / move-out cleaning:** distinct search, distinct scope (appliances, inside cabinets, baseboards); high-intent at point of real estate transaction
- **Post-reno / post-construction cleaning:** dust, debris, and construction residue; different scope, different pricing model, different clientele

One "services" page covering all four ranks for none of them specifically.

## Slide 4 — Commercial: Segment by Industry

"Commercial cleaning [city]" is one search. But the decision-makers and their requirements are different depending on the industry.

A dental office has different cleaning standards than a retail store. A restaurant has kitchen-specific requirements. A medical clinic has infection-control considerations. A co-working space has open-plan cleaning challenges that a private office building does not.

Industry-specific pages for commercial cleaners:
- Medical / dental office cleaning
- Restaurant and kitchen cleaning
- Retail and showroom cleaning
- Office building / commercial property cleaning

Each page: what the cleaning scope covers for that industry, frequency options, trust signals relevant to that sector (WHMIS-trained staff, satisfaction guarantee, discretion for client-facing environments).

## Slide 5 — Trust Signals: Residential vs. Commercial Are Different

**Residential trust signals:**
- Bonded and insured (state it explicitly — "bonded and insured, certificate available on request")
- Background-checked staff
- Key holder and alarm code protocols
- Eco/green product availability (if applicable and verifiable — do not fabricate)
- Testimonials from recurring clients

**Commercial trust signals:**
- WHMIS-trained staff
- Safety Data Sheets available for products used
- Proof of commercial liability insurance
- Industry-specific protocols (PIDAC standards for healthcare, HACCP for food-handling adjacent)
- Reference contacts from current commercial clients (by arrangement)

The residential client wants to know their home is safe. The commercial client wants to know their contract is reliable and their compliance obligations are covered. These are not the same page.

---

## Slide 6 — The Booking Page Converts Where the Services Page Does Not

Most cleaning websites end with a contact form. The best ones have a booking page that answers the last four questions standing between a visitor and a confirmed appointment:

1. How is pricing calculated? (per room, per hour, flat rate, custom quote)
2. Do I need to be home?
3. What do I need to provide vs. what do you bring?
4. How do I reschedule or cancel?

A booking page that answers these questions converts the visitor who is ready. A contact form sends them back to Google to check the next result.
