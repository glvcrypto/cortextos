## Outline 1

**Working title:** The Google Business mistake most local shops are making

**Hook line:** If your business doesn't show up here, this is why.

**Topic category:** Local SEO + GBP

**Talking-point beats:**
1. Most local businesses fill in their name and hours and stop there. That's the mistake. Google rewards completeness and treats a sparse profile as a low-trust signal.
2. The services section is the highest-leverage item on the whole profile. Most businesses leave it blank. Google uses it to match you to search queries.
3. The Q&A section is fully owner-controlled and almost no one knows it. You can pre-populate it yourself with the questions your customers actually ask.
4. Two things to add this week: a keyword-matched business description (under 750 characters) and at least 5 fresh photos. Both are free and take under 30 minutes.

**Suggested visual cues:** Phone screen showing a Google Maps search result where one business is fully built out and ranking top-3, others are sparse. Overlay text reinforcing each beat.

**Why this now:** Local search is the first thing most service-area businesses want to fix and the last thing they actually understand. This hits the "free wins" angle that gets saves and shares.

## Outline 2

**Working title:** Why I stopped promising SEO results in 90 days

**Hook line:** Months one through four of SEO, nothing moves. That's normal.

**Topic category:** SEO

**Talking-point beats:**
1. Most agencies promise results in 60 or 90 days because that's what closes deals. What they don't say is that Google doesn't rank new content quickly. Crawling, indexing, and trust-building take time.
2. The compounding curve is real: flat for months 1-4, then rankings start to move, then they accelerate. The businesses that quit at month three are the ones who never see the payoff.
3. What to track in the first 90 days instead of rankings: pages indexed, click-through rate on existing content, and Google Search Console impressions week over week.
4. The 4-month gap isn't a failure signal. It's the cost of building something that lasts instead of something that resets every time you stop paying.

**Suggested visual cues:** Simple graphic showing flat line then steep curve (can build in Canva). Or phone showing GSC impressions climbing over a timeline.

**Why this now:** Every business owner who has been burned by SEO was burned because expectations were set wrong. This reframes the conversation and positions GLV as the honest option.

## Outline 3

**Working title:** What's actually different between $300 and $3000 SEO

**Hook line:** It's not the keywords. It's not the reports. It's this.

**Topic category:** SEO / Marketing

**Talking-point beats:**
1. At $300 a month, you're getting a templated checklist: a few keyword tweaks, a monthly report pulled from a tool, and no ongoing strategy. It's maintenance, not growth.
2. At $3000 a month, you're paying for research cycles, regular content production, competitor monitoring, technical audits, and someone whose job is to understand your specific market.
3. The real gap is custom work versus automated work. One scales across hundreds of clients. The other is built for one business.
4. Neither is wrong. The $300 tier exists for businesses that already have strong organic traffic and just need it maintained. The $3000 tier is for businesses that want to take ground from competitors. Know which one you actually need before you sign anything.

**Suggested visual cues:** Split-screen or text overlay comparing the two tiers line by line. No brand names. Keep it clean.

**Why this now:** Price transparency is a FYP magnet because most agencies refuse to talk about it. This builds trust fast and attracts the right clients.

## Outline 4

**Working title:** Cold email myths that are costing local businesses customers

**Hook line:** The reason your cold emails fail isn't the subject line.

**Topic category:** Lead Gen / Cold Outreach

**Talking-point beats:**
1. Myth one: cold email is spam. It's not, if you're reaching out to relevant businesses with a real offer and following CASL. The problem is most cold email is genuinely useless, not that the channel is broken.
2. Myth two: you need a big list. You don't. 50 well-researched prospects who actually match your offer beat 5,000 scraped emails every time. Volume is a crutch.
3. Myth three: the subject line is everything. The subject line gets the open. The offer determines the reply. Most cold emails have a fine subject line and a useless ask.
4. What actually converts: one sentence about something specific to them, one sentence about what you do, one clear ask. That's the whole email.

**Suggested visual cues:** Phone showing an email inbox. Text overlay with a real before/after comparison (generic vs specific). Keep it fast-paced.

**Why this now:** Every service-based business owner has tried cold outreach and failed. Myth-busting this builds credibility fast and shows you know the real mechanism.

## Outline 5

**Working title:** Your about page is probably about you. Fix that.

**Hook line:** Your about page is written for you. It should be written for them.

**Topic category:** Website Building

**Talking-point beats:**
1. Most about pages are a biography. Year founded, where we're based, our values. None of that answers the question a visitor is actually asking, which is: can you solve my problem?
2. The reframe: your about page should open with the customer's problem, not your history. Tell them what you understand about their situation before you tell them who you are.
3. Three things every effective about page includes: a problem named clearly, a reason to trust you (credentials, years, specifics), and a next step (not just a contact form, a specific CTA).
4. Quick audit: count how many times the words "I", "we", or your company name appear in the first two paragraphs. If it's more than 3, rewrite the opening.

**Suggested visual cues:** Phone scrolling through a generic about page. Overlay text flagging the problems. Cut to a rewritten version. Fast and punchy.

**Why this now:** Website audits are one of the first things GLV does with a new client. This shows the expertise without a pitch and generates leads from business owners who recognise themselves in the reel.
