**Slide 2 — GBP category specificity**
"Lawyer" doesn't rank for what your clients search.

"Family Lawyer" does.
"Criminal Defence Attorney" does.
"Immigration Consultant" does.

Open Maps in a private browser. Search your service + city. Check what category the top 3 results are using. Then match — or get more specific than they are.

**Slide 3 — Named practitioner signals**
For professional services, Google weighs the individual, not just the business.

Every practitioner needs their own bio page:
- Full name + specific specialisation
- Licence or call number (if public)
- Years in practice (actual number, not "decades of experience")
- Real headshot

That bio page becomes the thing Google connects to "[name] + [city]" searches.

**Slide 4 — Review ask process**
You can't hand someone a QR card at the end of a court hearing.

For professional services, the ask goes by email — 2–3 days after a positive outcome.

Keep it short. Reference the matter type in general terms only. No client names, no case specifics.

"If you found the process straightforward, a quick Google review helps other people in a similar situation find us."

One sentence. High response rate.

**Slide 5 — Content: question-driven, not keyword-driven**
Professional service clients search questions, not services.

"Can I dispute a will in Ontario?"
"What happens if I miss a tax deadline?"
"How long does a divorce take in BC?"

One FAQ post per practice area per month — each post answering one PAA question directly. Structure: answer in first sentence, 40–60 words, follow-up paragraph for context.

That's 12 indexed pages a year that rank for high-intent queries before someone has even called a competitor.

**Slide 6 — Multi-practitioner opportunity**
If your firm has three lawyers, each one is a local search opportunity.

Individual bio pages let Google rank practitioners by name. That's "[first name] [last name] [city]" queries for free — brand-name visibility for each person on the team.

Most firms have one team page with three names listed. That's one URL where there could be three.

**Slide 7 — CTA**
Professional services local SEO has a different starting point.

Not GBP category alone.
Not reviews alone.
Named practitioner + specific practice area + question-led content.

## LinkedIn Caption

Most local SEO guides are written for plumbers.

That's not a knock on plumbers. It's an observation that the playbook for a trade business doesn't transfer cleanly to a law firm, a therapy practice, or an accounting office.

I've worked with both. Here's what's different.

**GBP category specificity matters more.**
"Lawyer" is a GBP category. So is "Family Lawyer," "Criminal Defence Attorney," and "Immigration Consultant." The specific category is what surfaces in filtered searches and Maps suggestions. Most professional services businesses pick the broad one at signup and never revisit it.

**Google weighs the individual practitioner.**
For a plumber, the business name is the brand. For a family lawyer or therapist, the practitioner name often carries as much or more trust weight. Each named professional needs their own bio page — not a team page with three names on it. Full name, specific specialisation, licence or call number if it's public information, actual years of practice (not "extensive experience"), real headshot.

**The review ask is different.**
You can't hand someone a QR card at the checkout counter after a therapy session or a court appearance. The ask goes by email, 2–3 days after a positive outcome. Reference the matter type in general terms — no client names, no case specifics. One short sentence. Firms that do this consistently build their review count steadily. Firms that don't have 4 reviews from 2019.

**Content is question-driven.**
People searching for professional services are asking specific questions: "Can I dispute a will in Ontario?" "What happens if I miss a CRA filing deadline?" "How long does a separation agreement take?" PAA boxes for professional service queries are full of these. One FAQ post per practice area per month — answering one question directly — indexes as 12 new pages a year. Each one answers a question a competitor probably hasn't written about.

**Multi-practitioner firms have an underused advantage.**
Three lawyers on the team means three potential bio pages that can rank for "[name] + [city]" queries. Most firms have one team page with three names on it. That's one URL where there could be three, each one a distinct ranking opportunity.

The plumber playbook works for plumbers. For professional services, the starting point is the practitioner, not the profile.
