# GBP Photo Strategy — What I Upload for Every New Client

## Slide 1 — Hook

Most local businesses upload a logo and one storefront shot.

That's not a photo strategy. Here's the framework I use for every new client.

---

## Slide 2 — The Photo Category That Matters Most

Google's photo categories aren't just labels.

"At Work" is the most valuable category for local service businesses.

These are photos of your team doing the actual job — on a job site, in the shop, delivering a service.

Google surfaces "At Work" photos more prominently in local search. They also convert better than exterior shots.

---

## Slide 3 — Full Category Guide

The six categories worth filling:

**Owner-supplied:** Logo (square, no white border) + Cover photo (landscape, your space or team at work)

**At Work:** On-site service delivery, in-shop work, team in action

**Team:** Individual or group photos — names optional but personalise the profile

**Interior:** Reception, showroom, waiting area, workspace

**Exterior:** Storefront with visible signage, parking, entrance

**Product:** Physical goods, completed work examples, before/after

---

## Slide 4 — What Not to Upload

Four things I never upload to a client GBP:

1. **Stock photos.** Google can identify them. They add no trust signal and may be flagged.
2. **Branded graphics or infographics.** These perform poorly. Real photos outperform designed assets.
3. **Blurry or dark photos.** Low quality signals a low-quality business.
4. **Irrelevant images.** Stay on-topic. Random team nights out don't help a service business's profile.

---

## Slide 5 — Upload Cadence

Upload 2–4 photos per month.

That's the minimum that signals an actively managed profile.

Consistency matters more than volume. One photo every two weeks beats 20 photos uploaded once.

Batch them on a specific day each month — same day you post your GBP update post.

---

## Slide 6 — Competitor Photo Benchmark

Before I plan a client's photo strategy, I check their top 3 competitors:

— How many photos do they have total?
— Which categories are they using?
— When did they last upload?

If Competitor A has 150 photos and you have 12, photo gap is your fastest signal improvement.

---

## Slide 7 — The Photo Setup Checklist

Day 1 minimum for a new GBP:

- [ ] Logo uploaded (square format, no border)
- [ ] Cover photo set (landscape, on-brand)
- [ ] 5+ At Work photos uploaded
- [ ] At least 3 Exterior photos
- [ ] At least 1 Team photo
- [ ] Recurring monthly upload scheduled

## LinkedIn Caption (300–600 words)

When I take over a new client's Google Business Profile, one of the first things I check is the photo section.

Most businesses have uploaded a logo and maybe one photo of the front of the building. Sometimes both photos are three years old.

That's not a photo strategy. Here's the framework I use.

**At Work is the highest-leverage photo category for service businesses.**

Google has categories built into the GBP photo upload — and "At Work" is the one most businesses ignore. These are photos of your team doing the actual job. On the job site. In the workshop. Delivering a service.

Google surfaces At Work photos more prominently in local search results and in the GBP photo gallery. They also convert better than exterior shots — because they show the real product: the service itself.

**Fill each category, then maintain cadence.**

The six categories worth filling: Owner-supplied (logo + cover photo), At Work, Team, Interior, Exterior, Product. You don't need 30 photos in each. You need at least a few in each that are sharp, on-topic, and real.

Then upload 2–4 new photos per month. Consistency signals an active, managed business. A profile updated regularly ranks better than one with 100 photos uploaded two years ago and nothing since.

**What not to upload.**

I never upload stock photos to a GBP. Google can identify them and they carry no trust signal. Branded graphics — infographics, promotional tiles — also underperform. Same for blurry, dark, or irrelevant images.

Real photos of real work, real people, and real locations. That's the standard.

**Benchmark the competition first.**

Before planning a client's upload strategy, I check their top 3 competitors: how many photos they have, which categories they've filled, and when they last uploaded.

If a competitor has 150 photos across all categories and your client has 14, photo gap is often the fastest signal improvement available without touching the website.

The day-1 minimum for any new GBP: logo, cover photo, 5 At Work photos, 3 Exterior photos, at least 1 Team photo. Then a recurring monthly upload date in the calendar.
