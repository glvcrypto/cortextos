# The Pause Conversation — How to Handle It When a Client Wants to Stop

## Slide 1 (Hook)

Every client I've ever had has thought about pausing SEO at least once.

Most don't say it out loud.

When they do, here's how I handle it.

---

## Slide 2

**First question I ask:**

"What outcome were you expecting that hasn't happened yet?"

The answer tells me which of three problems I'm actually dealing with.

---

## Slide 3

**Three diagnoses:**

1. Communication gap — I haven't shown the progress clearly enough
2. Timeline mismatch — they expected faster results than local SEO delivers
3. Actual underperformance — something isn't working and I need to own it

---

## Slide 4

**If it's a communication gap:**

Pull up the numbers together — live, on the call.

GBP Insights calls month-over-month. GSC impressions for their main service page. Direction requests.

Most clients have never seen these numbers. When they see the trend, the conversation shifts.

---

## Slide 5

**If it's a timeline mismatch:**

"Month 3 of local SEO always looks slow."

I explain how local ranking compounds over time — not with a fabricated chart, with an honest explanation. Then I ask if they want to stop at mile 3 of a marathon.

---

## Slide 6

**If it's actual underperformance:**

Don't defend. Diagnose.

Three questions:
— Has the GBP ranking moved at all since we started?
— Are there more reviews now than at the start?
— Has the primary category been edited by a third party?

Find the problem. Then fix it.

---

## Slide 7

**The two good outcomes:**

Either renewed confidence — because they can see what's actually happening — or a dignified exit where both sides understand why.

Both are better than a slow, resentful drift.

The worst outcome is losing a client who would have stayed if you'd just shown them the data.

## LinkedIn Caption (~420 words)

Every client I've ever had has thought about pausing SEO at least once.

Most don't say it out loud. They start going quiet on calls. The approval emails take longer. The questions get more pointed.

When they do bring it up, there's a right way to handle it and a wrong way.

The wrong way: get defensive. Remind them how long SEO takes. Reference the contract.

The right way: diagnose before you defend.

I ask one question first — "What outcome were you expecting that hasn't happened yet?"

The answer tells me which of three problems I'm actually dealing with.

**1. Communication gap.**
I haven't shown them progress clearly enough. This is more common than it should be. Pull up GBP Insights calls month-over-month on the call, together, live. Show the GSC impressions trend for their main service page. Most clients have never seen these numbers. When they see which direction things are moving, the conversation changes.

**2. Timeline mismatch.**
They expected faster results than local SEO delivers. This is usually a setup problem — the expectation wasn't set clearly at the start. The fix is honest: "Month 3 always looks slow. Here's what month 9 typically looks like." Not with a made-up graph. With a real explanation of how local ranking compounds. Then I let them decide if they want to stop at mile 3.

**3. Actual underperformance.**
Something isn't working and I need to own it. Don't defend — diagnose. Has the GBP ranking moved at all since we started? Are there more reviews now than at the start? Has the primary category been edited by someone else without us catching it? Find the actual problem, say it out loud, and fix it.

A good pause conversation ends one of two ways: renewed confidence because they can finally see what's happening, or a dignified exit where both sides understand why.

Both are better than a slow, resentful drift where the client stays unhappy and you stay frustrated.

The worst outcome is losing a client who would have stayed if you'd just pulled up the GBP Insights panel and walked through it together.

If you've ever had this conversation — or spent six months avoiding it — I'd like to hear how it went.
