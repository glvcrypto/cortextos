## LinkedIn Post

Most people check their average position in GSC. That single number hides more than it reveals.

Average position is a blend of everything you rank for — position 1 for a zero-volume keyword and position 22 for a high-volume one average out to position 11. You can look at 11 and feel good while your biggest opportunity goes untouched.

The more useful view: impressions and position together.

Four quadrants, and the action for each:

**High impressions + high position (top 1–3)**
Visible and ranking well. The question shifts to click-through rate. If impressions are high but clicks are low, the title tag or meta description isn't earning the click — that's a title tag problem, not a ranking problem.

**High impressions + low position (11–25)**
This is where most quick wins live. You're getting surfaced; you're not getting clicked. Usually a title tag and H1 alignment issue — the page covers the topic but the headline doesn't match the query Google is associating with it. Fix the headline match, re-request indexing, give it two to three weeks.

**Low impressions + high position (top 1–10)**
Ranking well for something with low search volume. Not a priority unless it's a service category you want to expand.

**Low impressions + low position**
New content still building authority, thin content, or a topic with no confirmed search demand. Decide: build it out or retire it.

How to get this view:
GSC → Performance → Web → last 3 months → Queries tab → export to a sheet. Add two columns: position bucket (top 3 / 4–10 / 11–25 / 26+) and impressions bucket (your thresholds — 100+/month and 50+/month are reasonable starting points for most local service sites). The quadrant 2 rows are your audit list.

Do this before commissioning any new content. Those pages are already indexed, already have impressions — they just need the signal adjusted.

## IG Static Caption

GSC: impressions and position together tell you more than average position alone.

Four quadrants:
— High impressions + top 3 position → check CTR (title tag problem if clicks are low)

— Low impressions + high position → small niche or low demand
— Low impressions + low position → new content or thin content
