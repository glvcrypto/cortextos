## LinkedIn Post

A client asked me what to do when their top competitor has 180 Google reviews and they have 23.

The gut reaction is "we need more reviews." That's true — but it's not a strategy. The gap doesn't close in a month. The question is how to compete while it closes.

**Three things I focus on when a client is behind on reviews:**

**1. Velocity matters more than count.**
Google doesn't just look at total review count — it looks at whether the business is being reviewed consistently. A competitor with 180 reviews but none in the last six months is not as strong a signal as a competitor with 40 reviews and a steady 4–5 new ones arriving every month.

Build a system for collecting reviews consistently, not in bursts. Consistent velocity is the metric to close, not the total number.

**2. Guide for specificity.**
A review that names the service, the person, and the outcome ("replaced the furnace in three hours, no mess, followed up two days later") carries more signal than five stars and "great service."

When asking for reviews, give customers context: "It helps others if you mention the service you had done and what the experience was like." Not scripted — just a prompt. The people who leave reviews are usually happy to say more if they know what's useful.

**3. Justification engineering.**
GBP pulls phrases from reviews and shows them in the local pack as justification text — snippets like "In reviews: [phrase]." If your reviews consistently mention the specific services you want to rank for, those phrases are more likely to appear as justification text under your listing.

This is the connection most business owners don't know about: the words in your reviews affect what Google shows in the search result, not just your star rating.

The 180-review gap doesn't close this week. But it closes faster when every new review is specific, and you're collecting them every month without stopping.
