## Slides

**Slide 1 — Hook**
"You sell in-store and online. Most local SEO advice assumes you only do one.

Here's how I set up both ranking tracks without letting either one fall behind."

**Slide 2 — Two ranking systems, two goals**
Local pack and organic product pages are separate ranking systems.

Local pack → GBP → map pack → in-store visits. Proximity + category + reviews.

Organic product pages → website + Merchant Center → Google Shopping → online orders. Content + intent match + product feed.

Both matter. They don't compete with each other. They serve different customers at different moments.

**Slide 3 — GBP first if in-store revenue dominates**
If most of your revenue walks through the door, the local pack is the priority ranking.

Three GBP signals that drive in-store intent:
- Primary category: match the specific product category your top competitors use in Maps
- Photos: active inventory photos — what customers are coming in to buy
- Review ask: prompt in-store buyers at the point of sale, not days later

In-store visibility lives and dies on GBP.

**Slide 4 — Local product pages for the "buy X in [city]" searches**
"[Brand] [model] [city]" searches are real. They have intent.

A dedicated local product landing page for your core categories captures them.

Title tag formula: [Brand] + [Model or Category] + in + [City]
First paragraph: what you stock, where you are, why a customer would come in versus ordering online.
Include a map embed and click-to-call.

One page per core product category. Not one page per SKU.

**Slide 5 — Google Merchant Center connects the two tracks**
The free tier of Google Merchant Center surfaces your inventory in local search — "in stock nearby" results — with no ad spend.

Setup: create account → link to GBP → verify/claim website → upload product feed.

The feed connects online discovery to in-store inventory. When someone searches for a product you carry, the Merchant Center listing points them to your physical store.

Reference: setup walkthrough covered in the Nov 21 post.

**Slide 6 — GA4 attribution for two channels**
You need to know which traffic is coming from GBP and which is coming from product pages.

GBP: add UTM to your GBP website field (utm_source=google, utm_medium=gmb). In GA4 → Traffic acquisition → filter medium = gmb.

Product page organic: GA4 → Traffic acquisition → google / organic. Filter by landing page to isolate product category pages.

Two segments. Two sets of conversion data. One clear picture of which track is working.

**Slide 7 — CTA**
Which track gets your attention first depends entirely on where most of your revenue comes from.

In-store majority → GBP first. Online majority → product pages + Merchant Center first. Both equally → GBP, then product pages one at a time.

Revenue tells you the sequence. Not preference.

## LinkedIn Caption

Most local SEO advice is built for one kind of business: a service business or a pure e-commerce shop. It doesn't say much about businesses that do both — a retail location where customers walk in, and an online store where they can order from anywhere.

I've set this up enough times to know the mistake: treating both channels as one problem, optimising the GBP for general visibility, and wondering why the product search traffic isn't converting.

They're separate ranking systems. They need separate attention.

**The local pack is for in-store intent.**
GBP → map pack → someone who is ready to come in today. The ranking signals are proximity, category, and reviews. The conversion signal is "call" or "directions." If in-store revenue is the majority of your business, this is the track that gets your focus first.

**Organic product pages are for purchase intent with more research time.**
"[Brand] [model] in [City]" — this person knows what they want, they're checking if you carry it and where you are. A dedicated landing page per core product category, with the right title tag and a live inventory signal, captures that search. One page per category, not one page per SKU.

**Google Merchant Center connects the two.**
The free tier of Merchant Center lets your in-stock inventory show up in local search results. It links GBP visibility to product inventory data. Setup is a product feed — a spreadsheet or WooCommerce/Shopify plugin — and the listings are free. If you have physical inventory, this is worth the one-time setup.

**GA4 tells you which track is actually driving revenue.**
Add UTM parameters to your GBP website field (medium=gmb) to isolate that traffic segment in GA4. Organic product page traffic shows up under google/organic filtered by the landing page. Two segments, tracked separately.

The sequence isn't complicated: whichever channel brings in most of your revenue gets first attention. Revenue decides, not preference.
