## Slide 2 — GBP: Category, Photos, and the First-Visit Signal

**Category:** Google's GBP category is "Pediatric Dentist" (US spelling). Use it as your primary — it separates your profile from general dentists in Maps filtering. Your on-site content should include both "Paediatric" and "Pediatric" spellings to capture Canadian and US-spelling search variants.

**Photos:** Parents are shortlisting on visual cues before they visit your website. GBP photos should show the reception and treatment rooms — bright, child-oriented design, visual/sensory-friendly details (if applicable) — not just the exterior and staff headshots. A parent of an anxious child is assessing the environment from your photos before they call.

**First-visit signal in your profile description:** "Welcoming children from their first birthday" or equivalent should be in your GBP profile description. The CAAPD and AAPD both recommend the first dental visit by age 1. Parents who know this are searching proactively. Your profile should confirm you see infants, not leave them to wonder.

## Slide 3 — The Parent is the Client

The child is the patient. The parent is the decision-maker and the searcher. Paediatric dentist content that speaks only to the clinical service (tooth eruption timelines, fluoride schedules) without addressing the parent's actual anxieties will not convert.

Common parent searches that your content needs to answer:

- "First dental visit age 1 [city]" — what to expect, how to prepare, what happens at the appointment
- "Dentist for anxious child [city]" — anxiety management options explained (nitrous oxide/laughing gas, tell-show-do technique, sensory-friendly environment)
- "Dentist for child with autism [city]" / "dentist for child with ADHD [city]" — increasingly common search; a practice that explicitly states experience with neurodiverse children captures a search that almost no paediatric website addresses
- "Dental x-ray safe for children [city]" — a parent anxiety driven by radiation concerns; a factual, calm explanation of digital X-ray dose context outperforms a practice that ignores the question
- "How much does a paediatric dentist cost [city]" — cost anxiety; address insurance coverage, fee guides, and payment options rather than leaving parents to discover at checkout

## Slide 4 — First Visit Page: The Highest-Converting Page

The first visit page is the highest-converting page on a paediatric dentist website. It is the page most likely to turn a parent's search into a new patient registration.

What this page needs:

- What happens at the first visit — step by step, in plain language (parent anxiety reduction = conversion)
- What age to bring a child for the first visit (CAAPD/AAPD: by age 1 or first tooth eruption — state this clearly and attribute it)
- What to bring (insurance card, health history form, comfort item for the child)
- What NOT to do before the visit (don't promise the child it won't hurt; don't bribe with post-visit treats — these are evidence-based dental behaviour guidance points that build credibility with informed parents)
- A gallery or photos from the treatment room — the parent is mentally preparing their child; visuals help

## Slide 5 — Per-Service Pages

Each of these has its own parent search and needs its own page:

- **Fissure sealants** — preventive; school-age children; "dental sealants for kids [city]" is a specific and common parent search after a teacher or school health nurse recommendation
- **Fluoride treatment** — another preventive search; some parents have questions about fluoride safety — the page should address this with accurate information rather than avoiding the topic
- **Nitrous oxide / laughing gas** — anxious child management; parents actively search this when their child has had a negative dental experience elsewhere; explicit page, not just a bullet point
- **Space maintainers** — post-early-tooth-loss; a specialised search that most general dentist websites don't address at all
- **Early orthodontic assessment** — "Phase 1 orthodontics [city]," "early braces assessment [city]" — parents referred by school dentist screenings; a page here captures the referral-driven search before they go to an orthodontist directly

## Slide 6 — Trust Signals That Differentiate

- **Specialist designation** — a paediatric dentist who has completed an accredited 2-year residency and holds the Royal College of Dentists of Canada (RCDC) specialist designation is a certified specialist, not just a general dentist who sees children. This distinction is verifiable and matters to informed parents. State it explicitly — do not assume parents will infer it.
- **RCDSO / CDSBC registration** — publicly verifiable; display the registration number
- **Experience with anxious or neurodiverse children** — if this is genuinely a practice strength, state it explicitly; it captures a high-value, underserved search
- **Sibling registration** — a practical signal for families with multiple children; "we'll keep all your children's records together" reduces administrative friction and increases the likelihood of registering additional family members
- **Reviews naming the child's experience** — "my four-year-old was terrified of dentists; he left saying he liked it there" converts more first appointments than a five-star average without context
