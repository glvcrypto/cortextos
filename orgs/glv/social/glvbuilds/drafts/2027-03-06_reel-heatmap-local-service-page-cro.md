## LinkedIn Caption

Heat mapping data for local service pages is often misread as a design question. It's actually a conversion question.

Here's what each data type is telling you and what to do about it:

**Scroll depth (mobile view)**
If the majority of mobile visitors are leaving before they reach your phone number or contact CTA, that's not a content problem — it's a placement problem. The test: open your service page on a mobile device as a first-time visitor. Can you see a way to contact the business without scrolling? If not, move the CTA or phone number above that fold. For most local service pages, the phone number should be in the header or sticky bar — not the footer.

**Click heatmap**
Clicks on non-interactive elements — images, headings, blocks of text — show where visitors expected to be able to do something. A photo of your work that gets clicked repeatedly is telling you: make it a link, or add a button near it. A heading that gets clicked could become an anchor link. The heatmap surfaces where intent exists but nothing happens.

**Rage clicks**
Repeated clicks in the same spot signal frustration: something looks like a button but doesn't respond, a link is broken, or a mobile tap target is too small. Check each rage click location for broken links, non-functional CTAs, and tap targets that overlap or don't register correctly on mobile.

**The session recording shortcut**
Clarity's session recordings let you watch real visitors navigate. Before reading heatmap summaries, watch 5–10 mobile sessions on your service page. The pattern — where they slow down, where they stop scrolling, what they click — usually becomes obvious in the first three recordings.

Most local service pages have the same conversion problem: contact information buried in the footer, phone number not coded as a `tel:` link for mobile tap, and a CTA that's below the fold on the device type that sends most of the traffic.

Read the data. Move the element. That's usually the entire fix.
