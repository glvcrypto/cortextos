# Local Link Building — The 4 Sources I Actually Use

## Slide 1 — Hook

Most local SEO link-building guides tell you to guest post.

I've never done that for a local service client.

Here are the four sources that actually work — and why they're easier to get than you think.

---

## Slide 2 — Source 1: Chamber of Commerce / BIA Membership

Joining your local chamber or business improvement area does two things at once:

**Citation:** Your NAP gets listed in a trusted local directory.

**Link:** Most chamber and BIA websites link back to member business websites.

Cost: typically $200–$600/year. You get a legitimate backlink + a NAP citation from a locally trusted source in one step.

This is the first link I get for every new local SEO client.

---

## Slide 3 — Source 2: Supplier and Manufacturer Dealer Directories

If your client sells or installs products from a manufacturer, check whether that manufacturer has a dealer locator or partner directory on their website.

Most do.

A link from a brand's official dealer directory is a high-authority, niche-relevant backlink. The only requirement: being an authorised dealer.

Worth checking for every product line your client carries.

---

## Slide 4 — Source 3: Local Press Story Pitching

Local news publications — whether print, digital, or radio — regularly run local business features.

A business milestone, a community initiative, or a niche expertise story can generate a permanent citation and a backlink.

Approach: one specific story angle, one email, one contact. Not a press release — a genuine story pitch.

A single story placed in a local publication can be worth more than ten directory submissions.

---

## Slide 5 — Source 4: Non-Competing Local Business Partnerships

Identify businesses that serve the same customer at a different stage or for a different need.

A plumber and an HVAC contractor. A real estate agent and a mortgage broker. A landscaper and a fence installer.

Offer to feature them on your resources or partners page in exchange for a listing on theirs.

These are the easiest links to get because they're built on an existing business relationship.

---

## Slide 6 — What to Track

For each source, log:

- Business/publication name
- Contact name and email
- Date outreach sent
- Response (yes/no/follow-up needed)
- Link live (Y/N) + URL

Don't manage this in your head. One simple spreadsheet is enough.

## LinkedIn Caption (300–600 words)

When local SEO guides cover link building, they usually recommend guest posting, HARO, or digital PR campaigns.

Those strategies can work — for national brands with full-time marketing teams.

For a local service business with a solo owner and no PR agency, they're almost impossible to execute. So I don't recommend them.

Here are the four sources I've actually used to build local backlinks for clients.

**1. Chamber of Commerce or BIA membership**

This is always the first one. Joining your local chamber or business improvement area does two things simultaneously: you get a NAP citation in a trusted local directory, and most chambers link directly to member websites.

The cost ranges from roughly $200 to $600 per year depending on the organisation. You're paying for networking and community presence — the link and citation are a byproduct.

This is the most defensible, lowest-effort backlink available to any local business.

**2. Supplier and manufacturer dealer directories**

If your client is an authorised dealer for any product — power equipment, HVAC systems, plumbing fixtures, software — check whether the manufacturer has a dealer locator on their website.

Most do. And a link from a brand's official dealer directory is niche-relevant, authoritative, and often high-DA. The only requirement is being an actual authorised dealer.

I check every product line a client carries before moving to any other link source.

**3. Local press story pitching**

Local publications — community newspapers, regional news sites, local radio station websites — regularly run local business features. A meaningful milestone, a community initiative, or a niche expertise story can place your client in front of a real audience and generate a permanent backlink.

The key is pitching a genuine story, not sending a press release. One specific angle, one email, one journalist contact.

A placed story in a local publication often outperforms dozens of directory submissions in both link quality and referral traffic.

**4. Non-competing local business partnerships**

Identify businesses that serve the same customer at a different point in their journey.

A plumber and an HVAC contractor share customers. So do a real estate agent and a home inspector. A landscaper and a fence installer.

A simple "partner resources" page on each website — where each business lists the other — creates a mutual link. These are the easiest links to secure because they're grounded in an existing relationship.

I manage all four of these with a simple tracking spreadsheet: source, contact, outreach date, response, link live. That's it.
