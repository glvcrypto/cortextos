## LinkedIn Caption (~360 words)

Something I notice consistently when local businesses start tracking their lead sources: organic search leads tend to close at a higher rate than paid.

It's not a universal rule. But it's common enough that I build it into how I explain local SEO ROI.

**Why the pattern exists:**

Search intent operates on a spectrum. A paid click often comes from someone in comparison mode — evaluating options, not yet committed. Organic clicks for local service queries tend to come from someone who has already decided they need the service and is now choosing a provider.

That's a different buyer at a different point in the decision.

For local service businesses with short buying windows — trades, health services, legal, automotive — that difference in intent can translate directly into close rate.

**What I do with this:**

I ask clients to track close rate by lead source. Even a basic spreadsheet: date, lead source (paid, organic, referral, direct), outcome (booked, no-show, closed, lost). After 90 days, the pattern usually becomes visible.

Attribution is imperfect — some businesses don't have the call volume to get clear results quickly. But directionally, it tends to show the same thing.

**Why it matters for the conversation:**

When a client asks "is local SEO working?", cost-per-lead is one frame. Close rate is often a better one. A lead that closes at a higher rate is worth more than a cheaper lead that converts less often, even if the acquisition cost looks lower.

Organic local SEO doesn't just lower cost per lead. It tends to improve the quality of the lead.

That's the framing that makes the long-term investment make sense — and it's the one I use when a client is comparing organic investment against shifting budget to paid.
