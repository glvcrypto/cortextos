## Slide 2

**The problem with one city page in a competitive market**

A single "Plumbing Toronto" page tries to be equally relevant to a searcher in Etobicoke and a searcher in Scarborough simultaneously.

Google handles this by varying local pack results by GPS location. Your generic city page competes against a competitor who has a specific page for each district — and their page has hyper-local signals yours doesn't.

## Slide 3

**When to build neighbourhood pages**

**The right time:**
- City page indexed + ranking position 4–10 for 60+ days
- GSC showing impressions or clicks from specific neighbourhood names or postal codes
- You actually service that neighbourhood

**Not yet:**
- City page isn't indexed or is under 60 days live
- You'd be creating pages for areas you don't cover
- You can't add at least one genuinely unique element per page

## Slide 4

**How to structure them**

URL: `/service/city-neighbourhood/`
e.g. `/furnace-repair/toronto-etobicoke/`

Title tag: `[Service] in [Neighbourhood], [City] | [Brand]`
H1: `[Service] in [Neighbourhood]`

Body (300–500 words) must include:
- Neighbourhood name in sentence one
- One local reference (a landmark, a housing stock note, a known area characteristic)
- Service area note: specific streets, intersections, or postal codes you cover

---

## Slide 5

**The thin content trap**

Don't copy-paste your city page with the neighbourhood name swapped throughout. Google identifies near-duplicate pages and discounts the one that appears less authoritative.

Each neighbourhood page needs at least one genuinely unique element:
- A neighbourhood-specific service note ("North York's 1960s homes often have original galvanised pipes — that's the first thing I check on new jobs there")
- Different FAQs relevant to that area
- Neighbourhood-specific service area note (streets, postal codes, landmarks)

If you can't write 300 words that are meaningfully different from your city page — wait until you have more local knowledge for that area.

---

## Slide 6

**The expansion cadence**

Start with 2–3 neighbourhoods: the ones where you do most of your actual work, or where GSC already shows impressions.

One new page per month, after your city page is live and indexed.

Track each page with a rank grid tool (Sep 24 — HEATMAP) at launch and at 30/60/90 days.

Target: neighbourhood page ranking top 3 in its district before adding the next one.

## LinkedIn caption

The "city page plateau" is one of the more frustrating stages of a local SEO engagement.

You published a well-structured "[service] [city]" page. It's indexed. The GBP is optimised. Some backlinks have been built. It ranks — position 6, position 7 — and it won't move.

In competitive metro areas, part of the answer is often a level of geographic specificity that a single city page can't provide.

**The case for neighbourhood landing pages:**

A single "Plumbing Toronto" page tries to be equally relevant to a searcher in Etobicoke and a searcher in Scarborough simultaneously. Google handles this by varying local results by GPS location — but your one page competes against a competitor who has a specific page for Etobicoke, a specific page for North York, and a specific page for Scarborough. Their pages carry hyper-local signals yours simply can't match.

**When the signal is right to build:**

Two indicators I watch for: the city page has been indexed and ranking position 4–10 for at least 60 days, and GSC is showing impressions or clicks from neighbourhood-level searches or specific postal codes. Both need to be true. Don't build neighbourhood pages to inflate page count — build them when you have geographic data supporting demand.

**How to keep them out of thin content territory:**

Each page needs at least one genuinely unique element beyond the neighbourhood name. The area's housing stock and what that means for service needs. Specific streets, intersections, or postal codes covered. Neighbourhood-specific FAQs. If you can't write 300 words that are meaningfully different from your city page, you're not ready for a neighbourhood page there yet. Swapping the city name throughout a copy-pasted page won't fool Google and won't rank.

**The expansion cadence:**

Start with the 2–3 neighbourhoods where you do most of your actual work, or where GSC already shows impressions. One new page per month after the city page is established. Track each at launch and at 30/60/90 days with a rank grid tool. Let a neighbourhood page reach top 3 in its district before adding the next one.

This is a 6–12 month strategy for a competitive city. But for clients with a healthy city page stuck at position 6, it's usually the highest-leverage next move.
