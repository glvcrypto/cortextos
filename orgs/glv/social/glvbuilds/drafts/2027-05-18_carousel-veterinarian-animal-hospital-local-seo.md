## Slide 2 — GBP Category: Veterinarian vs. Animal Hospital

"Veterinarian" and "Animal Hospital" are separate Google Business Profile categories.

"Animal Hospital" carries a specific association in Maps — it often signals emergency or extended-hours care. If your clinic is a full-service hospital with emergency capability, that distinction matters in how clients filter results. If your clinic is appointment-based daytime-only, "Veterinarian" is the accurate primary category.

A practice offering both appointment and emergency services can list "Animal Hospital" as primary and "Veterinarian" as secondary. What it should not do is list the wrong primary category and lose visibility in the filter that matches how clients actually search when their pet needs help urgently.

## Slide 3 — Per-Species Pages

"Cat vet [city]." "Dog dentist [city]." "Exotic animal vet [city]." "Reptile vet near me." "Rabbit vet [city]."

These are distinct searches made by pet owners who want a vet experienced with their specific animal. A practice that sees cats, dogs, small mammals, birds, and reptiles — but only has a general "services" page — ranks for none of the species-specific searches.

Per-species pages covering what wellness care looks like for that animal, which conditions the clinic commonly treats, and what an appointment involves answer the questions that move a pet owner from search to booking.

---

## Slide 4 — Per-Service Pages

"Dog teeth cleaning [city]." "Spay neuter [city]." "Senior pet care [city]." "Puppy vaccinations [city]." "Veterinary surgery [city]."

Each of these is a service search made by a pet owner with a specific need in mind. A single "services" page listing everything ranks for none of them specifically.

Service pages that cover what the procedure involves, what preparation the pet owner needs to do, recovery expectations, and a prompt to book — address the questions clients research before calling. They also capture searches from clients who want that specific service and are comparing clinics.

---

## Slide 5 — Veterinarian Bio Pages

Each named veterinarian is a local SEO asset on its own.

A bio page — DVM designation, provincial registration number (College of Veterinarians of Ontario, College of Veterinarians of British Columbia, or the relevant provincial college), species focus (small animal generalist, exotic, equine, feline-only), languages spoken, and areas of clinical interest — creates a searchable page that a staff directory grid never will.

In markets with large newcomer populations, a vet who speaks Cantonese, Mandarin, Punjabi, or another language and has that language signal on their bio page has a distinct ranking opportunity for pet owners who prefer to discuss their pet's care in their first language.

---

## Slide 6 — Emergency vs. Daytime-Only: Two Different Page Requirements

Emergency queries and appointment-based queries have completely different conversion requirements.

A client searching "emergency vet [city]" at 11pm is in crisis. They will call the first result that signals it can help right now — explicit after-hours hours, a phone number above the fold, a clear statement of what emergencies the clinic sees, and a page that loads in under two seconds.

An appointment-based wellness client searching "dog vaccinations [city]" has time. They want to know who the vets are, what the process looks like, and what other pet owners say.

A clinic that is daytime-only should make that clear on the site and on GBP — it avoids the frustrating experience of a client in crisis calling a closed clinic, and it removes the ranking signal that drives emergency searches to a page that can't convert them.
