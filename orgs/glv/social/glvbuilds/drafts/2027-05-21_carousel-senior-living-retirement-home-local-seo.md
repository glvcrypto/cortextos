## Slide 2 — GBP Category: Four Different Searches, Four Different Listings

"Retirement home [city]." "Assisted living [city]." "Memory care [city]." "Long-term care [city]."

These are four different GBP categories with four different search populations and four different regulatory frameworks. A "Retirement Community" listing does not appear to someone filtering for "Nursing Home" or "Long-term Care Facility." A facility that offers multiple care levels needs to understand which categories apply to its licensed services and list accordingly.

Getting this wrong means being invisible to clients searching for the specific care level they need — often at a moment when the need is urgent.

---

## Slide 3 — Who Is Actually Doing the Search

The majority of searches for senior living are made by adult children — typically in the 45–65 age range — researching options for an aging parent. Not by the prospective resident.

This changes everything about how copy should be written. The person searching wants to know: Is this a safe environment? What does the staff look like? What activities are available? What does daily life look like for a resident? What does the care escalation process look like if a parent's needs change?

A website written entirely from the perspective of "you'll love living here" misses the actual decision-maker. The person filling out the contact form is almost never the person who will live there.

---

## Slide 4 — Per-Care-Level Pages

Independent living, assisted living, memory care, and long-term care are four distinct living arrangements with different care models, different regulatory requirements, and different client concerns.

A family researching memory care for a parent with dementia is not the same prospect as one researching an independent living suite for a parent who is active and self-sufficient. Their questions are different, their timelines are different, and the trust signals they are looking for are different.

A single "care options" page that lists all four levels as bullet points answers none of them specifically. A page for each care level — what the level involves, who it's suitable for, what a typical day looks like, and what the transition process is if care needs change — gives the decision-maker what they actually need to evaluate a fit.

---

## Slide 5 — Proximity Matters More in This Category

"Retirement home [neighbourhood]" often outperforms "retirement home [city]" for this category.

Families want a facility close to them — so they can visit regularly and respond quickly if needed. A retirement home 45 minutes across a city is a harder choice than one 10 minutes from the adult child's home, even if both meet the care requirements.

Neighbourhood-level signals in copy — naming the neighbourhoods the facility is close to, noting proximity to hospitals, transit routes, and local landmarks — improve visibility for the hyperlocal searches that families who are nearby make first.

---

## Slide 6 — Tour Booking Page

A facility tour is almost always a required step before any family makes a commitment. A tour booking page — what the tour includes, how long it takes, who to ask for on arrival, and what to bring or prepare — reduces friction for the family that is ready to see the space.

Most senior living websites have a contact form and a general "schedule a tour" button that goes to a generic inquiry page. A dedicated tour page that walks the visitor through what to expect signals transparency about the experience and makes booking feel like the obvious next step rather than a commitment to a sales process.

Availability and current occupancy should not be stated as specific figures unless they are confirmed and can be kept current. "Contact us to inquire about current availability" is accurate and appropriate.
