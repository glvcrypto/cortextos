# IG Carousel Style Research — @glv.marketing
**Date:** 2026-05-16  
**Scope:** Readability-first carousel accounts in marketing/SEO/service-business adjacent niches. NOT over-designed or illustration-heavy — focus on "looks plain at first glance but performs."  
**Brief framing:** Plain ≠ bad. Question is: what's the minimum set of design moves that lifts plain → polished without sacrificing readability?

---

## Reference Accounts

### 1. @justinwelsh — Justin Welsh
**Followers (IG):** ~36K (primary audiences on LinkedIn ~582K, Twitter ~487K)  
**Niche:** One-person business / solopreneur scaling, productized consulting, creator economy  
**Carousel-native:** Yes — actively uses IG carousels as educational content, repurposed from LinkedIn

**Carousel Topics That Perform:**
- "How I built an $8M business with 0 employees" — transformation/proof hook
- "The exact systems I use to work 4 hours a day" — promise hook
- Step-by-step solopreneur frameworks (content flywheel, offer ladder)
- "5 things I'd tell my 30-year-old self about business" — retrospective hook

**Design Pattern:**
- Background: White or off-white — never a template gradient
- Typography: Heavy-weight sans-serif headline (often single sentence, very large); body text is sparse — one main idea per slide, not paragraphs
- Color: White + black + one warm accent (orange or amber on highlight slides). Neutral throughout with a single pop
- Layout: One claim per slide, no multi-column, no icon grids. The visual field is mostly whitespace with the headline dominating
- Whitespace usage: Generous — slides feel uncrowded even on mobile
- Hook: Specific numbers anchor slide 1 ("$8M," "4 hours," "6 months") — specificity creates the pattern interrupt, not visual complexity

**What Makes It "Polished Plain" vs Just Plain:**
1. Extreme size contrast — headline is 3-4x larger than any supporting text
2. Consistent slide rhythm — every slide feels like the same template
3. One idea only per slide — no scan cost
4. Hook specificity replaces visual complexity as the attention mechanism
5. Brand color appears once, not everywhere — restraint signals intentionality

## Design Axis Findings

### 1. Typography
**Pattern across readability-first accounts:**
- Heavy/bold sans-serif headline (think Montserrat Bold, Anton, or similar) — one sentence max
- Regular or light weight for supporting body text — extreme contrast between headline and body weight
- Body text: 2-3 lines maximum per slide, never a paragraph
- Minimum functional: 2 weights of the same typeface (bold for headline, regular for body). Adding a second typeface is optional, not required

**Specific font combos cited for professional/educational carousels:**
- Montserrat Bold + Open Sans Regular — "modern but human," most-cited pairing for educational content
- Roboto Slab Bold for statement slides needing authority weight
- Inter or Roboto for B2B/agency positioning where "clean tech" is the signal

**What reads plain-but-unfinished vs plain-but-polished:** The difference is not the font — it's the size ratio. Polished readability-first carousels have a headline 3-4x larger than body text. Unfinished carousels use one size throughout or a weak 1.2x ratio.

---

### 2. Color
**Pattern across readability-first accounts:**
- 1 dominant background (white, off-white/cream, or dark navy/charcoal)
- 1 accent color appearing on 1-2 slides only (not every slide)
- Zero gradients — gradient backgrounds are the clearest signal of a Canva default template

**What works:**
- White + dark text + one warm accent (orange, amber) — Justin Welsh model
- Cream/off-white + warm brown — more editorial, less clinical (Because of Marketing model)
- Dark charcoal + white + teal/green accent — stands out in feed of light-background accounts (Ross Simmonds model)
- Orange brand accent appears once (slide 1 headline) → never used again until final CTA slide — this restraint is a deliberate polished move

**The minimum color upgrade from plain → polished:**  
Pick one background tone (not pure white — slightly warm or slightly dark) + one accent color used maximum twice per carousel. That's it. Everything else is restraint.

---

### 3. Layout
**Pattern across readability-first accounts:**
- One idea per slide. One. Not two, not "tip 3 of 7 and tip 4 of 7 combined." One.
- Slide progress indicator in corner ("3 / 10") — reduces drop-off by signaling payoff and showing how much is left
- 20% text rule: text covers no more than 20% of the canvas — the rest is breathing room
- Safe zone: all text within the center 80% of the canvas (avoids crop on different screen ratios)

**What makes a plain layout feel unfinished:**
- Text touching the edges (no margin)
- More than one "section" worth of content per slide
- No indication of how many slides remain
- All slides look the same density with no visual rhythm (no "evidence" or "visual break" slides)

**Minimum layout upgrade:** Add margins (padding on all four sides, at least 8-10% of canvas width), add slide number in corner, enforce one-idea-per-slide rule.

---

### 4. Hook Structure
**Highest-performing hooks in readability-first accounts (no visual noise needed to land them):**

1. **Specific number + claim:** "How I built an $8M business with 0 employees" — the number does the stopping, not the design
2. **Checklist/step promise:** "7 things to do before you publish any blog post" — numbered format creates instant perceived value
3. **Contrarian/myth-busting:** "Backlinks don't matter as much as you think. Here's what does." — cognitive speed bump
4. **Distribution gap reveal:** "You published a post. Now what? The 27 places you should be distributing it." — reveals hidden problem
5. **Framework promise:** "The 3-step content system used by top brands" — authority positioning without visual decoration

**What separates a strong hook on a plain slide from a weak one:**
Strong hooks are specific. "5 tips to improve your Instagram" is generic. "The 5 GBP settings most local businesses never touch" is specific enough to stop scroll on a white background with black text.

---

### 5. Sequencing
**Patterns that work for readability-first educational carousels:**

- **Linear teach (most common in this tier):** Hook → credibility anchor (specific data point or "X clients / X years") → numbered content slides → summary → CTA
- **Framework reveal:** Hook → problem → the framework (one component per slide) → how to apply → CTA
- **Curiosity loop (used selectively):** Bold claim → slides 2-4 deepen why it matters → reveal at slide 6-7 → CTA. Higher swipe-through, needs a strong enough hook to earn it

**Key mechanic — slide 2 credibility anchor (often skipped):** After the hook, viewers need a reason to trust the source before reading 8 more slides. Neil Patel's authority is baked in by name recognition; for a smaller account, slide 2 should do this work: "We've run [X] campaigns for [type of client] across [timeframe] — here's what we learned."

**Mid-carousel soft CTA (often skipped):** A save-prompt at slide 7-9 ("Save this checklist for your next campaign") receives 31% more saves than a final-slide-only CTA. The decision to save usually happens before the viewer reaches the end.

---

### 6. Imagery
**What readability-first accounts do instead of designed graphics:**
- UI screenshots (Search Console, Analytics, GBP dashboard) — informational, not decorative. The "screenshot as evidence" move is the single most powerful image substitute in the marketing education niche
- Real client result screenshots (review counts, ranking positions, before/after)
- Founder photo on slide 1 or 2 — personal brand signal; builds trust without requiring design work
- Campaign photography for campaign-analysis carousels (Because of Marketing) — the visual is the content, not an addition to it

**What to avoid:** Stock photos, illustrations, Canva icon packs used decoratively. These read as template-default, not intentional.

---

### 7. CTA Placement
**Pattern in readability-first accounts:**
- Soft CTA at slide 7-9: "Save this for next time you ___" — drives saves, timed when viewers have consumed enough to value the content
- Hard CTA on final slide: ONE action only (save, follow, or DM — not all three)
- Comment prompt as engagement trigger: Binary or short-answer question ("Which of these are you getting wrong? Drop the number below")
- DM-with-keyword for service businesses: "DM 'GBP' and I'll send you the checklist" — low friction, specific deliverable

**Best CTA for GLV carousel context:** Save-prompt at slide 8-9 + "Follow @glv.marketing for [weekly/local biz/GBP] breakdowns like this" on final slide. Avoid asking for DM until there's a specific resource to offer.

## 5-Bullet Synthesis: Minimum Design Moves for @glv.marketing

**These are the only changes that move readability-first carousels from "plain and unfinished" to "plain and professional":**

1. **Fix the type ratio.** Headline on slide 1 should be 3-4x the size of body text. If the headline doesn't fill 40-50% of the slide height, make it bigger. This is the single move with the most visual impact for least design effort.

2. **Pick one background tone and one accent color, then hold the line.** Warm off-white (not pure #FFFFFF) + one brand accent color used maximum twice per carousel (hook slide + final CTA only). Never a gradient. The restraint is the design.

3. **One idea per slide, always.** If a slide has two tips or two sentences of equal weight, it becomes a paragraph — and paragraphs don't get saved. Cut to one thing. Put the rest on the next slide.

4. **Add a slide counter and padding.** "3 / 10" in the bottom corner tells viewers they're making progress and there's a payoff coming. Generous padding (10%+ on all sides) makes even text-only slides feel intentional, not rushed.

5. **Replace decorative images with informational ones.** For GLV's niche (local marketing, GBP, service-business): swap stock photos for actual screenshots — GBP dashboard, review count graphs, search ranking positions, before/after stats. These earn more trust than any illustration and require no design skill to use.

## What NOT to Apply (Excluded Accounts and Patterns)

The following styles were identified but excluded from recommendations as incompatible with the readability-first brief:

- **@prettylittlemarketer** — Y2K scrapbook aesthetic (pink gradients, sparkle overlays, texture cutouts). High-performing in lifestyle/freelancer community; wrong signal for local business owner audience
- **@thechrisdo / The Futur** — Panoramic seamless canvas, type-as-art. High design floor required; production-intensive; audience skews creative professional
- **@becauseofmarketing** — Editorial campaign teardown format works but requires continuous access to fresh brand/campaign material as visual content; not replicable at volume for an agency account
- **Gradient backgrounds, icon grids, illustration overlays** — All signal "made in Canva template" rather than intentional design
