### Getting Your Boat Ready for Spring in Northern Ontario

*By Lee, Service Technician, Reyco Marine*

Ice goes out on Lake Superior's eastern shore, near the Sault, around the last week of April most years. Inland lakes in the Algoma district clear a week or two later. If your boat has been sitting in storage since October, the season is closer than it feels.

Spring commissioning is the work you do before your first launch. Do it right and your season starts on the water. Skip it and your first trip out becomes a troubleshooting session.

This checklist covers the key steps. It applies to most aluminium fishing boats and trailerable fibreglass boats. If you have questions about your specific setup, come into Reyco Marine at 11 White Oak Drive East in the Sault and we will walk through it with you.

## Pull the Cover and Inspect the Hull

Start with a full visual inspection before anything else. Look for cracks, stress fractures around hardware, and any signs of delamination or impact damage from the off-season. On aluminium boats, check for any oxidation or pitting that went unaddressed last fall. On fibreglass, press along the hull for soft spots that indicate water intrusion.

Check the drain plug. It should be out during storage and reinstalled before you launch. It sounds obvious. It is also responsible for a significant number of sinkings every spring.

## Service the Outboard

If you did not fog the cylinders before storage and did not change the lower unit oil at the end of last season, do it now before starting the motor.

**Lower unit oil:** Drain and inspect. Milky or grey oil means water is in the lower unit, usually from a failed seal. This needs to be addressed before the motor goes in the water.

**Spark plugs:** Inspect and replace if there is fouling or corrosion. Fresh plugs are cheap insurance on a motor that sat all winter.

**Fuel system:** If you ran the carburettor dry in the fall, you are in good shape. If not, stale fuel causes the majority of spring start-up problems. Drain and replace, or use a quality fuel stabiliser additive before starting.

**Impeller:** The water pump impeller should be replaced every two to three seasons, or any time the motor has run dry. It is a small rubber part that keeps the outboard's cooling system working. If it fails on the water, your motor overheats fast.

**Gear shift and throttle cables:** Move through the full range of motion before launch. Check for stiffness or fraying at the connection points.

## Check the Electrical System

Test all running lights, bilge pump, depth finder, and any onboard electronics. Corroded connections are the most common electrical issue after a winter in storage. Clean the battery terminals and test the charge. If the battery is three years old or more and slow to hold a charge, replace it before the season starts rather than mid-summer.

## Inspect the Trailer

The trailer takes as much wear as the boat itself. Inspect the wheel bearings, brake lights, and safety chains. Check the bunks or rollers for wear. Repack the wheel bearings if it has been more than two seasons or 3,000 km of hauling.

Check the trailer hitch coupler and the winch strap. Both should lock securely with no play. A loose coupler or failing strap is a road hazard.

## Check Safety Equipment

Ontario requires specific safety equipment on board. Before your first trip, confirm you have:

- A Canadian-approved lifejacket or PFD for each person on board
- A buoyant heaving line
- A manual bilge pump or bailer
- A sound signalling device
- A waterproof flashlight or flares

Replace anything that is expired or damaged. Check the fire extinguisher if you have one — they have inspection dates.

## Test Run Before You Launch

Run the motor in a barrel or a test tank before your first launch if possible. Check for water flow from the tell-tale, listen for unusual sounds, and confirm the gear shift and throttle are working smoothly. Five minutes on a test stand catches problems that would otherwise show up at the ramp.

## Book Service Early

Spring is the busiest time in any marine shop. If you find something during your commissioning that needs a technician, book it now. Reyco Marine's service department handles outboard motor repairs, lower unit rebuilds, and spring commissioning work. Call us at (705) 253-7828 or stop by to book a service appointment before the rush hits.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**When should I start spring commissioning in Northern Ontario?**
Target the first two weeks of May. Ice-out on most Algoma district lakes happens between late April and mid-May depending on the winter. Commissioning two to three weeks ahead of your planned first launch gives enough time to address any issues without rushing.

**What is the most common spring commissioning mistake?**
Skipping lower unit oil inspection. Milky lower unit oil means a failed seal and water intrusion. Running a motor in that condition damages the gears. It is a $10 fluid change that prevents a $600 to $2,000 repair.

**Can I do spring commissioning myself?**
Most of it, yes. Visual inspection, fluid checks, battery testing, and safety equipment review are straightforward. Impeller replacement requires pulling the lower unit and is manageable for a mechanically confident owner. Anything electrical beyond basic connections, and any issue found inside the powerhead, is worth having a technician handle.

### How to Winterize an Outboard Motor

*By Lee, Service Technician, Reyco Marine*

Water expands approximately 9% in volume when it freezes. A single freeze-thaw cycle inside an outboard's cooling passages is enough to crack a powerhead. On a motor worth several thousand dollars, proper winterization is not optional maintenance. It is basic protection.

Northern Ontario winters are hard on equipment that was not prepared correctly. The Sault sees average January lows around -15°C, and Algoma district temperatures regularly hit -25°C or colder. Any water left in the cooling system will find the weakest point.

This guide covers outboard winterization for typical 4-stroke and 2-stroke motors. If you are not confident completing any of these steps yourself, the service team at Reyco Marine in Sault Ste. Marie handles winterization appointments from September onward.

## Flush the Cooling System First

Before you do anything else, flush the outboard with fresh water. This removes salt, sediment, and minerals from the cooling passages — important even in freshwater environments where mineral deposits build up over a season.

Attach a flushing attachment to the lower unit and run the motor at idle for five to ten minutes until the water coming out of the tell-tale runs clear. Do not skip this step. It protects the impeller and the passages that the water flows through.

## Change the Lower Unit Gear Oil

Drain the lower unit oil and inspect it before refilling. Healthy lower unit oil is golden or amber. Milky or grey oil means water has entered through a failed seal, usually from a damaged propeller shaft seal or a propeller impact.

If the oil is milky, the seal needs to be replaced before winter storage. Water sitting in the lower unit through a freeze cycle accelerates corrosion and can seize internal components. This is a technician-level repair.

Refill with fresh lower unit oil rated for your motor. Consult your owner's manual for the specification. Fill from the lower drain plug until oil appears at the upper vent plug, then reinstall both.

## Fog the Cylinders

Cylinder fogging protects the internal surfaces of the engine from corrosion while it sits. Use a fogging oil designed for outboards.

With the motor running at idle, spray fogging oil into the carburettor throat or the air intake (for EFI motors, follow the manufacturer's instruction, as the process varies). On multi-cylinder motors, rotate through the cylinders. You are looking for the motor to begin running rough as the oil coats the cylinder walls, then shut it down while still fogging.

Do not run the motor to clear the fog. That defeats the purpose.

## Stabilise or Drain the Fuel System

Stale fuel is the most common cause of spring start-up problems. You have two options:

**Option 1 — Stabilise the fuel.** Add a marine-grade fuel stabiliser to a full tank of fresh fuel, run the motor for ten minutes to circulate it through the fuel system, then shut down.

**Option 2 — Drain and run dry.** Drain the fuel tank, then run the carburettor dry by letting the motor run until it stalls from fuel starvation. This prevents gum deposits from forming in the carburettor jets over winter.

EFI (electronic fuel injection) motors should use the stabiliser approach, not the run-dry approach.

## Grease All Fittings

Grease the steering pivot points, tilt tube, swivel bracket, and any grease fittings specified in your owner's manual. Use marine-grade grease. This prevents corrosion and seizing at all the points that take mechanical stress.

Spray the exterior hardware, clamp screws, and electrical connections with a moisture-displacing lubricant. Pull the propeller, grease the shaft, and reinstall or store the prop separately.

## Store the Battery Separately

Remove the battery from the boat and store it in a cool, dry location above freezing. A discharged battery can freeze in a Northern Ontario winter, which destroys it. Connect it to a battery maintenance charger over the off-season to keep it at full charge.

## Prepare the Motor for Storage

Disconnect the fuel line. Leave the engine cover off for a day if possible to allow any moisture to evaporate. Then reinstall the cover and store the motor in the upright position if it is a tilt-and-trim unit, or tilted down if it will be in an enclosed storage space where full height is not available.

Store the motor where it will not be exposed to direct moisture or freezing rain.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**When should I winterize my outboard in Northern Ontario?**
Before the first sustained freeze. In the Sault and Algoma district, that typically means completing winterization by mid-October. Do not wait for a hard freeze warning — by then you are already behind.

**How long does outboard winterization take?**
For a typical 4-stroke outboard, plan on two to three hours if you are doing it yourself for the first time. An experienced technician can complete most of the service in under two hours, depending on the motor size and what is found during inspection.

**Should I take the propeller off for winter?**
Yes. Pull the prop, grease the shaft, and store the prop separately. This prevents water from sitting against the shaft seal and eliminates freeze damage at that point. It also gives you a chance to inspect the prop blades and hub for impact damage before spring.

### Small Engine Maintenance Schedule for Northern Ontario

*By Damian, Service Technician, Reyco Marine*

Most small-engine manufacturers recommend an oil change every 50 operating hours. For a homeowner running a lawn mower four hours a week, that is roughly once per season. Most people skip it entirely.

Northern Ontario puts more strain on small engines than most of Canada. The Algoma district runs from -30°C winters to +30°C summers. Equipment sits idle for months, then runs hard when the season switches. A $15 oil change and a $4 spark plug prevent the majority of the repair jobs that come into the Reyco Marine service shop.

This schedule covers residential and light-commercial small engines: lawn tractors, zero-turn mowers, snowblowers, chainsaws, and generators. Follow it and the equipment lasts. Ignore it and you pay for it.

## Spring — Before the First Start

**Lawn tractors and zero-turn mowers:**
- Change the engine oil and oil filter
- Replace or clean the air filter
- Replace the spark plug
- Inspect and sharpen the blade — a dull blade tears grass rather than cutting it, which stresses the turf and the engine
- Check the tire pressure and battery charge
- Start the season with fresh fuel; drain or stabilise any fuel left from last year

**Chainsaws:**
- Clean the air filter and bar groove
- Inspect the chain tension and sharpness
- Check the chain brake and all safety features
- Inspect the bar for wear and burrs
- Start with fresh mixed fuel (2-stroke chainsaws)

**Generators:**
- Change the oil if it was not done before storage
- Test under load before you need it; do not discover a problem during a power outage
- Inspect the battery if it is an electric-start model

## Mid-Season — Midsummer Check

Midsummer is when equipment is working hardest and heat causes the most stress. Northern Ontario sees sustained temperatures above 25°C through July and early August. Hot ambient air reduces engine cooling efficiency.

**Check fluid levels:** Oil, hydraulic fluid on riding mowers with hydrostatic drive, and battery water on non-sealed batteries.

**Inspect belts and blades:** Mower decks take rock and debris hits all season. Check for blade damage and cracked or stretched belts. A damaged blade at speed is a safety issue.

**Clean cooling fins:** Blocked cooling fins cause overheating. Compressed air or a brush clears the debris. On air-cooled engines, the fins do all the cooling — keep them clear.

**Chainsaw maintenance:** After every tank of fuel on a professional-use saw, and every few tanks on a recreational saw, clean the bar groove and re-tension the chain. A loose chain at speed is dangerous.

## Fall — Pre-Storage

This is the most important maintenance window. Equipment that goes into storage correctly starts up reliably in spring. Equipment that doesn't often needs repair before it runs again.

**Mowers and tractors:**
- Final oil change of the season
- Fuel stabiliser in the tank, or run the carburettor dry
- Grease all fittings
- Remove and charge the battery; store it in a warm, dry location
- Clean the underside of the deck before storage — dried grass and debris hold moisture

**Snowblowers:**
- Change the engine oil before first use (fall is the right time, not spring)
- Check all shear bolts — carry extras
- Inspect the auger and impeller for wear
- Lubricate the chute rotation and all grease points
- Fresh fuel for the season; stale fuel from spring storage is a common cause of hard starts in October

**Generators:**
- Change the oil
- Stabilise the fuel or run the carburettor dry
- Test before storage; do not put a non-running generator away for six months

## Winter — Snowblower Season

Snowblower maintenance during the season is minimal if you did the fall prep correctly.

**Each use:** Clear the chute and auger housing after any clog. Never use your hand — use the clearing tool. Running dry or with a blocked discharge damages the impeller.

**Monthly check:** Oil level, belt condition, and shear bolt supply.

**When it is not starting in -20°C:** Check the spark plug first (they foul faster in cold starts), then fuel, then battery on electric-start models. Cold-weather starting problems are almost always one of those three.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I sharpen my lawn mower blade?**
Once at the start of the season, and again mid-season if you are mowing regularly. A sharp blade cuts cleanly and puts less stress on the engine. Most blades can be sharpened two or three times before they need replacement.

**Can I use regular motor oil in my small engine?**
Use the oil your manufacturer specifies. Most small four-stroke engines call for SAE 30 or 10W-30. Do not use automotive motor oil with friction modifiers — they can damage small engine components. When in doubt, bring the equipment in and we will confirm the right spec.

**What is the most common small engine repair in Northern Ontario?**
Carburettor problems from stale fuel are the most frequent reason equipment comes into our shop in spring. A carburettor clean costs less than $100 in most cases. It is entirely preventable by using fresh fuel and a stabiliser before storage.

### When to Repair Power Equipment and When to Replace It

*By Damian, Service Technician, Reyco Marine*

A new spark plug for most small-engine equipment costs under $10. It is also the cause of roughly one in four hard-start complaints that walk into a service shop. Before you decide that your mower, snowblower, or chainsaw is done, it is worth understanding what repair costs make sense and what does not.

This is a question the service team at Reyco Marine fields every week, especially at the start and end of each season. The answer is not always obvious, and it is not the same for every piece of equipment or every repair. Here is a framework for thinking about it.

## The 50% Rule

A repair is generally worth doing if the cost is less than 50% of the replacement cost of equivalent equipment. If a repair quote is approaching or exceeding half the price of a new unit, replacement becomes the more rational choice.

The 50% threshold is a starting point, not a hard rule. It needs to be adjusted based on:

**Age of the equipment.** A five-year-old lawn tractor with a $300 repair is a different calculation than a twelve-year-old tractor with the same repair. The five-year-old unit likely has another decade of useful life if maintained. The twelve-year-old unit may have additional failures coming.

**Availability of parts.** Some discontinued equipment is no longer serviceable because parts are not available or cost more than the repair makes sense. Before committing to a repair, confirm the parts are in stock.

**Maintenance history.** Equipment that has been serviced regularly tends to have discrete failures. Equipment that has been neglected tends to have cascading failures — one part fails, and the technician finds three more that are close behind.

## Repairs That Are Almost Always Worth Doing

These are low-cost fixes that return the equipment to full function:

- Spark plug replacement
- Air filter replacement
- Carburettor clean or rebuild (on equipment under ten years old)
- Belt replacement on lawn tractors and zero-turn mowers
- Shear bolt replacement on snowblowers
- Chain sharpening and bar replacement on chainsaws
- Lower unit oil change on outboard motors

Most of these are under $100 in parts and under an hour of labour. They restore the equipment to running condition and prevent further damage.

## Repairs That Need a Cost-Benefit Assessment

**Carburettor replacement (vs. clean):** A carburettor clean is almost always worth doing. A carburettor replacement on an older piece of equipment may not be, depending on whether the rest of the machine warrants it.

**Engine rebuild or replacement:** This is where the 50% rule matters most. A short block or engine replacement on a mid-range riding mower typically costs $400 to $900 in parts and labour. If the mower is ten years old and basic, replacement may be better value. If it is a higher-end commercial unit with a good deck and frame, the engine replacement extends the life of a platform worth keeping.

**Hydrostatic transmission on riding mowers:** These can be expensive to rebuild or replace. A hydrostatic transmission failure on a seven-year-old entry-level riding mower often tips the calculation toward replacement.

## When to Replace Without Hesitation

**The frame or deck is structurally compromised.** A cracked deck on a riding mower or a bent auger housing on a snowblower is usually uneconomical to repair. The structural component often costs more to replace than the machine is worth.

**Repeated failures on the same system.** If the same component fails twice within a short period, there is usually an underlying cause. One carburettor clean is worth it. Two in one season suggests a fuel system problem or an incompatibility with the fuel being used.

**Parts are discontinued or unavailable.** If the repair requires parts that are no longer manufactured or have to be sourced used, factor in lead time, reliability of used parts, and the risk of the repair not holding long-term.

**The machine has reached the end of its service life.** Residential lawn mower engines typically last 500 to 1,000 hours. Snowblowers, with proper maintenance, often outlast the tractors and mowers they run alongside. If the equipment has clearly given all it is going to give, continued repair investment is not a good use of money.

## How to Get an Honest Assessment

Bring the equipment in before you decide. The Reyco Marine service team at 11 White Oak Drive East in Sault Ste. Marie will diagnose the problem, give you an honest repair estimate, and tell you what else they find during the inspection.

A diagnosis appointment is worth doing before you commit. You may find the repair is simpler than expected. Or you may find out that the equipment has other issues that change the math. Either way, you make the decision with the right information.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it worth repairing a lawn mower that is ten years old?**
It depends on the repair. A carburettor clean, spark plug, and belt replacement on a ten-year-old riding mower is almost always worth doing. An engine rebuild on the same machine requires more thought. Ask for a condition assessment alongside the repair quote.

**How do I know if my snowblower is worth repairing?**
Snowblowers are generally durable machines. Most repairs short of an engine replacement or major auger housing damage are worth doing on a machine that is otherwise in good shape. A fifteen-year-old snowblower with good auger and impeller condition is often worth a $200 to $400 repair.

### Fishing Season Prep Guide for Northern Ontario

*By Casey Davieaux, Reyco Marine*

Walleye season on most inland lakes in the Algoma district opens on the third Saturday in May. For the anglers who have been watching the ice since March, the countdown is real. For the ones who left their gear where it was in October, the third Saturday comes up fast.

Good fishing starts with a boat that works, electronics that are dialled in, and gear that is ready. The fish are there. The question is whether you are ready to find them.

This guide covers what experienced Northern Ontario anglers do in the two weeks before opening day. It applies to walleye, muskie, pike, bass, and panfish setups in the Algoma district, Lake Huron North Shore, and the waters around Sault Ste. Marie.

## Start With the Boat, Not the Tackle

Every year, boats show up at the ramp on opening weekend with a dead battery, a motor that will not start, or a live well that has not been checked since October. The fish are not going anywhere. Take the hour.

**Run the motor.** If you winterized properly, the first start should be straightforward. If you did not, now is the time to find out. Flush the cooling system, check the tell-tale water flow, and run it up through the gears. Do not discover your motor problem at the ramp.

**Check your battery and electronics.** Fish finders, GPS units, and trolling motor batteries all self-discharge over winter. Connect them to a charger at least a week before the season opens. This gives you time to diagnose a battery that no longer holds a charge.

**Inspect the livewell and bilge pump.** Run the livewell pump and check for leaks. Test the bilge pump with a small amount of water in the bilge. These are easy checks that take five minutes and prevent problems in the field.

## Electronics and Navigation

The Humminbird units at Reyco carry software updates periodically. If you have not updated your chart plotter or fish finder software in the off-season, do it now on shore, where you have a stable internet connection and time to let it run.

Check your transducer mount and cable connections. Vibration over a full season loosens connections. A loose transducer cable causes intermittent signal loss at exactly the wrong moment.

Download or update your lake maps. If you are fishing new water this year, download the charts before you leave the dock. Cell signal on Northern Ontario lakes is often non-existent once you are past the first bay.

## Trolling Motor Check

Trolling motors sit hard against a Northern Ontario winter. Inspect the head unit, check the prop for damage or weeds from last fall, and run through the full range of functions before you launch.

For GPS-anchor and spot-lock systems like Minnkota Ultrex and Terrova, test the spot-lock function on land with the remote. Check the software version — updates to the navigation system are released periodically and can improve holding accuracy.

Check the battery connection terminals for corrosion. A poor connection causes intermittent power loss to the trolling motor, which is a significant problem when you are holding position on a walleye school.

## Fishing Gear and Terminal Tackle

Inspect your reels before the season. Open them up, clean and re-grease the internal gearing if they are due, and check the drag. A drag that slipped last fall under a big fish will slip again.

Inspect your line. Monofilament and fluorocarbon degrade over time and from UV exposure. Braided line lasts longer but check for fraying near knots and at the guides. If you are not sure, re-spool. Line is cheap relative to a lost fish or a lost lure.

Go through your tackle. Remove anything with dull, bent, or corroded hooks. Sharpen or replace them. Northern Ontario walleye fishing often comes down to whether the hook point is sharp enough to set on a light bite.

## Know the Regulations Before You Launch

Fishing regulations in Ontario are updated annually. Confirm the season dates, size limits, and possession limits for the species and waters you are targeting. The Ontario Fishing Regulations Summary is available online and in print at Reyco Marine. Get the current year's version. Do not fish on last year's regulations — specific waters and species can change.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**When does walleye season open in the Algoma district?**
Most inland waters in the Algoma district open on the third Saturday in May. However, specific lakes and rivers may have different seasons — including some that are closed or have special regulations. Check the Ontario Fishing Regulations Summary for the water you plan to fish.

**How do I update my fish finder software?**
Most Humminbird units update via an SD card downloaded from the Humminbird website, or via the OneBoat Network app if your unit supports it. Download the update at home on a reliable connection before you head out. Updates can take 15 to 30 minutes and should not be interrupted.

**What is the biggest mistake anglers make at the start of the season?**
Launching without testing the electronics. A fish finder or trolling motor that fails on opening weekend is a long paddle back in. Test everything on shore, at dock, or in the driveway before you commit to the water.

### Lake Superior Boating Prep: What You Need Before You Go Out

*By Casey Davieaux, Reyco Marine*

Lake Superior's average depth is 149 metres, and its surface water temperature stays cold enough to cause cold water shock even in July. It is the largest freshwater lake in the world by surface area. It is also the water that is closest to Sault Ste. Marie, and it demands more preparation than any inland lake in the Algoma district.

The customers who come to Reyco Marine to rig a boat for Lake Superior are not necessarily running bigger boats than the ones heading to Batchawana Bay or Thessalon. They are, however, doing more to prepare. That preparation makes the difference between a good day and a dangerous one.

## Understand the Conditions First

Lake Superior does not behave like an inland lake. Several factors make it categorically different.

**Water temperature.** Superior's surface temperature peaks around 13 to 17°C in August in the nearshore areas around the Sault — significantly colder than inland lakes, which regularly hit 22 to 25°C. Cold water shock is a real risk at these temperatures. A capsize in July on Superior gives you significantly less time to respond than the same capsize on an Algoma district lake. Wear your lifejacket, not just carry it.

**Wave development.** Superior's size allows large waves to develop quickly across a long fetch. A two-foot chop in the morning can become four-foot swells by early afternoon if the wind increases across open water. Weather forecasts for the lake are specific. A forecast that looks acceptable from shore can change significantly once you are past the shelter of the coastline.

**Fog.** Lake Superior fog is common, particularly in spring and early summer when cold water meets warmer air. Fog on the big lake with no GPS and no chart plotter is a serious situation. Have working navigation electronics before you go.

## Minimum Equipment for Lake Superior Day Trips

This list assumes a trailerable aluminium or fibreglass boat in the 16 to 22 foot range — the typical boat we see rigged for Superior trips out of the Sault.

**Navigation:**
- A chart plotter with current Lake Superior charts loaded — not a phone app as your only navigation
- A working compass as backup
- A marine VHF radio — this is the communication standard on the Great Lakes, not a phone

**Safety:**
- A lifejacket for every person on board, worn in rough conditions
- Flares — visual distress signals for open water
- A buoyant heaving line
- A first aid kit
- A whistle or air horn
- Sufficient fuel for your planned route plus a reserve — plan to return with at least 25% in the tank

**Weather:**
- Check the Environment Canada marine forecast for Lake Superior, not just the general Sault Ste. Marie forecast. Marine forecasts are specific to wave height, wind speed, and visibility on the water.
- Check conditions before you leave and at intervals during the day. Superior's weather can change within a few hours.

## Motor Readiness for Open Water

Mechanical failure on Lake Superior puts you in a more serious position than the same failure on a sheltered inland lake. Basic preparedness before you leave the dock:

**Carry a basic tool kit and spare parts.** Spare shear pins if you run a propeller that uses them, a spare propeller if you have it, a basic tool kit, and extra fuel line clamps and connectors.

**Know your motor's condition.** If your outboard has not been serviced recently, do not take it to open water. Spring commissioning, including lower unit oil inspection, impeller check, and fuel system review, is essential before Superior trips.

**Carry an anchor appropriate to the water.** A light anchor that holds on a sheltered lake may drag in a Superior swell. Know the bottom type where you plan to fish or anchor, and carry an anchor with enough scope.

## The Rule on Lake Superior: Tell Someone

Before you leave, tell someone where you are going and when you plan to be back. Superior is not forgiving of late returns that no one notices. A float plan does not have to be formal. A text to someone saying "heading to Cape Gargantua, back by 4pm" is enough. If you do not come back, they call for help.

Canada Coast Guard Aux and the Marine unit of Ontario Provincial Police operate on Lake Superior. Know the VHF emergency frequency (Channel 16) and carry a radio that reaches it.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**What size boat is safe for Lake Superior?**
There is no single answer — conditions matter more than size. That said, most experienced boaters on Superior prefer a minimum of 18 feet for day trips in open water, with appropriate freeboard and a reliable engine. In rough or uncertain conditions, a larger, heavier boat handles better. Never go out in conditions that exceed your boat's or your own capability.

**Do I need a VHF radio to boat on Lake Superior?**
You are not legally required to carry one on a recreational vessel, but it is strongly recommended. VHF is the standard communication on the Great Lakes. Cell service is unreliable on open water. A VHF radio lets you reach the Coast Guard and other vessels in an emergency, and lets you receive marine weather broadcasts (Environment Canada broadcasts on WX channels via VHF).

**What is the water temperature on Lake Superior near the Sault in summer?**
Surface temperatures near the Sault typically range from around 4 to 8°C in May and June, rising to 12 to 17°C in July and August in nearshore areas. Open water and deeper zones stay colder. Cold water shock can occur at any of these temperatures. Always wear your lifejacket in rough conditions or when boating solo.
