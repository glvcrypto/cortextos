## Their Story

TopLine Electric Plumbing Heating & Cooling operates as a multi-trade contractor based at 488 Gran Street, which sits on the **Rankin Reserve (Rankin Location 15D)**, the main reserve land of the **Batchewana First Nation of Ojibways**. Rankin Reserve is a 15.66 km² tract of land located between the City of Sault Ste. Marie and Garden River First Nation, formally recognized as a reserve in 1952.

**Indigenous ownership/affiliation: Not confirmed. The address is physically on Batchewana First Nation land. That does not by itself mean the business is Indigenous-owned.**

Key facts:
- Multiple public directories (Alignable, ConstructConnect, POST Training) list the business address explicitly as "Batchewana First Nation" or "Rankin Reserve."
- The company self-describes as "Canadian-owned" — not "Indigenous-owned" or "First Nations-owned."
- No press releases, government grant announcements, or Indigenous business certifications (CCIB, CAMSC) have been found linking TopLine to Indigenous ownership.
- The business explicitly notes "a long-standing history of quality work" in remote communities, including Wawa and White River — this is consistent with Northern Ontario contractors who serve fly-in and remote reserves, but does not itself indicate Indigenous ownership.
- CLAC (Christian Labour Association of Canada) union representation suggests a structured, multi-employee employer, not a micro-shop.

**Working hypothesis:** TopLine Electric is a non-Indigenous-owned contractor that operates from a physical location on Batchewana reserve land (likely leased commercial space or band-approved business). This is a pattern seen in Northern Ontario where contractors base out of reserve locations for various reasons (land costs, access, long-standing relationships).

**Do not present grant opportunities premised on Indigenous ownership unless the owner confirms this directly.** If the owner is Indigenous, they will say so. Mentioning NOHFC or CCIB grants unprompted is appropriate — Indigenous business grants (ABD, NACCA) require them to confirm eligibility.

**Trades profile:** Fully licensed across all four trades — electricians (Master level, ESA registered), plumbers (Master level), HVAC/refrigeration technicians (Red Seal), oil and gas technicians (G1/G2/G3 licensed). Sheet metal and ductwork in-scope. Renewable energy (solar, wind, hydro) listed as a service area — SSM has significant renewable infrastructure (Prince Wind Farm, Sault Solar Park) making this plausible commercial work.

**Union:** CLAC Local 52 members ratified a 3-year collective agreement — 3% annual wage increases, 7% employer pension contribution + 3% matching, $250 PPE allowance, improved travel and on-call language. This indicates a real workforce, not just a couple of guys with a van.

## Retainer Viability

**Multi-trade = materially higher average ticket.** A single-trade electrician fixing an outlet is a $200 call. TopLine can bundle electrical + plumbing + HVAC on a single residential or commercial job — $1,500 to $8,000+ per project is realistic for combined service calls. Commercial maintenance contracts and "large commercial infrastructure projects" mentioned on their own site.

Evidence of commercial and industrial capacity:
- Listed as "Sub-Contractors" on ConstructConnect (used by commercial GCs and project owners to find subs)
- Renewable energy work (solar, wind, hydro) — this is commercial/utility scale, not residential
- CLAC union shop — structured workforce required for institutional and commercial bids
- Services include traffic control and utility locating (Yellow Pages listing) — strongly suggests infrastructure/civil work beyond residential
- Oil, natural gas, propane sales and service — commercial and industrial accounts
- Food and beverage equipment, security systems, refrigeration (Yellow Pages) — commercial verticals

**Team size:** Unionized workforce (CLAC Local 52) — minimum viable CLAC bargaining unit typically 5–10 workers. With four licensed trades plus administrative staff, a 10–20 person company is the plausible range. Not confirmed by a headcount figure.

**Service area breadth:** Wawa and White River are 190 km and 270 km from SSM respectively. Running crews to those locations means vehicles, fuel, dispatch, and scheduling overhead — this is not a sole-prop with a pickup truck. They have operational infrastructure.

**Revenue estimate:** Based on trade mix, union payroll, and stated service scope — $2M–$6M annual revenue range is plausible. No public financial data available.

**$2,000/month is proportional.** At even $2M revenue, a $24K/year marketing retainer is 1.2% of revenue — industry norm is 2–5% of revenue for contractors at this stage. The ask is well inside what this business should be spending.

## Red Flags

- **Ownership is ambiguous.** Three different names (Brad King, Richard Levesque, George Bumbacco) appear in different directories — no single owner is publicly confirmed. Justin Furano is the only verified named contact (GM). Walk in without knowing who you're pitching to at the ownership level.
- **Address is on First Nations reserve land** — this may mean the business operates under a different regulatory context for some municipal permits or business licensing. Not a dealbreaker, but worth understanding.
- **CLAC union shop** — some contractors with union staff have rigid internal communication structures. The owner may be insulated from vendor conversations that Justin Furano screens. Build rapport with Justin first.
- **Website times out repeatedly** — the site (toplineelectric.ca) failed to load in multiple fetch attempts during research. If the site has uptime issues, this is both a problem and a pitch point.
- **No BBB listing** — either they are not members or have not engaged the BBB. Not a negative per se, but a data point that they have not been proactive about formal credentialing.
- **"Three Best Rated" SSM electricians does not include TopLine** — despite being a larger, multi-trade operation, they are not top-of-mind in the market. This could mean they rely on relationship-based referrals and have not invested in visibility. Good for the pitch; raises the question of whether they see marketing as necessary.
