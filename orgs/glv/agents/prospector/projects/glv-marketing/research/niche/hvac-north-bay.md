## Industry Stats

1. **Approximately 69.7% of North Bay homes were built before 1980** (11.5% pre-1945, 18.1% between 1946–1960, 18.7% in the 1960s, 21.4% in the 1970s — Statistics Canada 2021 Census). Gas furnaces have a typical lifespan of 15–20 years, meaning a significant portion of this housing stock is on its second or third furnace cycle. Replacement demand is structurally elevated.

2. **North Bay's average January low is -15°C, with extremes reaching -26°C** (WeatherSpark climate data). At these temperatures, furnace failure is a safety emergency, not a comfort inconvenience. Emergency call volume spikes sharply in January and February — the highest-urgency, highest-margin segment of the HVAC service calendar.

3. **93% of Canadian homeowners read online reviews before hiring a HVAC contractor**, with more than 90% rating those reviews as "extremely important" or "very important" when making their decision (HVAC industry research, 2025). In North Bay, several active HVAC businesses have fewer than 5 visible Google reviews — a direct revenue leak the right review strategy can fix within 60–90 days.

4. **Ontario homeowners can now claim up to $14,000 in combined HVAC rebates and federal tax credits in 2025–2026** — the highest incentive stack in Canadian history. The provincial Home Renovation Savings (HRS) program offers $500–$2,000/ton for air-source heat pumps and up to $12,000 for ground-source systems. This is creating a buyer education opportunity for any HVAC business that ranks for "heat pump rebate North Bay" terms.

| Local Context | Harsh continental climate with ~3.3 months below freezing and -26°C extremes. CFB North Bay (Canadian military) significant employer. Nipissing University and local health sector anchor middle-income homeowners. French-English bilingual community (~15% francophone). |

**How they buy:**
- **Search behaviour:** "Near me" and city-name searches dominate at point of need. Emergency searches spike November–February (furnace) and June–August (AC). 90%+ begin online via Google Search or Maps before calling anyone.
- **Purchase triggers:** Furnace or AC breakdown (emergency, highest urgency and lowest price sensitivity), rising energy bills, home sale preparation, government rebate programs (Ontario HRS, federal Greener Homes), seasonal tune-up outreach from existing providers.
- **Average transaction:** Furnace repair $200–$600. Furnace replacement $5,000–$6,000 installed (North Bay pricing per furnaceprices.ca). AC installation $3,500–$7,000. Full system replacement $9,000–$18,000. Maintenance plan $200–$450/yr.
- **Decision timeline:** Emergency = same day or next day (no shopping). Planned replacement = 1–3 weeks of research, 2–3 quotes. Rebate-driven upgrade = weeks to months of awareness-to-action. Most residential customers compare 2–3 local contractors before committing.

**What motivates them:**
- Reliability and speed (a furnace failure at -20°C is a safety event, not just inconvenience)
- Energy cost savings (Northern Ontario electricity and gas costs above provincial average; rebates make heat pumps compelling)
- Trust through reviews and word-of-mouth (small-city dynamics; reputation travels fast)
- Transparent pricing with no surprise charges
- Local, family-run businesses preferred over national chains (Reliance/Enercare have poor reputations for high-pressure rental agreements)
- Financing options that make large system replacements accessible on a $64K median income

**What frustrates them:**
- Long wait times during peak winter emergency periods
- Being told to "book an appointment for a quote" before getting any pricing guidance
- No-shows or late arrivals without communication
- High-pressure upselling toward rental programs with long contracts
- Difficulty reaching anyone after hours (several North Bay operators do not offer weekend service)
- Discovering online that a business they're calling has no reviews or a poor reputation
